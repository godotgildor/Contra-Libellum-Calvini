Calvin 35.
But although a like iniquity once oppressed the holy Prophets and Apostles of God, they did not deny that the worship of God and his heavenly doctrine might lawfully be defended by legitimate power, so that a divinely prescribed penalty might be exacted from the impious subverters of it. Certainly God had established a severe judgment, as we shall afterward see, against those who should attempt to lead the people away from the true religion.
Vaticanus.
Nor do we deny it. But who exactly these impious subverters are is the matter of controversy. For those are not straightaway to be held as impious subverters of the heavenly doctrine if any disagree with Calvin in disputed matters. I will bring forward an example by which the whole matter may be illustrated. Before and in the time of Christ there were four sects of the Jews: Scribes, Pharisees, Sadducees, and Essenes. All these thought well concerning the Law, but they interpreted it, some one way, some another. If any of them had attempted to deny the Law or impiously to subvert it, and to lead his own away to worship Jove or another God, he would deservedly have paid the penalty. And so the Maccabees deservedly punished those who had introduced the rites of the Greeks into the gymnasia of the Jews. But no Law commanded those sects to be punished with the sword, who did not impiously and of set purpose subvert the Law, but erred in the interpretation of the Law. So today there are many sects of Christians, who all think piously and religiously concerning the Gospel, but interpret it, some one way, some another. If anyone should plainly deny the Gospel which he had before professed, and should speak evil of Christ or of God, and wantonly blaspheme, I should indeed by no means wish to speak for him. But he who, believing the sayings of Christ, understands them otherwise than we do, him I do not believe to be an impious subverter of religion: for impiety is one thing, error another. If Calvin had been a Pharisee, he would have killed all the Scribes and Sadducees and Essenes, nor would he have left them to be taught by Christ. But I do not think that before the coming of Christ those could have been called heretics; but after Christ came, those who did not obey him duly admonishing were heretics and obstinate. So today I do not think that so many sects ought all to be called heretical. But when there shall have come the mouth and wisdom which no one can resist, if anyone shall not have obeyed this, him I shall hold for a heretic. But if Calvin's wisdom were such, I could not now duly contradict it: from which it appears that he is not endowed with that wisdom. And so Christ the Judge is to be awaited, nor is one rashly to run ahead of the judgment of God—to use Calvin's own words set down above.
Calvin 36.
Jeremiah, dragged to capital punishment by wicked tyrants and cast into the pit, by no means fled to this refuge, that the worship of God is not to be vindicated by the sword: but he uses a different plea, that the enemies of truth would pay the penalty when they had shed innocent blood.
Vaticanus.
Most ineptly is this example cited, because Jeremiah had not yet been cast into the pit. But this is a lapse of Calvin's memory, which can easily be pardoned—although he himself is unwilling to bear the errors of others. Jeremiah had been seized by the Priests and false Prophets, who were attempting and endeavoring to inflict a grievous death upon him, because he had prophesied against Jerusalem. Hence Jeremiah thus addresses them: "Indeed I am in your hand, do what seems good to you. But know this, that if you kill me, it will come to pass that you, and this city, and its inhabitants shall pay the penalties of innocent blood." Here you see that the matter is not of Religion, but of Homicide. For he does not threaten them with penalties if they have opposed the truth, but if they shall have shed innocent blood; and he threatens [it] not from the Magistrate (for they themselves wished to kill him, not through the Magistrate) but from God. And this example can be alleged not for Calvin, but for Servetus. For he could say to the Priest Calvin and to the Genevans: "Indeed I am in your hand, but if you kill me, you will pay to God the penalties of homicide." There are also in the same Jeremiah other examples more suited to this passage—concerning truth despised, but plainly contrary to Calvin. Jeremiah admonishes Zedekiah to surrender himself to the Chaldeans; and if he should refuse, he foretells grave penalties to him from the Lord. Here no mention of the Magistrate. He admonishes Hananiah the false Prophet to cease deceiving the people with his flatteries; when that man persevered in his error, he foretells death to him, but from the Lord, not from the Magistrate.
Calvin 37.
What of the Apostles? Do they expostulate with adversaries who defend Religion with penalties from the mandate of the Law? Nay, relying on the goodness of their cause alone, they refuse no severity, if they shall have been caught having sinned in anything.
Vaticanus.
Nor do they expostulate with those who still maintained circumcision and the other ceremonies, which Christ had then abolished. Does it follow on that account that those did rightly who defended the Law with ceremonies? Calvin, destitute of arguments, since he cannot say "they assert," says "they do not deny," as a little before concerning the Apostles and Prophets: "they did not deny," he says; and presently concerning Jeremiah: "he by no means fled to this refuge." In this way it will be permissible to defend errors innumerable: the Apostles did not deny that one must pray for the dead; they did not deny that there is a purgatory; they did not deny that one must go to the Mass—therefore it will be lawful. If this man had true arguments, he would never thus play the fool.
Calvin 38.
I confess indeed that Hilary is carried somewhat too far: for he complains that to terrify the Church with prisons and exiles, and thus to compel to belief, [the Church] which was believed through exiles and prisons, is too inclined toward the dignity of those who favor it—[the dignity] which has been consecrated by the terror of persecutors: to put priests to flight, [the Church] which was propagated by priests put to flight: to glory in the favor of the world, [the Church] which could not be Christ's unless the world had hated it. But the ferocity of that (1) Auxentius and of his like provoked his stomach, so that he carped at the present indignation rather (since a plausible argument was given him) than weighed the perpetual rationale of guarding the faith.
Vaticanus.
What is best in Hilary, this Calvin censures: for if you consider Hilary's words, they are plainly divine. And the same Calvin cites and praises the same Hilary against the same Auxentius in the preface of his Institutes in these words: "And indeed he already reckoned it in the place of the highest vice, that, occupied by a foolish admiration of episcopal dignity, they did not notice the deadly Lernaean [serpent] lurking beneath that mask." For thus he [Hilary] speaks: "One thing I warn: beware of Antichrist; for an evil love of walls has seized you; evilly do you venerate the Church of God in roofs and buildings, evilly under these do you obtrude the name of peace. Is it doubtful that Antichrist will sit in them? Mountains to me, and Woods, and Lakes, and prisons, and chasms are safer; for in these the Prophets, either abiding or sunk down, prophesied." These things Calvin then wrote, serving his own cause. Now, after he has been made a Bishop, he turns the page, and—men's minds being occupied by a foolish admiration of Calvinian dignity—he nourishes the deadly Lernaean [serpent] beneath that mask. (1) Nay, the boldness of Servetus (who had not hesitated to reprehend him) provoked Calvin's stomach, so that he indulged the present vengeance rather (since a plausible argument concerning the Trinity was given him) than weighed the perpetual rationale of guarding the faith best described by Hilary.
Calvin 39.
For as to his alleging as a pretext that Christ is argued to be destitute of his own power, while earthly suffrages commend the faith (1)—it must not be drawn to that side, as though the authority of believers should detract anything from the certainty of the Gospel, or Religion grow cheap when aided by the patronage of Kings: but the complaint is directed only against those (2) who, destitute of the word of God (3), with armed hand upheld perverse errors.
Vaticanus.
Yet Ezra, when he was returning from Babylon, was unwilling to demand any garrison from King Cyrus against the enemies, because he said that all were safe under the protection of the Lord who duly invoked him. And in Isaiah Christ says that he alone trod the winepress, with none of the peoples helping him (2). They had the word of God no less than Calvin: for they were Christians, as you are. (3) For if you had the truth, there would be given you a mouth and wisdom which no one could resist: but, conscious of the weakness of your word, you call in an armed hand.
Calvin 40.
In the same place it is permitted today to bewail that the state of the Papal Church is propped up by nothing but force and slaughters; that, the office of Pastors being omitted (1), those fulminate with tyrannical edicts alone who vend themselves under the title of prelates: (2) whereas the true and genuine Church of Christ, sprung from persecutions, flourished under those same.
Vaticanus.
And these things Calvin writes with hands still bloody with the blood of Servetus—who, unless he defended himself with tyrannical edicts and vended himself under the title of prelate, could not stand against those to whom he is known. For he knows no other office of a pastor than those very ones whom he accuses. It pleases me here to set down the edict of the Genevan Magistrate, by whose authority the Institutes of Calvin are fortified. The cause of the edict was this: there was at Geneva a certain citizen by the name of Trouillet; he dared to reprehend certain things in Calvin's Institutes; for this cause he was called to law. After there had been long litigation, a decree of the senate was made in the French tongue in these words:
Estans ouys en conseil & savans Ministres de la parolle de Dieu, Maistre Guillaume Farel & Pierre Viret, & apres eux spectables Maistre Iohan Calvin, et maistre Iohan Trouillet en leurs dires & reproches souvent debatues, de l'Institution Chrestienne du dict monseur Calvin, & le tout bien consideré le conseil arresté & conclu que toutes choses bien oyes & entendues a prononcé & declaré le dict livre de l'Institution du dict monsieur estre bien & sainctement faict, sa doctrine estre saincte doctrine de Dieu, que l'on le tient pour bon & vrai ministre de ceste Cité, & que de ici a l'avenir personne ne soit osé parler contre le dict livre ou la dicte doctrine. Commandans aux pareilles & a tous se doivent tenir a cela. Le Merquedi, que fut neufieme de Novembre, l'an mille cincq cens cincquante et deux.
[EN (French): The worshipful and learned Ministers of the word of God, Master Guillaume Farel and Pierre Viret, and after them the worshipful Master Jean Calvin and Master Jean Trouillet having been heard in council, in their statements and reproaches often debated, concerning the Christian Institution of the said monsieur Calvin, and all having been well considered, the council resolved and concluded that, all things having been well heard and understood, it has pronounced and declared the said book of the Institution of the said monsieur to be well and holily made, his doctrine to be holy doctrine of God, that he be held for a good and true minister of this City, and that from here onward no person be so bold as to speak against the said book or the said doctrine. Commanding the parties and all to hold themselves to this. The Wednesday which was the ninth of November, the year one thousand five hundred fifty-two.]
hoc est [that is]:
When there had been heard in the Senate the worshipful and most learned ministers of the word of God, Master Guilhelmus Farellus and Petrus Viret, and after them the worshipful master Iohannes Calvinus, and Master Iohannes Trouilletus, in their statements and objections now often disputed concerning the Christian Institution of the Lord Calvin himself, all things being well considered, the Senate bears and decrees in this manner. All things being duly heard and understood, the Senate has pronounced and declared, and pronounces and declares, that the Book of the Institutes of the Lord Calvin named above is well and holily made, and that his doctrine is holy doctrine of God, and that the author himself is to be held for a good and true Minister of this City. And so it is decreed that henceforth no one dare to speak against that book and his doctrine. It is enjoined on both parties and on all, that they abide by this decree. Given on the day of Wednesday which was the ninth of November, in the year one thousand five hundred fifty-two.
Weigh this writing, Reader; you will see with what arms the doctrine of this man is fortified, and how unworthy it is that he should inveigh against the Papists, when he himself does the same things. (2) And of what sort was the Genevan Church, sprung from persecutions: it flourished under those same, with Farel as leader. For then some Charity flourished at Geneva. But now, after it has begun to defend itself with Calvinian arms—that is, with the sword—it has deflowered with the fruit ripe [i.e., lost its bloom though the fruit was ripe], and its silver has been turned into dross.
Calvin 41.
But the swords of persecutions do not stand in the way of pious Magistrates protecting with their scepters the Church, which was once unjustly afflicted. Nor do the crosses of martyrs hinder the faithful from being aided, by the just protection of the Laws, toward tranquilly worshiping God.
Vaticanus.
If Servetus had assailed you with arms, you would rightly have been defended by the Magistrate: but since he assailed you with writings, why did you fight against writings with sword and flames? Do you call this the defense of a pious Magistrate? Is there among you no other piety than to drag foreigners into the fire as they pass through without any disturbance? And do you dare to inveigh against the Papists? Show among the Papists any Lutheran or Calvinist who has been snatched from the Mass into prison, as Servetus among you [was snatched] from the sermon.
Calvin 42.
For prudently does Augustine say (1): "Emperors, if they were in error (which God forbid), would on behalf of their error give Laws against the truth, by which the just would both be proved and crowned, by not doing what those commanded, because God forbade [it]: just as Nebuchadnezzar had commanded that the golden statue be adored, and because they were unwilling to do this, they pleased God who forbade [it]. But when Emperors hold the truth, they command on its behalf against error, and whoever shall have contemned this draws judgment upon himself. For he both suffers penalties among men, and before God has no portion, who was unwilling to do this which truth itself commanded through the heart of the King: just as Nebuchadnezzar himself afterward, moved and changed by a miracle, set forth an edict on behalf of truth against error."
Vaticanus.
Prudently—not to say maliciously—Calvin dissembles the passages of Augustine in which he speaks against persecutions, and condemns this whole Calvinism. Book 3 against Cresconius; likewise On Faith and Works, Chapter 5; and Epistle 158 and 159 to Marcellinus, and 50 to [Apennius?]; and in the Book of Questions on the Gospel according to Matthew, Chapter 13; and against the Epistle of the Manichaean, and in other passages. All these passages are of no moment to Calvin: there Augustine was to him an Epicurean or Lucianic, or certainly Unskilled. And this is indeed the custom of those men, that out of the same authors they condemn some things and praise others at their own discretion, but they condemn above all those things which are more agreeable to Christian clemency, so that it may be understood that they themselves shrink from all mercy. But nevertheless let us weigh Augustine's saying: for it is plainly contrary to Calvin. (1) But there will be many in error in the last times, as Christ shows, who says that he will not find faith on the earth when he comes. And Paul says that the last times will be most wicked. And John in the Apocalypse, who says that the beast is adored by Kings and finally that they make war on the lamb. There are very many such passages in the sacred Letters. And so Emperors and Magistrates will, in the last—that is, in our—times, give Laws on behalf of error against the truth. And Calvin, when he inflames them to persecute, does nothing else than arm them against the truth, for they all think they hold the truth.
Calvin 43.
(1) Those who seek the rule from the pure word of God—by them Religion is worshiped duly and in order. (2) If anyone fabricates for himself a worship at his own will, or by chance seizes upon what has been rashly devised by others, the more he busies himself, the more he binds himself with greater guilt, because superstition merits no praise.
Vaticanus.
(1) All sects do this. Calvin says that all err except his own. But who gave us him as Judge? Why is Christ the Judge not awaited? (2) Calvin himself does this in his Wednesday, in the singing of fixed psalms on certain days and other ceremonies. I do not reprehend order, without which a multitude cannot be contained. But I know how superstitiously these things are observed.
Calvin 44.
(1) If a true and legitimate worshiper of God, insofar as his calling will bear, fights for the defense of his faith, a blind (2) rage will seize the unbelieving.
Vaticanus.
Namely, with his own arms—Justice, faith, patience, and the rest of the virtues, which arms Paul attributes to Christians. But Calvin [attributes] the sword. (2) If to roast with flames a man invoking the name of Christ, and not only not convicted but not even accused of any crime, is not blind rage, there can be no blind rage.
Calvin 45.
Thus in the Papists we deservedly condemn the foolish zeal void of knowledge, which precipitates them: no less, namely, than the insane superstitions by which, bewitched, they proudly trample on the word of God.
Vaticanus.
Why did you learn from them to roast men? Or will you say that you learned this custom from Christ? Say whence you learned it.
Calvin 46.
If the vice of preposterous zeal flows from ignorance, why will not that zeal be laudable, which does not by chance snatch (1) the sons of God, but inflames [them] to asserting the faith (2) which has been solidly attested to them?
Vaticanus.
If you were the sons of God, you would do the works of God. But you kill men who are not to be killed. This the Son of God never did. (2) To assert one's faith is not to burn a man, but rather to be burned. "He who shall have persevered unto the end," he says, "shall be saved." How? By persecuting? Nay, by suffering. This is the true assertion of faith, but [one] unknown to Calvin.
Calvin 47.
(1) Finally, just as among martyrs and blasphemers, in the likeness of penalty, yet the fault establishes the distinction; (2) so cognition makes [men] pious and right Zealots; temerity and a blind impulse [make them] iniquitous and perverse.
Vaticanus.
And so there is a distinction between Raymond, who on account of horrendous and indubitable blasphemies against God was struck with the axe at Geneva: and Servetus, who concerning God had written as well as he could, and if he had written anything badly, he had done it erring—just as Farel himself [testified] to the people, with Servetus praying before the pyre, testifies it. And likewise Calvin himself, who in the title of this Book called them "errors." Nor did Raymond ever say that he had acted erring, nor did he ever defend his blasphemies; from which it appears how great is the distinction between him and Servetus. (2) So it is. But Christ instructs pious zealots when he says: "You know not of what spirit you are. The Son of man came not to destroy, but to save." But the Wolf comes only to slaughter.
Calvin 48.
He will be dragged to punishment, who contends that whatever has been delivered by the sacred oracles is a fiction. Who would dare to adorn such a monster with the honorific title of martyr? Namely, as Augustine himself rightly defines: "The cause makes the martyr, not the penalty."
Vaticanus.
This man wishes to burden with this odium, and render hateful, those whom he himself holds for heretics, when no one attributes more to the sacred oracles than those who are commonly held for heretics. For those who deny the sacred Letters are to be held not heretics, but infidels and impious—concerning whom we are not here treating.
Calvin 49.
Therefore here prudence and moderation are to be held, lest princes either be tumultuously inflamed for an unknown cause (1), or rush to the shedding of blood with empty savagery (2).
Vaticanus.
(1) Namely, the causes were not unknown on account of which Servetus perished—concerning the Trinity, concerning Fate, concerning Free will, and the rest—when the whole world is in doubt and disputes about them. But to Calvin nothing is unknown. (2) If to roast with green wood a living man, not even guilty of a crime, is not monstrous savagery, show what savagery is.
Calvin 50.
By an old proverb it has been called audacious ignorance: but of superstition the audacity is as marvelous as the cruelty.
Vaticanus.
So it is: both audacity and cruelty appear in killing those whom God has forbidden to be killed.
Calvin 51.
And so we see that nothing is more bloody than hypocrites, since they sleep securely in their ignorance and errors. Nor indeed can it be otherwise but that all the unbelieving reproduce the nature of their father, who was both a homicide from the beginning and the father of lying.
Vaticanus.
I was about to write these things against Calvin, had he not anticipated me. He seems to have written these things with the blood of Servetus.
Calvin 52.
But by the true worshipers of God both vices are to be guarded against, lest they either rashly seize the patronage of an unknown [cause], or be borne intemperately toward severity.
Vaticanus.
He thinks his Magistrates are the true worshipers of God, through whom he has avenged himself on his enemy. But God knows all things.
Calvin 53.
For if in earthly causes the sloth of a judge is deservedly traduced with the reproaches of all, who (1) out of the opinion of others, himself ignorant of the whole matter, passes sentence: how much less are those to be borne who, in the cause of piety, neglect with supine fastidiousness the inquisition (which is enjoined even on any private persons whatsoever)?
Vaticanus.
(1) Just as the Genevan magistrate judged Servetus out of the opinion of Calvin, themselves ignorant of the whole matter, being indeed illiterate men: this they showed in the letters which they wrote to the Churches, in which they said that they did not doubt about the faith of their pastors.
Calvin 54.
And so detestable is that barbarism which dominates in the Papacy: nor indeed will it escape the horrible judgment of God. There are martyrs ready to render an account of the sheep, if it be permitted. So far is no defense conceded out of the word of God (1), that the beginning of the action (2) is to admit no disputation.
Vaticanus.
(1) Nay, it is conceded: but just as to Servetus—namely, on this condition, that he must perish, unless he recant. (2) And the beginning of your action was prison, and to admit to disputation no friend of Servetus, or at least no one not his enemy. Witness Gribaldi, so proudly repudiated by you.
Calvin 55.
And so, the handle being cut off from their reasonings, they butcher with dire torments the wretched men (1) unheard (2); afterward, their tongues raised (3), they thrust them to the fire, not [a fire] that may consume them at once with its flame, but [one that] may finish them with slow scorching, that they may feel themselves dying, as that tyrant used to say. That example indeed is one both of brutish stupor and of monstrous savagery.
Vaticanus.
(1) Nay, heard—although what does it matter whether they are heard or not heard? Since they are in the hand of iniquitous judges, from whom nothing but death can be expected: whether they speak rightly or otherwise, as was done with Servetus, who was heard by none but his enemies—that is, by Calvin and the Helvetians, who themselves ought to have been the accused, not the judges, since Servetus had written against them all. (2) The Genevans approved of doing the same to Servetus, if I have heard the truth. For when of his own accord he had confessed concerning the Book and concerning all his own truth [position], the Gehenna (so they call torment in their native tongue) was moreover applied, and he would have been butchered, had not Petrus Vandalus the senator intervened—himself also a chief enemy to Calvin: that you may understand that those who are a little more clement cannot be friends of Calvin. (3) Calvin glories under this name, that he did not cut off Servetus's tongue. But he cut off his life and burned his Books, lest Servetus could, at least by his Books after death, plead his cause throughout the whole world. But Calvin thinks his judgment concerning Servetus ought to be ratified by all, and that no further inquiry is to be made, after our master himself has spoken. But why did he burn the books? He feared, I believe, lest men be corrupted. Why had he not feared the same before in the Interim—a book which he himself saw to printing at Geneva, with his own writing against it? Why did he not do the same with Servetus? Unless because Servetus uncovered Calvin more than the Interim did. But if he shall say that he could not extinguish the Interim: why did he not at least exclude it from his own city? Why does he not prohibit other pernicious books from being either printed or sold at Geneva? There it is permitted to have Aristotle, who denies the first and chief article of faith concerning the creation of the world: it is permitted [to have] the Koran, Apuleius, Martial, Plautus, Terence, Horace, Catullus, Tibullus, Propertius, and the rest of the nefarious corrupters of morals: it is permitted [to have] Ovid On the Art of Loving—that is, of adulterating—and his imitator Clément Marot. Some years ago a certain man had come from Gaul to Geneva: he wrote thus to a certain friend of his: "I have emigrated from Babylon: send me all the works of Ovid with the commentaries." Babylon they themselves call the Papacy. What for them is Jerusalem? What shall I say of the trifles which are printed there? The Zographia of Beza and his Epistle of Passavant, on the death of the wife of Viret—which little books are nothing other than scurrilities and trifles. But these men do not hold scurrilities for sin: for they sit in the seat of the scornful. While they carry such books about in their bosom, they burn the books of Servetus. Why? Unless that they not be uncovered, but may with impunity say and do whatever they please. They fear the eyes of men, and contemn the eyes of God beholding all these things. (3) He describes the death of Servetus, and apprehends others as tyrants. For he believes that these things are permitted to himself and his own alone.
Calvin 56.
And rightly also in this part [says] Augustine: "If they are terrified," he says, "and not taught, it will seem an improbable [kind of] domination, as it were. (1) But again, if they are taught and not terrified, hardened by the oldness of habit, they will rise too sluggishly to take up the way of salvation." He speaks of convicted heretics, who, induced only by proud peevishness, had made a secession from the Church.
Vaticanus.
We too judge that [men] are to be terrified, but with those arms with which Paul, discoursing of justice and continence and the future Judgment, terrified the fortunate praetor. But this Calvinian manner of terrifying all the Apostles knew not, and Calvin has something more than they.
Calvin 57.
What then do we think he would have said, if he had cruelly seen pious and simple worshipers of the one God roasted for this cause alone, because they opposed the clear word of God to the figments of men?
Vaticanus.
Certainly he would not have approved.
Calvin 58.
For the Pope's incendiaries have no other reason for raging, except that they do not endure anything to be derogated from their fictitious laws.
Vaticanus.
Those men [the Papists] sanctioned their laws almost first with blood against John Hus, in the Council of Constance, when they had already reigned for many ages: this same man [Calvin], when he had scarcely yet begun to reign. What sort will the tenth from Calvin be? And afterward he does not blush to condemn others.
Calvin 59.
But it is no wonder that those rage so impotently, the light of doctrine being oppressed, whose domination is founded on the confused ruins of the word of God, and whose hope of victory is placed in nothing but darkness.
Vaticanus.
And Calvin does the same, who burns the Books of others, and snatches away the liberty of speaking with a violent hand, lest his nefarious attempts be laid open—he indeed whose hope of victory is placed in nothing but darkness.
Calvin 60.
Far otherwise the Church of God, which, as it seeks the confession of its faith from the mouth of God, so it tempers its zeal lest it be borne preposterously toward the same rule.
Vaticanus.
As Christ taught his Disciples, when they wished to call down fire from heaven by the example of Elijah: "You know not," he says, "of what spirit you are; the Son of man came not to destroy, but to save." But this tempering of zeal Calvin does not lay down.
Calvin 61.
For us, therefore, there is no doubt that an exact prudence as well as gentleness is to be observed: then, that a placid and religious cognition of doctrine ought to go before Judgment.
Vaticanus.
Of what sort was [the cognition] observed in Servetus, who, as soon as he was spotted on the Lord's day, was dragged from the sermon into prison, whence afterward he never came out, except when he was led to the fire. This is that placid and religious cognition of doctrine of Calvin's.
Calvin 62.
But this is by no means an obstacle, that it is the Magistrate's office to coerce with the sword and penalties those who, since they themselves are apostates, solicit others from the right faith to defection, and, contumelious against God, ensnare wretched souls with their fallacies; disturb the peace of the Church (1) and rend and tear the consensus of piety.
Vaticanus.
Calvin had promised that he would speak about the penalty of heretics: then he spoke much about those erring, and the impious, and blasphemers—as though those erring, the impious, blasphemers, Apostates, and heretics were the same. And lest anyone be able to see the distinction and notice Calvin's fallacy, nowhere does he define what a heretic is. Thus he jocosely mocks us in a matter so serious. Now, what he says about Apostates, he says badly. For an Apostate, in the sacred letters, is called he who falls away to another God: but a heretic [is] he who, retaining the worship of the same God, obstinately clings to some false sect or opinion. These two things Calvin wishes to confound, and not even thus does he attain his proposed end: for if he ought to be called an Apostate who solicits others from the right faith to defection, and has himself already fallen away from it (otherwise he ought not to be called an Apostate, just as a Jew is not an Apostate from Christ, since he never believed in him)—Servetus never fell away from Lutheranism or Calvinism. For he was always opposed to them. But if he was an Apostate, as Calvin seems to wish (for he took from him the occasion of this disputation), then the faith of the Pope, from which Servetus fell away, must be right. Thus Calvin himself will, by his own opinion, be one to be burned, inasmuch as he fell away from the Pope. (1) He calls Calvinism the Church, for he does not hold the rest for Churches.
Calvin 63.
Yet what reasons the unskilled—but not bad—men hold forth, to whom it seems that impunity should be given to so great a wickedness, must be briefly seen. "The Lord," they say, "was unwilling that his kingdom be constituted by hand and arms, but by the spiritual sword of the Gospel: and he exhorted his own to be ready to shed their own blood; that they should shed another's he nowhere commanded: he sent the Apostles as lambs into the midst of Wolves, he did not equip them with the power of the flesh." But if I should except on the contrary, that the Lord nowhere mandated to his own that they punish thefts, robberies, adulteries, slaughters, poisonings, and that therefore all these are to be borne with impunity—what will they say? Will they concede that the reins are to be loosened to the wicked? Or will they not rather say that the tolerance which is enjoined toward individuals does not stand in the way of the sanctions of the Laws being in force?
Vaticanus.
When thefts, robberies, adulteries, slaughters, etc., are punished, they are not punished for the constituting of the Kingdom of Christ, not for justifying or rendering men pious, not for generating a new creature, but for guarding the bodies or faculties of the good. And so Calvin himself will concede to the Papists, Jews, Turks, and the rest—whom he thinks outside the kingdom of Christ—to punish crimes of this sort: but on account of faith he will not concede that anyone be punished, since he said above that this is permitted to none but a pious and good Magistrate. And so all things ought not here to be confounded, but to each its own be attributed, and the heavenly kingdom be distinguished from the earthly. Christ does not touch those things which are outside his kingdom. But those things which pertain to his kingdom he delivers all. And so all things are to be sought from him. But of these more hereafter.
Calvin 64.
It is indeed true, I confess, that the kingdom of Christ neither was erected from the beginning by armed force, nor stands by the protection of arms. For by the preaching of the Gospel Christ must reign, that that prophecy be fulfilled: "A people whom I had not known was made subject to me: at the hearing of the ear it obeyed me." And so the Lord, that he might be the more illustrious by the efficacy of his own voice, sent the Apostles naked and unarmed, nor only willed [them] to be destitute of earthly power, but to have well-nigh the whole world hostile, so that it might be established to all that the victory of the Gospel was Heavenly, while it emerged like a palm against so many and so arduous obstacles. Nor indeed otherwise in this age, in which it has either risen anew or still flourishes, than with the world unwilling and resisting. By its own power, therefore, not by the hand of men, is Religion sustained. (1) Nor in vain does Christ arm the ministers of the Gospel more with power than with the faculty of Warring, because it befits [them] to be so animated that the doctrine which they assert with the tongue, they may not hesitate to seal with their own blood, as often as there shall be need.
Vaticanus.
These things are truly said: would that he persevered in these. (1) It is so, if you understand it concerning the true Religion: but your Religion is sustained by the hand of men. For take away the violence and persecutions of your Magistrate, and restore the liberty of speaking or writing—which, handed down by Paul ("for you can," he says, "all prophesy"), you have taken away—and you will see what free truth can do. But if you deny this, make the trial; let the outcome be judge. This you yourself seem to perceive, who so anxiously take care lest it be permitted to others to speak or write: which assuredly you would never do, unless you stood by the hand of men.
Calvin 65.
First, the princes of the world for the most part oppose true and sound doctrine.
Vaticanus.
And so, armed with this Law of Calvin's, they will kill those who hold true and sound doctrine.
Calvin 66.
Then, he exasperates by his rigor many to whom [it] smiled for a short time.
Vaticanus.
Therefore Calvin's doctrine is not sound. For both I have experienced, and many daily experience, that there is so little rigor in it that there is none more joyful on earth. And so those who fall away from the Pope to Calvin feel themselves lightened, not burdened: and much more easily do they bear the doctrine of the Genevan Pope than of the Roman.
Calvin 67.
Few are they who willingly submit the neck to the yoke to be borne; and just as they make delights for themselves, so (1) they crave bland and honeyed Teachers, who would servilely flatter [them].
Vaticanus.
And so, when the matter is conducted by suffrages in causes of Religion—just as it was conducted in the Servetus affair—the greater part will conquer the better, and those few will always be conquered by the more numerous. (1) Who exactly the bland teachers are, he will easily judge who has tasted both ways, namely of the bland and of the severe.
Calvin 68.
Now we see that the succession of princes is not equal, but a son, as it were by a violent storm, suddenly overturns the well-composed state [given] by the father to the church.
Vaticanus.
And so, even if today the Genevan state were well-composed, tomorrow it would be overturned; and if today some impious heretic were burned, tomorrow the same would be done to a pious man.
Calvin 69.
But it by no means becomes the ministers of the Gospel to waver, flexible to whatever breeze; rather, in whatever direction the minds of men incline, and however dissimilar the condition of the times may be, [it becomes them] to hold a straight course, lest the eternal and immutable truth of God, which has been deposited with them, fall through their perfidious cowardice.
Vaticanus.
If Calvin had done this, he would not have changed his opinion concerning persecution, to be silent about the rest. But if he had changed for the better, I would praise [it]; but to have changed for the worse is vicious.
Calvin 70.
Thus we see that the ancient Prophets, although sometimes, with the Kings propitious, they used and were aided by their protection, were nevertheless tossed by various tempests from time to time, so that it cannot be denied that all served under the banner of the cross of Christ. This certainly is common to all ages: that whoever faithfully serve Christ do not please men.
Vaticanus.
But Men, by Calvin's opinion, will be judges in the cause of religion: and so they will always condemn those who will not please them. Calvin indeed they will not condemn: for he wishes that the Magistrate judge concerning heretics. But they judge by suffrages, in which—since the greater part conquers the better (that there are few good, Calvin said above)—the Calvinists must please the greater, that is, the worse part, that he may be able to oppress the [several] Servetuses; otherwise he could not.
Calvin 71.
They object that it is absurd that the spiritual kingdom of Christ rest upon the power of the flesh—just as [it is] absurd that faith be founded on human eloquence. Now if anyone should order all who are eloquent to fall silent, and prohibit all the learned and those polished in the Liberal arts from the pulpit, lest eloquence empty out the faith—will he not openly do an injury to God and to his gifts? Paul indeed orders all who seem to be wise to become foolish, that they may be made Disciples of Christ: namely, that the wisdom of the flesh being reduced to order, faith alone may stand eminent. But if no reason hinders the preaching of the Gospel from being efficacious only by the power of the spirit, and yet being able to have human arts as handmaids: so neither does Religion, although it is contained by the hand of God alone, and triumphs under the cross, shrink from legitimate aids, where it has so seemed [good] to God.
Vaticanus.
An absurd and fallacious similitude, and the same obscurely handled. For Calvin has this [trait], that whenever he wishes to deceive Readers or hearers, he speaks obscurely, that by this obscurity he may cover the absurdity, and thus escape like an eel in turbid water. But let us, this obscurity being shaken off, explain his similitude. Just as, he says, eloquence can be employed for Teaching the Gospel, although it is not necessary: so for guarding Religion the sword can be employed, although it is not necessary. In this similitude this is absurd, that he joins the sword to religion as eloquence to doctrine. For in truth the sword is not employed for Religion as speech [is] for doctrine. Without speech it cannot be taught, but without the sword religion stands, nor does it require the sword any more than riches or the plough. Otherwise, in imitation of Calvin, it would be permissible thus to reason: just as eloquence can be employed by a physician for persuading the sick man, so the sword [can be employed] for guarding his health. These things are most absurd. But this man so desires the sword that he is eager to mix it into all things. But we can bring forward a far more fitting similitude. Just as for fighting there is need of a sword, nor does it matter whether it be polished or rusty, provided it be sharp: so for Teaching the Gospel there is need of speech, nor does it matter whether it be unadorned or ornate, provided it have the sharpness of the spirit. And as battles are conducted with the sword, not with words: so Religion is to be conducted with words, not with the sword.
Calvin 72.
"The things which are foolish of the world God has chosen, that he might confound the wise": but he who made the beginning from fishermen, afterward chose for himself other (1) ministers, not so rude and tinged with more elegant doctrine.
Vaticanus.
The things which are foolish of the world Calvin with his own [followers] has rejected, that he might extol the wise: and so to the office of teaching or preaching they admit no one unless skilled in the sciences and tongues, especially Latin. Christ himself, if he came, unless he spoke Latin, would not be admitted by these men. This is certain. There was a certain preacher at Lausanne, a man of blameless life, but who knew not Latin: him these men compelled to listen to Corderius teaching boys the Latin tongue. The man, now rather advanced in age, not bearing this indignation, departed. So much is placed by these men in the Latin speech. Finally, if you compare them with Christ, you will find them utterly contrary. Christ gives thanks to the Father, that he has concealed his secrets [from] the wise and learned, and has laid them open to little ones and the unlearned: these men give thanks, that he has concealed them [from] little ones and the unlearned, and has laid them open to the wise. The Church of Christ glories that it has not many wise, not many noble: these men glory in the wise and noble alone. (1) He speaks of those teachers who began to destroy the church constructed by the fishermen: for after human arts began to be mixed with Theology by them, all things thereafter went into the worse; vain and useless questions (Paul forbidding [it]) were introduced, by which that chaste truth is so entangled and obscured—not to say extinguished—that it cannot be restored, unless one return to the same fishermen and the unlearned, so that that [saying] may be true: "Out of the mouth of infants and sucklings you have perfected praise." An example of this matter is to be seen in Calvin himself. Farel in the beginning taught the Gospel with great ardor, rejected profane authors as a plague, threw away any Poet he had laid hold of as dung, was so averse from avenging an injury that, although he bore the gravest and most numerous injuries on account of the Gospel, he never called anyone to law—nay rather, he always deterred his own from avenging his cause. By these arts he made progress in putting errors to flight and sowing charity. But after Calvin was introduced, [and] mixed profane sciences with sacred, [and] devised a thousand ways of avenging injuries, covered with the garment of Christ, [things] went so far into the worse, that Farel himself (alas, the grief) now calvinizes, and has learned vices from him whom he ought to have taught virtues. So great power had that falsely named science, to use Paul's words. Finally, the more was always attributed to human and profane sciences, the further was one led astray from the divine and sacred. And those who so studiously cultivate them, and for that purpose construct and maintain colleges, do nothing else than arm soldiers against that simple and just truth. But let them equip [them] as much as they will: God will do what he once did, that armed in the name of God, David may with his sling cast down that monstrous, prodigious Goliath, and cut off his head with his very own sword.
Calvin 73.
Nay, even Paul himself, who confesses that he is destitute of eloquence, was not devoid of all doctrine, as his other colleagues [were], but from boyhood had been educated (1) in the Law.
Vaticanus.
(1) Rightly in the Law, but you exalt the profaned sciences. Although what you say about Paul is against you. For, inflated with that doctrine of the letter, he persecuted the truth far more keenly than he otherwise would have done. And so afterward, having attained the true science—that is, Christ and charity—he held that knowledge of the letter for dung, which now Calvin exalts.
Calvin 74.
But to be silent about the successors: the speech of Isaiah, not only pure and shining, but also adorned with artifice (1), testifies sufficiently that eloquence is sometimes a handmaid of faith.
Vaticanus.
(1) It is not so: for that prophetic spirit does not sport with artifice in the human manner, nor did holy men of God speak by human will or art, but by the impulse of the Holy Spirit. There was therefore in Isaiah a native, not an art-sought eloquence, which differs from that which these men affect by as much as the beauty of an honorable matron [differs] from the paint of a harlot. But these men wish to mix the sacred with the profane. So did Sebastian Castalio do before, who in the preface to his Latin Moses attributed to Moses all the arts which Plutarch had before attributed to Homer, and for that purpose uses great apparatus, as though about to do something distinguished: as though indeed Moses did not write by divine impulse, with no account at all had of any art. But light men draw all things to their own design, and, that they may extol the human arts (of which they are disciples), they mix them with sacred authors, so that, like deadly flies, they may by their contact infect the fragrant ointment of sacred eloquence.
Calvin 75.
And certainly Christ no less led the magi to himself by the rays of a star (1), than the shepherds by the voice of an angel.
Vaticanus.
(1) And he would lead the Calvinists, if they would deign to lower themselves so as to descend into the stable. For God spurns not the Learned but the proud. But because the learned for the most part labor with pride (for knowledge puffs up), it comes about that few of the learned are saved. There will be indeed some of the learned who will lower themselves to the manger of Christ, such as the Magi were, the rest despising him, such as the Scribes and Pharisees were. But shepherds and the unlettered will be the first and the most, to whom the angel announces the nativity of Christ—whereas these men hold the unlettered of no account. For if some shepherd or unlettered [man] came to them with something announced concerning the truth, I know for certain they would be no more moved than if an ass brayed.
Calvin 76.
Wherefore neither did the rude and obscure common crowd, out of which Christ took the first-fruits of his church (1), preclude the way to kings, but that they too should offer both themselves and all their own to him, and that the very power of the sword, with which they are equipped, should be a sacred oblation.
Vaticanus.
Paul delivers that Christ, when he had ascended on high, gave Apostles, and Prophets, and Evangelists, and Teachers for the composing of the saints, for the work of the ministry, for the building up of the body of Christ—that is, of the Church. These are the sacred oblations for this sacred work. The sword indeed is not employed for the kingdom of Christ, except for killing Christ and his own. There are indeed those who bear the sword, ministers of God, but for punishing malefactors, not for building up the kingdom of Christ. And this is common to Christians with the other nations, which themselves also bear the same sword, nor yet serve the kingdom of Christ.
Calvin 77.
Now we see how, to bear the cross, to undergo the hatreds and reproaches of the world, the ministers of the Gospel ought to be ready: just as the Lord equipped them with no other arms than those of patience: and yet Kings are ordered to protect the doctrine of piety (1) with their patronage.
Vaticanus.
(1) To kill a man is not to protect doctrine, but is to kill a man. When the Genevans killed Servetus, they did not defend doctrine, but killed a man. To protect doctrine is not [the office] of the Magistrate (what has the sword to do with Doctrine?) but of the teacher. But to protect the teacher is [the office] of the Magistrate, just as to protect against injury the farmer and the smith and the physician and the rest. And so if Servetus had wished to kill Calvin, the Magistrate would rightly have defended Calvin. But since Servetus fought with reasons and writings, he was to be repelled with reasons and writings.
Calvin 78.
They object then that nothing is less fitting than that men be violently compelled to faith (which consists of a willing obedience). I—to omit what Augustine usefully disputes truly and skillfully to this opinion, namely that those are drawn unwilling who resist God omnipotent, so that, subdued by chastisement, they may come of their own accord—I answer that in guarding the state of the Church there is another use of the sword than that anyone be compelled to faith.
Vaticanus.
"No one can come to me, unless my father shall have drawn him." These men wish men to be drawn by the Magistrate, [men] resisting God who draws [them]—namely, that the Magistrate may accomplish more than God. "Namely, subdued by chastisement, they will come of their own accord." But if they came of their own accord, they would be deterred: just as fishes, which come of their own accord to the bait or net, flee if you wish to compel [them]. Witness Geneva and part of Savoy, which admitted the Gospel compelled by force. For there are so few there who do not hate the preachers capitally, and most avidly desire the mass, that it is incredible to be told. I testify the whole of that people.
Calvin 79.
For granting that it is not in the hand and arbitration of princes to penetrate by their edicts into the hearts of men, so that, obediently embracing the doctrine of salvation, they submit themselves to God: this nevertheless their calling demands (1), that they not permit the sacred name of God to be lacerated by impure and petulant tongues, and not allow his worship to be ruined.
Vaticanus.
Just now he had praised Augustine, who disputes usefully that the unwilling are drawn, that they may come of their own accord; now, forgetful of himself, he confesses that the edicts of Kings do not penetrate into the hearts of men, so that, obediently embracing the doctrine, they submit themselves to God—which things are plainly contrary: for to come of one's own accord and to embrace the doctrine obediently is the same thing. (1) These things are said accusatorily and maliciously. For it is not straightaway an impure and petulant tongue, if anyone dissents from Calvin in the Lord's Supper, or in the Baptism of children, or in Predestination, or in persecution, provided he believes the sacred Letters to be true.
Calvin 80.
A private man, who has not the power of life and death, if he should allow the house over which he presides to be polluted by sacrileges, will not be free from fault: how much fouler will be the cowardice of a prince, if he should connive at an unbridled violation of piety? Will he sit, forsooth, as Judge on a lofty and magnificent throne, and silently bear the glory of Christ being shamefully cast down (1), and—asserting empire for himself by the fear of penalties—concede to the wicked the license of dissipating the church?
Vaticanus.
He proceeds to exaggerate the matter rhetorically rather than Christianly, that we may understand how much more he has profited in profane arts than in sacred. For what does he call an unbridled violation of piety? Let Servetus be set before us, out of hatred of whom Calvin writes all these things. Servetus denies that infants are to be baptized. Does Servetus think so as he says, or does he not think [so]? And this thought Calvin calls an unbridled violation of piety. By what reasoning? For an unbridled violation is understood in him who sins knowingly: Servetus, if he sins, does it unknowing. Whether therefore do you kill Servetus, Calvin, because he thinks so, or because he speaks so? If you kill [him] because he speaks as he thinks, you kill [him] on account of the truth: for it is truth to say what you think, even if you err; and the 15th psalm pronounces him blessed who truly says what he has in his mind: and you kill him? But if you kill [him] because he thinks so, teach him to think otherwise, or show from the sacred letters that those erring and thinking badly are to be killed.
Calvin 81.
Therefore pious Magistrates will be vindicators of the doctrine of piety, not only that they may compel the less voluntary to (1) Faith, but lest Christ be exiled from that dominion (2) in which by his benefit they reign; lest his sacred name be subject with impunity to mockeries (3), by whose splendor their power is honorable; lest the impious act insolently against his doctrine (4), which contains their tranquil empire; lest they see the weak, of whom they have been divinely appointed guardians, snatched into destruction, while they are silent and at ease (5).
Vaticanus.
(1) That this cannot be done he had just confessed; now again he affirms [it], and fights basely with himself: to say nothing meanwhile that he now attributes to the Magistrate this which in the first Institutes he had attributed to the Pastor. For there, Chapter 6, page 427, he thus discourses concerning the word of God: "These are those secret spiritual weapons of God for the demolition of fortifications, by which they may demolish the counsels [against] the faith—soldiers—and every loftiness which is exalted against the knowledge of God, and may lead captive every understanding to the obeying of Christ, and have a ready vengeance against all disobedience." Behold piety plainly and perspicuously defined, with which it befits the pastors of the church—by whatever name they be at last called—to be endowed: namely, that with the word of God (of which they have been placed ministers and dispensers) they confidently dare all things: to whose Majesty they may compel all the glory, virtue, sublimity of the world to yield; that it itself may command all from the highest even to the lowest; that they may build the house of Christ, subvert the house of Satan, feed the sheep, reprove, rebuke, refute the rebellious and obstinate, loose, bind, lighten [as with lightning], and finally fulminate—but all in the word of God. These are the things which in that time Calvin wrote, yet doing all by the word: by the word compelling the rebellious to the obedience of faith, by the word killing the Wolves, by the word finally flashing and fulminating. Now out of his word (since it has no powers) he forges a sword, and assigns this province to the Magistrates, that they may compel by the sword the less voluntary to faith. In which again he basely dissents from himself. For in the last Institutes, printed by Robert in the year 1553, Chapter 8, Distinction 170 (these things therefore I thus diligently note, lest anyone err, if perchance Calvin shall afterward, as he is wont, have changed these things) he thus writes: "There is therefore a far different rationale, because neither does the Church take to itself whatever is proper to the Magistrate: nor can the Magistrate accomplish this which is performed by the Church." These things Calvin there—separating the Magistrate (to say it in passing) from the Church, as if the Pastors alone are the Church. There therefore he denies that the Magistrate can accomplish this which is performed by the church; now he says it is the Magistrate's office to compel to faith the less voluntary—which nevertheless in the first Institutes he had attributed to the word, not the sword. O versatile man, changing all things on the spur of the moment at his own arbitration. O blind men who follow this blind man, when at length will you open your eyes? (2) Namely, Christ would be more exiled [from] Geneva, if Servetus had not been killed. (3) Nay, then [his name] is most subject to mockeries and is ill spoken of, when those are killed who confess it. (4) And then the impious tyrants act most insolently, when they think it permitted to themselves to rage, with Calvin as master. (5) That is, the Calvinists, whom forsooth Servetus snatched into destruction.
Calvin 82.
They hold forth besides that the gentleness which appeared in the master is to be cultivated by Christ's disciples. "For neither," they say, "did he compel the obstinate into his obedience by arms, but tried to allure all blandly by doctrine. That he might fulfill that prophecy of Isaiah: 'Behold my servant will not wrangle nor contend, nor will his cry be heard in the streets: a bruised reed he will not break, and the smoking wick he will not extinguish.'" As though indeed a contrary exception were not at hand for us: as Christ performed the office of teacher, so a scourge was snatched up by him, with which he purged the temple of God from profane huckstering. (1) If the Son of God routed by an onslaught those who under the pretext of divine worship were selling victims in the court of the temple, why should pious Magistrates not exert the sword committed to them for coercing the perfidious (2) apostates, who profane and violate the whole temple of God with open contumely?
Vaticanus.
(1) And it would be permissible for us too, in Calvin's manner, thus to argue. If the Son of God did not condemn the adulteress, why should the Magistrate condemn adulterers? But if Calvin shall say that he was not a judge in [the case of] the adulteress: we will say the same about the scourge: for neither did the Law (which Christ, if he had borne the office of Magistrate, ought to have followed) prescribe this penalty of the scourge for those hucksters. Likewise, if Christ only ejected those hucksters, [and] did not kill [them], then the Magistrate ought only to eject the Apostates, not to kill [them]. Likewise, if Christ did it with his own hand, the magistrate ought to do it with his [own] hand, not through an executioner. If Christ ejected them from a temple made with hands, the magistrate ought to do the same. But if Calvin shall say that that temple was a figure of the temple not made with hands—that is, of the heart of man (for the temple of God is holy, which you are. Likewise: Your bodies are temples of the Holy Spirit)—we too will say by the same reasoning, that that scourge made with hands was a figure of the spiritual scourge—that is, of the word of God, which word has been delivered not to the magistrate, but to the pastors, through which they ought to kill the Wolves, subvert the house of Satan, and perform all the other things which a little before we cited from Calvin himself. Likewise, if Christ repelled injury neither from himself nor from others, but suffered many in his time—and especially John the Baptist—to be unjustly killed, the Magistrate ought to do the same. Finally, if it pleases [Calvin] to recall the office of the Magistrate to the life of Christ, we shall have an unarmed Magistrate, who could not kill even Robbers, much less heretics. Let us rather, without sophistical subtleties, thus argue, and cut Calvin's throat with his own sword. If Christ did not mandate to the Magistrate that those hucksters be ejected from the temple of the Lord, nor gave himself as an accuser against them, but himself ejected them with his own hand and his own weapon, Calvin (who wishes himself to be the vicar of Christ) ought not to have mandated to the Magistrate that Servetus be ejected, much less killed, nor to have given himself, or his cook, as an accuser against him, but himself with his own hand and his own weapon—that is, the word—to eject, conquer, beat, afflict with contumelies, compel, slaughter, finally to flash and fulminate. But since there was in his heart so ardent a spirit of vengeance (just as Servetus reproaches him in this Book of Calvin's own, page 104), this wisdom did not enter into his malevolent soul. And so, forgetful of his office, he most basely perpetrated homicide, and now, that he may cover it, he contrives moreover infinite homicides in this Book, and endeavors to draw all the divine oracles to his own design, as though all had covenanted with him that they would believe whatever he blurted out, as his disciples are wont. Now, now, Calvin, your arts are laid open: God sees you, and men will see [you] shortly. (2) This man ineptly compares all things—hucksters with apostates, and Christ with the Magistrate, and to rout with to coerce: whereas neither was Christ a Magistrate, nor do apostates huckster in the temple of the Lord, nor is to rout or eject the same as to coerce with the sword. For an Apostate is he who of his own accord goes out of the temple of the Lord—that is, the church. How then can he huckster in the church, or be ejected thence, who is outside? Rather Calvin himself and his like huckster in the temple of the Lord, and are truly Canaanites—that is, merchants: for this is what the name Canaan signifies. First, they teach for nothing but a stipend. I know that he who serves the altar ought to live from the altar: but these men are so addicted to the stipend that, if you take away from them the stipends, not a twentieth part of them would remain. When the Interim had been introduced into the County of Montbéliard, by which the stipends were cut off from the Preachers, there were there a great part who were unwilling to continue preaching, at least until Easter, which was at hand—although their chief exhorted them. At Lausanne indeed they huckster so, that there are always there [men] standing ready, who thus hunt after the office of preaching, just as in the Papacy benefices, Abbacies, and the other merchandise are awaited. And to such a degree has it proceeded in hucksterings and Lawsuits (for they litigate continually among themselves just like merchants), that the Bernese Magistrate—when he was continually deafened by their litigations—deducted from each one a hundred florins of that coinage from their annual stipends. A prudent Magistrate indeed, who, since he understood that those merchants rejoiced in nothing more than Gain, judged that they could be coerced by no fitting penalty, or better, than by loss. And this is the first part of their huckstering. The other [is] that out of Theology they have made an art similar to the human [arts]. And so to the office of preaching they admit no one unless he be skilled in the human sciences, and unless he know the tongues—that is, Hebrew, Greek, and Latin, or at least Latin: for these three, which are dead (inasmuch as they are now in use among no people), they alone reckon among the Tongues; the rest, which are living and which peoples use, they hold in no place. And so if anyone should know of the other Tongues even twenty, if he is ignorant of those three, he will nowhere be called trilingual: but if he shall have learned those three, even if he be skilled in others, yet he is only called trilingual: so highly do these men value what the common people are ignorant of. Besides, they admit no one unless he be sent by them: but they send no one except him who agrees with them in all things, and approves all their sayings and deeds, and not only assents to them but also flatters [them]. He who has not received this mark from them, to him it is not permitted to exercise his memory [i.e., to preach]. Christ himself, if he came, unless he at least knew Latin, and unless he were sent by them, I scarcely know whether he would be admitted.
But now, when they huckster the souls of men, it must not be passed over that they interpret the Church in two ways. But let us rather set down the words of Calvin out of the last Institutes, Chapter 8, in the expositions of the fourth part of the creed: "In two ways," he says, "do the sacred letters speak concerning the Church: for sometimes when they name the Church, they understand that which truly is before God, into which none are received except those who both are sons of God by the grace of adoption, and true members of Christ by the sanctification of the Spirit. But very often by the name of the Church they designate the whole multitude of men diffused throughout the globe of the earth, which professes to worship one God and Christ: is initiated into his faith by Baptism: by the participation of the Supper testifies its unity in true doctrine and charity: has confession in the word of the Lord, and conserves for his preaching the ministry instituted by him. In this, however, very many Hypocrites are intermingled, who have nothing of Christ but the title and appearance: others very many [are] ambitious, avaricious, envious, evil-speaking, nefarious, some of impurer life—who are tolerated for a time, either because they cannot be convicted by legitimate judgment, or because that severity of discipline which ought [to] does not always flourish. As therefore it is necessary for us to believe the church invisible, conspicuous to the eyes of God alone, so [it is necessary] to observe this [church] which is called church in respect of men, and to cultivate its communion." Likewise, Distinction 9: "Again, nevertheless, because he saw it to be somewhat expedient that we should know who are to be held by us for his sons, he in this part accommodated himself to our capacity. And since there was not the certainty of faith, he substituted in its place a certain judgment of charity, by which we may acknowledge as members of the church those who, both by the confession of faith, and by the example of life, and by the participation of the sacraments, profess the same God and Christ with us." These are the words of Calvin.
Such a church do these men observe and have—namely, that which is called church in respect of men, in which the certainty of faith is not necessary, but only a certain judgment of Charity, concerning which charity they judge without the certainty of faith. In this are intermingled very many hypocrites and the others whom he commemorates. I ask therefore, why does he not eject those? Is it because they cannot be convicted by judgment? Nay, far more easily than heretics: for concerning Hypocrites, the ambitious, the avaricious—who exactly they are, there is no controversy, especially concerning Hypocrites, who come in the clothing of sheep, and are known by their fruits—that is, by homicides—just as the Wolf [is known] by the eating of sheep. But concerning Heretics there is controversy, nor could Calvin yet show what a Heretic is, although he writes by name concerning Heretics to be killed. Why therefore did he never burn anyone on account of avarice or envy, since he knows very many, and could both accuse and convict [them]? Is it because that severity of discipline which ought [to] does not flourish at Geneva? Nay, it flourishes, with Calvin as author. For above he showed that it is [the office] of none but a good Magistrate to persecute Heretics. Which, since he persuaded his Magistrate of [it] against Servetus, he holds his Magistrate for good. Why therefore can he not obtain from him that he kill those Hypocrites and the avaricious? Or are Hypocrites better to him than Heretics? Or if they are not, why does he have a Magistrate more compliant against Heretics than against those? But why do I pursue these things? Calvin does not hold for great vices those things which God holds for the greatest, as appears in those laws which he founded concerning the administration of the Church, in which he makes [several] kinds of vices: one of those which in a minister can in no way be tolerated, of which he places first heresy, among the rest Games forbidden by the Laws, and dances. The other of tolerable [vices], in which he places scurrility, mendacity, calumny, avarice, excessive irascibility, and contentions. These vices are tolerable to him, whereas the sacred letters find nothing worse than buffoons—so much so that in the 1st Psalm they are placed among the worst—and the avaricious, since Paul calls avarice the root of all evils. But these vices Calvin can tolerate, which are most notorious: Heresy, games, and dances he cannot. What would you do with this man, who thinks scurrility and avarice and calumny lighter vices than Heresy or games and dances? And so therefore for the avaricious, the envious, the evil-speaking, informers, calumniators, liars, Hypocrites (of which Hypocrites Calvin made no mention in those Laws—of so little moment with him is hypocrisy) there is easily a Place among these men: but if anyone dissent from them in any matter, even if he be otherwise of blameless morals, he is a Heretic, he cannot be borne.
I will set down and narrate one example, from which the rest may be perceived. Johannes Gastius, a preacher of Basel, narrates in his Book on the Anabaptists, that there was a certain turner, an Anabaptist, condemned to capital [punishment] at Basel, but Oecolampadius obtained grace for him from the Magistrate, provided that he recant. He recanted, and afterward was a most wicked man, nor yet was anyone troublesome to him on account of his flagitious life. I will set down the words of Gastius himself: "He revokes," he says, "with a laughing mouth in the Church: but what shall I say? Afterward no one ever saw a more wicked, more obscene man, and one of more brazen forehead. He boasted himself before his own, [saying] that when he was zealous for probity, [and] adhered to sound Religion, he had suffered persecution: then, when he overflowed with crimes, drank till morning, whored, led dances, [he was] corrected by no one." These things Johannes Gastius.
Thus therefore they teach: provided there be sound doctrine and use of the sacraments, the Church is present: that sins cannot be conquered: that we are and will be flesh until death. By these methods they flatter the people, and continually incite the Magistrate, ignorant of the matter, against Heretics (so they call those who dissent from them); they teach that nothing is worse than these or worthy of greater punishment. And so to have been rebaptized is among them far more capital than to commit adultery, or to envy, or to speak evil, to play with dice, to consume uselessly the time than which nothing is dearer, to wander hither and thither as all Whoremongers are wont, to pass the years of one's adolescence with dishonorable women, or to be proud or unjust, a detractor, or one seeking his own advantage, or avaricious, or a buffoon, or a calumniator. They order the rebaptized to be killed, not from the sacrosanct scriptures, but from the opinion of Zwingli, who brought forth this excellent law: that whoever has been immersed, let him be immersed [i.e., drowned]. And although they cry that they do all things from the sacred scriptures, yet they have not been able to show thence one syllable, which commands [a man] to be killed, if he err in the understanding of the Supper or of Baptism. O Magistrates, those who lead you, mislead you. These are truly profane hucksterings of souls: by these methods they vivify those souls which God does not vivify, and kill those which God does not kill. And so when the Son of God shall have come, he will eject those merchants, not with the scourge with which they themselves eject us (for to them the scourge of Christ is the sword of the executioner) but with his fiery and unconquered speech, since they profane and violate the whole holy temple of God with open contumely and blasphemy.
Calvin 83.
(1) Here indeed Christ exhibited one specimen of this holy Zeal. But in the rest of the course of his calling he abstained from the external kind of correction, lest he mix the spiritual kingdom committed to him by the Father with earthly empires. For he who refused to be the arbiter of an inheritance to be divided among brothers, nevertheless wishes each man's right to be preserved for him by pious judges.
Vaticanus.
(1) But if nevertheless he gave an example to the Magistrate, why not also thereafter? But if not, why is that drawn to the Magistrate (2) rather than this? Or who will distinguish for us when he is to be imitated, or is not?
Calvin 84.
To these things, he distinctly notes that the prophecy of Isaiah, which commends the gentleness in Christ, does not regard all promiscuously.
Vaticanus.
Yet he himself is the gate, through which whoever does not enter is a thief and a Robber. "Learn of me," he says (addressing all the burdened), "who am meek, and I will refresh you." But Calvin does not think this gentleness pertains to himself: and so he is not refreshed.
Calvin 85.
A placid and meek custom is attributed to Christ, by which he may sustain the weak, not by which he may more harden a robust malice.
Vaticanus.
Yet he bore the robust malice of Judas, nor yet hardened [him]: nor would Calvin have hardened Servetus, if he had borne [with him]: rather he hardened [him] by not bearing [with him]. For men are not hardened by gentleness, but rather are invited to repentance.
Calvin 86.
Christ is meek so as not to break the bruised reed: is he therefore [meek] also so as to foster the obstinacy of those who break the weak?
Vaticanus.
Yet he bore the Scribes and Pharisees, who broke the weak and called the people away from the truth, nor yet fostered their obstinacy: for obstinacy is not fostered by patience, but rather is broken, if by any means it can be broken: for evil is conquered by good, not by evil.
Calvin 87.
He is meek so as to spare the smoking wicks: is he therefore [meek] so as not to dispel the darkness?
Vaticanus.
By what means should he dispel [it]? By the sword or by Light?
Calvin 88.
Nay rather, he is armed with an iron scepter, with which he may crush the heads of his enemies.
Vaticanus.
To crush heads is not to dispel darkness: and the iron scepter of Christ the sacred Letters call a powerful and impregnable fervor, and the spirit of his mouth, with which he will lay low Antichrist, as we cited above from Calvin himself. Where [is] the sword?
Calvin 89.
I know indeed that, although Christ uses no other sword than that of the word, vengeance is ready for him against all the impious: (1) but we will not say that Peter was acted by any other than his spirit, when he struck Ananias and Sapphira with sudden death: nor will we say that Paul was unlike his Master, when he avenged himself on Elymas the magus with blindness.
Vaticanus.
This man marvelously mocks us: he uses the adversative conjunction "But," and yet sets down nothing contrary to what he had said. For Peter used no other than the sword of the word. And this is the very thing which we say, that the sword of Christ is the word. But Peter was acted by the same spirit: who denies [it]? But show that he used that sword which you used in Servetus. Then he falsely accuses Peter: for Ananias was not killed by Peter, but only chided and reprehended. But if, astounded by the chiding, he perished, this ought to be attributed not to the chider, but to God the avenger: otherwise it will have to be said that even Nabal was killed by Abigail his wife, since after she narrated to him in how great peril he had been, the man stiffened, and after some days died. And the same I say about Sapphira. For what Peter said to her—"The feet of those who have buried your husband are at the doors, who will carry you out"—he predicted death, [he] did not inflict [it]. Otherwise it will have to be said that the Prophets, as often as they predicted death to anyone, also brought it on—which is false. The same I say about Paul and Elymas. But let it be granted that Ananias was killed by Peter, since God held his sentence ratified, and what Peter bound on earth, that was bound also in heaven. To what end is this cited by Calvin? For whether ought the pastor to imitate Peter, or the Magistrate? Certainly the pastor: for Peter did not bear the office of Magistrate. Why therefore did not Calvin strike Servetus with the word, just as Peter [struck] Ananias? Why did he mandate that province to the executioner? He could not, if only that opinion of Calvin's which we mentioned is true—that the Magistrate can do nothing which is performed by the Church.
Calvin 90.
Nor do I see to what the "powers," which the same Paul commemorates among the gifts of the Spirit, pertain, unless that the severity of the Spirit might be in force for striking terror into the contemners.
Vaticanus.
Paul glories that power has been given him for building up, not for destroying: and Christ says that he came not to destroy, but to save. And so for this matter he gave gifts of healing and consoling, and the other powers: gifts of killing, that he gave [them], I have not read: but that he will abolish all power, this I have read. The word indeed he gave, powerful and unconquered, by which the obstinate might be compelled, but the sword he did not give. Those powers were for saving, these of Calvin for destroying: those new, this drawing its origin all the way from Cain. And those long ago ceased, just as Paul foretold would come to pass, that prophecies and tongues would cease, which same we see has happened concerning miracles. And so Calvin himself, who glories about these powers, yet lacks them. For he would never have killed Servetus with any other than the weapon of the word, if he had had the powers of the word. But in truth, since with the word he could neither heal nor kill, he omits the whole province of saving (for he came not to save but to destroy), but commits the province of destroying to the Magistrate, lest, if it were done by his own hand, the hypocrisy be detected. For to hypocrites it is not permitted to kill anyone, inasmuch as they are covered with a sheep's skin.
Calvin 91.
Therefore it was permitted to the ministers of the word to coerce the impious with corporal (1) penalty: will princes, whose proper office it is to protect the holy order with the sword, cease in the disposition of faith?
Vaticanus.
He contradicts himself. For he said in the Institutes, as we showed, that the Magistrate can do nothing which is performed by the church. Now he argues thus: If it was permitted to the ministers of the word to coerce the impious with corporal penalty, it is permitted also to princes: whereas thus, from that prior rule of his, it would be permissible to argue: If it is permitted to ministers, it is not permitted to princes. But we will argue thus: Just as the Apostles with their own weapon—that is, the word—punished the guilty whom they had in the power of the word, and that on account of sins which consisted in words and doctrine: so the Magistrate ought with his own weapon—that is, the sword—to punish the guilty whom they have in the power of the sword, and that on account of sins over which the sword has right. And just as Peter could not have punished a homicide with the word, but would have left him to the Magistrate: so the Magistrate cannot punish with the sword a hypocrite, or a heretic, or a liar (for he has no law for it), but ought to leave them to the Pastors to be punished: otherwise the sacred will be mixed with the profane. This plainly Elisha teaches, who, to the king of the Israelites asking whether he ought to kill the Syrians whom Elisha had taken, answered: "You shall not strike [them]: for neither did you take them with your sword and bow, that you should strike [them]." So will the true Pastor say to the Magistrate: "You shall not kill hypocrites, heretics, liars, for neither did you take them with your sword and bow, that you should kill [them]." The Lord will take and kill them with the spirit of his mouth. Kill homicides, ravishers, and the rest, whom you can take with the sword. And, that I may finish this example, I will compare Calvin with Peter, that from the comparison it may be perceived of what sort this imitator of Peter has been. Peter chided Ananias, and if you please (that we may concede this to Calvin), killed [him] by the word alone, without anyone's assistance. Calvin killed Servetus by the sword alone, by the assistance of the Magistrate. Peter [killed] that man on account of a lie, because he had lied to the Holy Spirit: Calvin [killed] this man on account of the truth, because he had been unwilling to lie: for if Servetus had been willing to recant and to speak against [his] conscience, he would have escaped; but because he said what he thought, he perished. Peter by that deed brought it about that others thereafter dreaded to lie: Calvin by this deed brought it about that thereafter others, from fear of penalty, dread to speak the truth. Peter showed them the powers of his word, to the praise of God: Calvin here shows the weakness of his word, to the Praise of the sword, which is Calvin's God. Peter afterward did nothing such, that we know: Calvin wrote this very book pertinaciously and obstinately (1), by name, for the exciting of like persecutions throughout the whole world. Woe to the blind leaders of the blind: Woe, woe also to those who are led by them, unless they here wish to open their eyes, and to be led back into the way.
Calvin 92.
Moreover, that is a cruel clemency which they praise—to expose the sheep as prey, that the wolves may be spared.
Vaticanus.
That the Wolves are killed by the word, and indeed by the pastor, Calvin himself before taught: now he wishes [them killed] by the Magistrate: but they cannot be killed by both. I say nothing of [the fact] that the wolves come in the skin of sheep, but within are ravening wolves; by their fruits you shall know them. But the fruit of a wolf is to eat raw flesh. And so it is not those who are killed, but those who kill, that are wolves.
