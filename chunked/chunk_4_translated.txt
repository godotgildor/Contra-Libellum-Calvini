**Calvin 128.**

Now let Paul's vote be added, by which every scruple is removed. He orders solemn prayers to be offered in the Church for Kings and all who excel in honor and power. To what end? "That we may lead a quiet and tranquil life," he says. Is it only this? Nay rather, "with all piety and temperance." If mention had been made only of modesty or temperance, those who do not wish the cause of religion to be touched by Princes might perhaps pretend some pretext. But since Paul assigns this office to them—that they procure God to be duly worshiped—how great a rashness is it, I ask, to strip them of a power divinely enjoined? Two things here are worth observing. For Paul does not instruct private men to devote themselves to God, but discusses the public function of princes, inasmuch as they are placed upon a throne and equipped with the sword for ruling the human race. He establishes their office not only in protecting each man's right, but teaches that they are ordained for fostering piety.

[Vaticanus]

**Vaticanus.**

On the preceding page Calvin had said that there were no Magistrates to whom the Apostles might prescribe what was right; now he contradicts [himself, saying] that their function is described by Paul and divided into two parts: which is not true. For Paul writes thus: "I exhort therefore, first of all, that supplications, prayers, intercessions, [and] giving of thanks be made for all men, for Kings, and all who are endowed with power, that we may lead a tranquil and quiet life, with all piety and gravity." He shows next to what end he says these things: "For this is honorable [and] acceptable," he says, "to our Savior, who wishes all men to be saved and to come to the knowledge of the truth"—as if he should say this: God must be prayed to for all men, that he may open their mind to them, so that they may come to the knowledge of the truth. And if this should happen, we would serve God tranquilly. Since Kings impede this from happening (for they themselves persecute us on account of Religion, and thus impede tranquility), God must be prayed to that he may open their mind also (since he wishes them too, just as all the rest, to be saved)—not that they may protect Religion with the sword, but that they may cease to assault Religion with the sword. For since the Magistrates and Princes alone persecute the pious ("for you shall be led to Kings and Princes for my name's sake," says Christ), if they cease to persecute, we shall lead a tranquil life with all piety. For no one will interrupt our tranquility on account of crimes, if we commit none. Piety itself, moreover, will easily protect itself, if [their hand] is kept off our bodies.

Not therefore does Paul here demand that princes protect sound doctrine with arms (for against whom would they protect it, except against themselves, since they alone assaulted it with arms?), but that they accept [it], and cease to assault [it]. And if this should happen, tranquility will follow. Just as if Merchants should wish that Robbers become good, so that the Merchants might make their journey tranquilly—not that the Robbers furnish them tranquility, but that they cease to impede it. For certainly in Paul's time the princes were Robbers and Wolves against the Christians, which Christ had judged when he said: "I send you as sheep in the midst of Wolves." And Paul, when he says that he was delivered from the mouth of the Lion.

**Calvin 129.**

Whence it will follow that the sword has been placed in their hand, with which they may defend sound Doctrine: which they cannot accomplish unless it is permitted to restrain impious corruptions by penalties. Let unlearned and little-considered men therefore cease to deny that penalties must be exacted from the corruptors of true Doctrine, unless they wish openly to make an outcry against God.

**Vaticanus.**

Paul calls sound Doctrine that which renders men sound—that is, endowed with Charity and unfeigned Faith and a good conscience; but unsound [Doctrine] that which renders [men] curious, contentious, obstinate, impious, irreligious, profane, parricides, etc., and whatever else is contrary to sound doctrine. But these men look to another rule. For they hold as sound those who agree with them concerning Baptism, concerning the Supper, concerning Predestination, etc. Such men, although they be avaricious, envious, detractors, dissemblers, liars, buffoons, usurers, and whatever else is opposed to sound doctrine, are easily tolerated, nor is anyone killed on account of men's vices, unless someone has committed homicide, or theft, or some atrocious crime of this kind, or has offended the preachers—for this among them is just like the sin against the Holy Spirit, as is now commonly said somewhere in a proverb. But if someone dissents from them concerning Baptism, or the Supper, or Justification, Faith, etc., he is a Heretic, he is a Devil, he is to be opposed by everyone on land and sea, as an eternal enemy of the Church and a nefarious overthrower of sound doctrine—even if otherwise his life is uncorrupted, so clement, patient, kind, merciful, generous, and so religious and God-fearing, that in his conduct neither friends nor enemies have anything they can reprehend. All these virtues, and innocence of life (which Paul does not disdain to commend in himself), cannot protect a man among these people, if he dissents from them on any point of religion, but [that] he is held as impious and blasphemous. These things [are demonstrated], besides that example of the Turner which [I] cited above, also by many others. For among the people of Zurich many men, otherwise of blameless life, were killed merely on account of [their] opinion concerning Baptism, with Zwingli as the author—among whom were two men of Zurich, father and son, who were so pious that scarcely could others such be found in that church, as those who knew them testify, even Zwingli's friends. Again, some, when they had previously lived blamelessly, after they were compelled to recant, thereafter lived so wickedly that no one matched them in disgraceful acts—and yet on account of this disgraceful life they suffered no persecution from anyone. This infamy of persecuting men's religion seems to have raged already in the time of Daniel: for since his enemies could not carp at his life and conduct, they resolved to cavil at his religion. Thus these men, whose life they cannot blame, invade their religion. And in this indeed they act prudently and maliciously: for since they themselves do not excel in conduct, they come to Doctrine, concerning which—since the Magistrate cannot judge of it, as he can of conduct—they more easily impose upon him, and thus, peddling that sound Doctrine of theirs, they oppress the helpless. But Christ, on the contrary, will repudiate their doctrine on the day of Judgment, and will require conduct: for when they say to him, "We ate with you and drank, and you taught in our streets," he will answer them: "I know not whence you are; depart from me, all you workers of iniquity." Here you see that doctrine is displayed in vain, unless there are present morals congruent with the Doctrine—even if Christ himself has taught in our streets; and yet we boast of nothing but the word and the Sacraments. O blind blindness of the blind! O most incurable and most bloody hypocrisy! When at length will you be uncovered, so that Magistrates may thereafter cease to shed at your discretion the blood of those whom they do not know?

Thus far Calvin has collected from all sides everything he could toward shedding the blood of Heretics, nor yet could he find any sacred author who commanded Heretics to be killed. And so, that he might the better deceive Readers, he nowhere defined what a Heretic is. But, having promised that he would dispute concerning heretics, he said many things about false prophets, about worshipers of a strange God, about the impious and blasphemous, and at last concluded—because those are to be killed—that Heretics too are to be killed. A fine conclusion indeed! As if someone, when he has shown that arsonists, and Robbers, and murderers ought to be killed, should infer that therefore drunkards too are to be killed—and yet should bring forward no law which commands drunkards to be killed, but should only try to show by sophistical reasonings that drunkards do no less harm than those, and so are therefore worthy to be killed. Shall we then straightway kill men, led by deceptive and specious inductions? Are not Judges commanded to have the law set before their eyes? Produce the Law concerning heretics being killed: There is none. But there is one concerning false Prophets: What have I to do with false Prophets? You accuse [this man as] a Heretic (the good Judge will say); produce the Law which commands a Heretic to be killed. But Heretics are false Prophets: Show this: I do not believe you. For they do not order a strange God to be worshiped; nor do they predict anything future. But they are blasphemers. Show this also: Show that the blasphemer and the heretic are the same. Could not God have named heretics, if he wished them to be killed? Was he so forgetful, that in so many ages, in so many books, he nowhere made mention of heretics being killed? And now Calvin in the last times writes for us a new Law—although it is written that to the Law of God nothing is to be either added or taken away. But indeed let us show what Calvin himself thinks a heretic [to be], so that it can be perceived to what end the matter will come, if Princes shall have followed his counsel. When we write about a matter commonly known, we are not accustomed to define it; in which indeed we judge [the writer] to think about that matter as the common people do. And so if you should write that Robbers ought to be killed, all peoples who will wish to follow your law will kill those whom they themselves hold for certain to be Robbers. Thus when Calvin writes that heretics are to be killed, all peoples who will wish to obey him will kill those whom they hold for certain to be heretics. And so it will come to pass that the Papists kill Lutherans and Zwinglians and Anabaptists. And the Lutherans, the Papists and Anabaptists and Zwinglians; and likewise all the rest: for no one of the sects doubts that it itself judges rightly, [and that] the rest are heretical. Likewise men will conspire among themselves, with Calvin as the author, after the manner of the Midianites, and so far will the one not spare the other, that they will snatch on the right hand and on the left, what they may eat, and being hungry and not yet satisfied will devour the flesh of their own arm—and with the Manassites [conspiring] with the Ephraimites, and the Ephraimites with the Manassites, against the Jews, not even thus will the wrath of the Lord be appeased, but that he still has his hand stretched out.

For Calvin writes in his Institutes, Chapter 8, section 26, concerning Heretics, in these words: "Heretics and schismatics are those called who, a division having been made, sever the communion of the church: This moreover is contained by two bonds, the agreement of sound Doctrine and fraternal Charity. Whence Augustine places this distinction between Heretics and schismatics: that the former indeed corrupt the sincerity of faith by false dogmas; but the latter sometimes, even in a likeness of faith, rupture the bond of fellowship." Calvin holds for Churches his own, both the Helvetic [churches], and that of Strasbourg, and the Saxon [churches]—that is, the Lutheran [ones]. From these Churches whoever indeed dissent, they themselves are Heretics and corrupt the sincerity of faith, with Calvin himself as the author. Now they must be contained by two bonds, namely the agreement of sound Doctrine and fraternal charity. Now since among those Churches which Calvin embraces there is the greatest dissension, both of Doctrine and of minds (which can most easily be shown), and they are to be held for Heretics by Calvin's verdict—those who break these two bonds—nothing remains except that the Calvinians invade others, and others the Calvinians, and the one, holding the other for heretics, gnaw [at them], until the one shall have finished off the other. And if men shall wish to follow Calvin's counsel, there will be no sect which does not condemn and persecute all the others (for there is no sect which in its own judgment is not the best), so that thus the Ammonites and Moabites and Esauites fall by mutual wounds. Then let the true Jews come upon [them], who without sweat and blood gather the spoils. Now after we have refuted Calvin's sayings, it remains that—what he did not do—we show who truly are heretics, and in what manner [they are] to be treated.

*What a Heretic Is, and In What Manner He Is to Be Treated*

It often happens that words of a foreign language, not understood, bring great and dangerous errors: we see this in the word *ecclesia*, which word, although it signifies a gathering or an assembly, has nevertheless so degenerated that in the vernacular tongues it signifies a temple. The same I say of *Idolum*, which is an image; and yet in this matter there is such error, that those who have images think themselves to be free of Idols. But if each thing were stated in the language of its own nation, this error would have no place. So it happens in the word *heretic*, which is Greek. And when it is named among those ignorant of the Greek language, they think a heretic to be the same as a blasphemer, or a magus, or an atheist, or a worshiper of a strange God, or some monster of that kind so horrendous, that they more easily grant pardon to Robbers and traitors and parricides than to him who is called a heretic. Understanding this error of the common people, these incendiary men, if they wish to oppress someone dissenting from them, call [him] a heretic—the odium of which name not even Christ could escape. But if instead of "heretic" they named [him] "obstinate," this fog of error would be dispelled, and the Judges could judge far more justly concerning the obstinate [man] than concerning the heretic—that is, concerning a known crime, than concerning an unknown one. We therefore will explain the word thus. *Heresis* is a Greek word, which denotes a sect, or an opinion. (1) Now there are some sects [that are] praiseworthy, as in Acts 24 and 28, where the sect of the Nazarenes is named—that is, of the Christians. (2) Others [are] wholly impious, concerning which Peter wrote thus: "But there were also false prophets among the people, as there will be also among you false Teachers, who will introduce pernicious sects, and, denying their Lord who redeemed [them], will bring upon themselves swift destruction; and many will follow their destructions, through whom the teaching of the word will be ill spoken of; and who through avarice will make merchandise of you with fictitious words." (3) Other sects are intermediate, which cannot in themselves be called impious—namely [those] of those who retain Religion, and believe the sacred Scriptures, but understand them wrongly—of which kind were the Scribes, Pharisees, Sadducees, and Essenes. Those who adhere more obstinately to vicious sects of this kind, and do not allow themselves to be recalled to sounder [views], although they are truly and duly admonished—these in Greek are called *heretics*, in Latin *obstinate*. I say "truly admonished" for this reason, that one is not to be held for a heretic if he is falsely admonished—that is, if he is called to a false opinion, not to a true one: As if a Sadducee should have admonished a Pharisee to desist from the opinion which he held concerning the Resurrection, he would wrongly hold the Pharisee for a heretic, and himself for steadfast; and the Sadducee would rather be the obstinate one. Likewise I say "duly admonished," because if the Pharisees, who were avaricious, had admonished someone to desist from avarice (for obstinacy is discerned also in morals), and he nevertheless had not desisted, he would not at once [be] obstinate and rebellious (although he ought to desist), but that [is] rather to be attributed to the fault of the Pharisees, who, since they were avaricious, would use dead and invalid admonitions. For speech has no force which proceeds only from the Lips, not [from] the heart—such as is [that] of actors. But if someone avaricious, admonished by Christ, nevertheless had persisted in avarice, he truly would have been obstinate.

Now let us come to our own age. There are today also three kinds of sects, namely the pious, the impious, and the intermediate. The pious are those who in this age live in a holy and just and pious manner, awaiting the coming of the blessed Lord God, whom they worship in spirit and in truth. The impious are despisers and blasphemers of God, enemies and mockers of all religion, who believe the sacred Scriptures no more—not to say less—than profane [writings]; avaricious men, lustful, and the greatest followers of pleasures. A great part of these are Apostates, who in the beginning believed the Gospel: Then, when they did not persevere, they became Atheists, and there befell them what Christ said—"The evil spirit returned into them, having taken seven others worse"—and so the latter condition of those men became worse than the former. Concerning these Peter prophesied in that place which I mentioned above. The intermediate are those who reverence God indeed, and believe the sacred scriptures, but do not understand them. And concerning the Impious and the Atheists they can easily judge, inasmuch as these betray themselves not only by the impurity of their morals, but also of their words, and do not claim piety for themselves even in words. But concerning the true Christians and sons of God—who they are—there is great controversy, for the reason that of the many sects there is none which does not claim this for itself. If it should come into controversy among many physicians, who is the best, and the people could not judge concerning their doctrine—therefore, since all would argue plausibly, and would cite both Hippocrates and Galen, the people could not judge rightly concerning them—[then it would be better], if, their reasonings being set aside, [the people] judged by the fruit; that is, [if] it awarded the palm to him who had cured the most and the gravest diseases. So when it is dealt with concerning the medicine of souls, since the common people cannot judge a man on the basis of the Masters' disputations, it would be best and most expeditious to judge by the fruit—that is, to think those the best who heal the diseases of souls (that is, vices) the best, and render the greatest number sober out of drunkards, continent out of the intemperate, generous out of the avaricious, patient out of the impatient, clement out of the cruel, chaste out of the lustful. This method of judging Paul handed down: "The works of the flesh are manifest," he says—"namely, Adultery, fornication, impurity, lasciviousness, worship of the stars, sorcery, enmities, Strife, jealousies, fits of anger, contentions, seditions, sects, Envyings, murders, drunkenness, revelings, and the like; but the fruit of the spirit is charity, joy, peace, clemency, kindness, goodness, faith, Mildness, continence." From these fruits it can easily be judged which sect is the best—namely [that] of those who believe Christ and obey [him], and imitate his life, whether they be called Papists, or Lutherans, or Zwinglians, or Anabaptists, or by any other name. For truth is not situated in a name, but in the thing. Those who are such, and for whom it is not enough to say "Lord, Lord," but who in addition obey his will, are truly pious, and sons of God, and can judge concerning Heretics and concerning others. But if any do not obey the admonitions of men of this kind, these you may rightly call heretics and obstinate—just as, if some disease cannot be cured by a good physician who has cured many others, this disease may duly be called obstinate. But if some [disease] could not be cured by a bad physician, you would not on that account call this disease incurable—for perhaps it could be cured, if there were a good Physician. So in morals, if some bad [man] could not be corrected by a bad man, he is not straightway to be thought obstinate—for perhaps he could be corrected, if he obtained a good Master. Just as it sometimes happened that a man was corrected in one hour by a pious man, who for many years could not be corrected by men like himself. So today, morals and holiness of life being neglected, men are accustomed to judge concerning Doctrine, and to kill those who dissent from them in any matter, even if they are otherwise of upright morals—which ought to be done the contrary way. For even if someone had sinned, pardon ought nevertheless to be granted him, if the rest of his life were innocent—just as Jehu said to Jehoshaphat: "Indeed you would pay the penalties of this deed to the Lord," he said, "unless there had existed in you certain praiseworthy things, in that you exterminated the [sacred] Groves from this Region, and applied your mind to the zeal of God." Therefore, since men wish to be judged and killed not on the basis of morals but on the basis of Doctrine, and since which is the best of the sects has not yet been judged, nor can be judged before the coming of the Judge—it is asked in the meantime what is to be done with the obstinate? Here if you counsel that they be killed, men of all sects will be killed among those who are of another sect.

Thus it will come to pass that the Calvinians in France, and the Papists in Geneva, are killed; and the Lutherans by the Zwinglians, and the Zwinglians by the Lutherans, and the Anabaptists by everyone, and all by the Anabaptists, nor will any limit be set to the occasions, until we have all fallen by mutual wounds. Whoever thinks that the Church is built up by this madness of killing makes of Christ a Satan. For thus, by blood, not by charity, is Satan's church accustomed to be built up. Again, if you should order that Heretics be let go with impunity, the thought will arise concerning those impious and atheists and blasphemers, whether they are not to be punished. And so I judge that the matter must be distinguished, and that those impious [men] must be discerned from those who err. And since Calvin, disputing concerning Heretics, confounds everything, and now by attacking blasphemers, now worshipers of strange Gods, now false prophets, has burdened the Heretics with the odium of these names—I will show that those who are held as Heretics are not such. Those impious [men] and despisers of sacred letters, and blasphemers, I do not comprehend under the name of Heretics, but I judge [them] to be treated as impious. If they deny God, if they blaspheme, if they openly speak evil of the holy Doctrine of the Christians, [if they] detest the holy life of the pious, I leave them to the Magistrates to be punished—not on account of Religion, which they have none of, but on account of Irreligion. But if any Magistrate should hold them in bonds, in case perhaps they should correct themselves (since immense is the mercy of God), that Magistrate would not seem to me alien from Christian clemency.

But when it is dealt with concerning heretics—[since] judgment is made concerning them on the basis of the common opinion—it must be seen who today are held for Heretics. Held as Heretics are Papists, Waldensians, Lutherans, Zwinglians, Anabaptists, Schwenckfeldians, and any who are of this kind. Of these sects I say there is none which ought to be called impious in itself, even if all err: for all believe in the same God, and in the same Lord and savior Christ. I know indeed with how many, with how prodigious errors the Pope has filled the world, so that those sects which I just mentioned have deservedly defected from him; I know also how many errors yet remain in the rest, which a greater Light could dispel—although many among them are masters no better than the Pope. But I speak of those who are religious in their own sect. Suppose [there is] a good Papist—that is, one fearing God, who fears to perjure, to kill, to commit adultery, or to bear false witness, or to do to another what he would not wish done to himself: I say this man ought by no reasoning to be called impious, or to be killed; and yet he worships images: what then? He worships by error, not by malice, as we all also worshiped. But if you press the words of the Law, we all ought to have been killed. But it is not so: for God in his mercy wishes those who err to be taught, not killed. You will say: But he perseveres in his error: what then? The Apostles too could not heal a certain demoniac, so obstinate was that Demon: ought they on that account to have killed [him], because they could not heal [him]? Or [ought they] not rather to accuse their own unbelief, which was at fault, as the Lord reproaches them—because that kind of spirits could not be cast out except by fastings and prayers? What if we do not have enough faith, as the Apostles [did], or do not fast enough from sins, or [do not] give effort to prayers? Ought we to kill those whom, on account of our unbelief and fault, we cannot heal? O woe to us if we do [so]. Is our faith and integrity and continence so great, that we dare to set ourselves before the Apostles? Why do we not accuse our own unbelief, which is the cause that we cannot cast the spirit of error out of him? And what I say of the Papists, the same I wish to be understood also of the others. For if the Papists, who err so gravely and in so many [matters], are nevertheless not to be killed on account of those errors, how much less the others, who have departed in great part from their errors? And so Luther ought not to kill Zwingli, nor Calvin (if however Calvin agrees with Zwingli), although he holds him in the [matter of the] Supper for a heretic, or rather for a Devil and a reprobate. Nor [ought] Zwingli [to kill] Luther, although he judges him also evil-speaking and blasphemous; nor [ought] Calvin [to kill] Melanchthon, although [he] dissents from him in [the matter of] Predestination; nor [ought] all these [to kill] Osiander, although [he] differs from them in Justification. In short, those who confess one God and one Lord ought not the one to condemn the other, much less to kill [him]. Otherwise, whoever shall do this, he will by no art escape but that he is said to be that bad servant who, while the Lord delays to return, beats his fellow-servants.

And, that I may at length complete this Disputation concerning persecution, I will expound this very thing which I just touched upon—the oracle concerning this persecution of the last times—from which the whole matter (if anyone will wish to open his eyes) can be perceived. Thus therefore the Lord speaks: "If there is any faithful and prudent servant, whom the master has set over his household, to furnish it nourishment in its time—blessed is that servant," said Christ, "whom, when his master comes, he shall find performing [his] office; assuredly, I say to you, he will set him over all his goods. But if that bad servant, thinking in his mind that his master delays his coming for a long time, shall begin to strike his fellow-servants, and to feast and to drink with the drunken, the Lord of that servant will come on a day he does not expect, and at an hour he does not know, and will cut him asunder, and will reduce him to the condition of Hypocrites (that is, of dissemblers), where there will be weeping and gnashing of teeth." He speaks of the good shepherd, who feeds the sheep with Doctrine, [and] does not kill [them]. He speaks of the bad shepherd, who kills the sheep, [and] does not feed [them]. And in that he speaks of the delaying of the master's coming, he shows that he speaks of the last time, when Christ will already seem to delay his coming for a long while. For those ancients—Augustine, Jerome, Hilary, and the rest—did not persecute their fellow-servants thus: for they feared the coming of the Lord. But these last, lost servants conduct themselves as though he were not going to come. Likewise, in that he says "he began to strike his fellow-servants," he indicates that he does not speak of the persecution of those who are not our fellow-servants—that is, who do not acknowledge Christ the Lord, of which kind are the Turks and Jews—for they do not persecute us on account of Christ, nor we in turn [persecute] them. But he speaks of those who persecute those who acknowledge the same Lord Christ, of which kind are all the sects which I commemorated above, excepting those impious [men] who do not acknowledge Christ, and therefore do not suffer persecution. And in that he says "He began," it signifies that this persecution will be [such], not such as was that of the heathen, who not only began the persecution but completed [it], nor were impeded in it: This persecution will not endure to the end of the lust of the bad fellow-servant, for it will be crushed by the sudden coming of the Lord. And in that he says "to feast and to drink with the drunken," he speaks of the powerful and the abounding, who, drunk with prosperity, run riot and grow wanton against [their] fellow-servants—which previously they did not do, when they did not yet abound. For Zwingli and Calvin and the rest of the persecutors, before they had obtained strength, conducted themselves humbly and modestly, and condemned the persecutors. Now, fattened and having attained strength and elated with wealth, they grow wanton, and strike their weak fellow-servants—just as God foretold of old in these words: "I myself will judge between the fat cattle and the lean, since you thrust with [your] sides and shoulders, and butt with your horns all the weakest ones, until you scatter them abroad. Therefore I will preserve my sheep, that they be no longer a prey, and I will judge between cattle and cattle, and I will raise up for them one shepherd who will feed them, my David, who will feed them, and will be a shepherd to them."

Likewise, in that Christ himself says: "The Lord of that servant will come on a day he does not expect, and at an hour he does not know"—it signifies the security that will then be, such as was in the time of Noah, which security is brought about both by men's hardened wickedness, and by the bland doctrine of the hypocrites congruent with this. And in that he says: "He will reduce him to the condition of hypocrites"—he shows that the authors of this persecution will be hypocrites, that is, feigned men, who wish to seem better than they are, and therefore protect their reputation by every means, and most of all attack those by whom they are uncovered. For nothing is more bloody than hypocrites—to use the words of Calvin himself. And so all men whosoever, of Christian name, who inflict persecution on others (who acknowledge the same Lord Christ) on account of religion—they without doubt are that bad servant who beats his fellow-servants. Weigh the whole matter, Reader; open your eyes, and love the truth; you will find it to be so.

Thus far to those things which Calvin wrote concerning persecution; now let us come to Calvin himself, who, that general persecution being set aside, betakes himself to Servetus, that we may see of what kind this iron soldier of Christ is, and may examine this deed of his, lest the simple be deceived by this double man. First he repels from himself the charge with which he is accused—as if it had been done by his agency that Servetus was captured at Vienne, in the province of Lyon—and denies that it is credible that he had such familiarity or favor with the Papists. As if indeed we did not know that a Genevan messenger was afterward sent to Vienne to seek the sentence pronounced there against Servetus; or as if great favor were needed to bring it about that Servetus be captured by the Papists—[he] who had written so sharply against the Pope, and in the very book which was then being printed at Vienne demonstrates by many reasons that he is Antichrist; or as if friendship could not easily be joined between Pilate and Herod against someone who is an adversary to both. But indeed Calvin so denies this charge that he very nearly confesses [it]. For he writes thus:

**Calvin 130.**

Nor indeed, if they were truly to object this to me, would I think there was cause for denying [it]—I who do not dissemble that it was done by my agency that, apprehended in this city, he was summoned to plead his case.

**Vaticanus.**

As if he should say this: If I had seen to it that he was captured at Vienne, I would have done rightly, since I did it at Geneva, and did it rightly. Therefore if he could bring it about that he be captured at Geneva, he ought to have done it, since he confesses it to be right: and he could [do it] most easily—for nothing is easier than to bring about, even by the slightest information, that someone be captured in France who has said anything—much less [written] such atrocious things, much less written [them]—against the Pope. And so I no longer doubt that Calvin did it. And moreover, as for what he mentions, that he "either wrote, or saw to it that it was written": if Servetus will deny [it], we have certain things written in his hand, which will be shown, if you wish. These were written to Lyon from Geneva to the Papists. Now indeed let it be weighed of what kind this confession of Calvin is. "If I had brought it about," he says, "that Servetus were captured in France, I would have done rightly, since I did [so] at Geneva." But if Calvin had done rightly, the Papists too would have done rightly, who had captured and killed Servetus by Calvin's judgment. Therefore to the Papists also, just as to the Genevans, it is permitted to kill heretics. But above Calvin said that this is permitted only to a pious magistrate: Therefore the Papistic magistrate is pious, and can judge concerning the causes of religion no less than the Genevan [one]. But if he is pious, and this is permitted to him, he can by his own judgment—this pious [judgment]—kill those whom he judges heretics; therefore he can kill both Calvin himself and all the Calvinians, since he holds them for heretics. But if Calvin will say that this was permitted to them in [the case of] Servetus, but is not permitted in [the case of] others: who will admit this exception? For thus will those men say: We kill Servetus, not as Servetus, but as a heretic, and hence we gather from your Doctrine, Calvin, that it is permitted to us to kill those whom we judge similar to Servetus—that is, heretics: For either this ought not to be permitted to us, or that ought to be permitted to our judgment. For since we are Magistrates, we use our own judgment, not another's. But if you snatch from us the judgment concerning religion, snatch also the power of judging concerning Servetus. But if you permit [it]: permit [it] also concerning yourself, whom we hold in the same place as Servetus. For just as, if induced by your counsel we should kill murderers, we would kill you yourself also, if we judged [you] a murderer. So that we judge it permitted to kill only adversaries, not also our own [people].

**Calvin 131.**

Let either malevolent or malicious men make an outcry [as they please], I freely confess, and openly declare (because according to (1) the laws of the city the matter could not otherwise be conducted with the man by law) (2) that the accuser proceeded from me: Nor do I deny that by my counsel was dictated (3) the formula, by which someone's involvement in the case might be made manifest.

**Vaticanus.**

(1) At Geneva there is a Law, that no one be permitted to be captured on the Lord's Day, except on account of a capital crime. And so when Calvin took up [the role of] accuser on the Lord's Day, [and] accused Servetus of a capital [offense], either [the crime was capital], or he acted against the Law of the city. (2) In the Law of Moses the accusers are the witnesses, who can say "I saw" or "I was present," and they first cast the stone. Whether Calvin's cook could judge concerning Servetus's errors about the Trinity, and Fate, and the most obscure questions of that kind (in which the Church has now sweated for so many ages), and cast the first stone—or, as a false witness, be afflicted with the same punishment—I leave to be judged by those who know the man's ignorance. Likewise, whether it was permitted to a Pastor to bring forth from his kitchen an accuser of a man's life [i.e., on a capital charge], let those judge who know the morals and character of the Apostles. That cook is not an accuser, but a mute person and a mask of an accuser, so that the Magistrate, deceived by this, might allow the true accuser Calvin to conduct the whole case of Servetus—which would not have been permitted to him, if according to the Law of the city he himself had been in bonds. (3) What that formula could have been I do not see, except that from the outcome I suspect it was of this kind: "I say this man is a heretic"—this accusation Calvin then concludes [thus]: "Heretics are to be killed; therefore I say this man is to be killed, on this condition, that unless I prove the defendant [guilty], I be killed in his place by the Law of Retaliation." This is Calvin's accusation. Let him now answer before God, who sees the hearts of all, whether he himself wishes it to be dealt with him in the way he dealt with Servetus.

**Calvin 132.**

But what my counsel then was appeared afterward from the very progress of the action.

**Vaticanus.**

Nay rather, from this—that he was captured on the Lord's Day—and from the very outcome, that is, the punishment.

**Calvin 133.**

For as soon as we were summoned, I and my colleagues, it by no means rested with us that he should not be free to confer peaceably with us concerning his Dogmas.

**Vaticanus.**

Wretched and too servile Liberty of disputing, when, unless you wish to come down into Calvin's opinion, the executioner is going to make the conclusion! This Calvin has from elsewhere than from the Gospel. If he wished to dispute, he ought not to accuse (for to an accusation responds not a disputation, but the pleading of a case), but rather to invite [him] peaceably to a supper. Nor was there reason why he should fear Servetus's flight, if he had permitted him a disputation free from bonds—since [Servetus] perhaps had come to Geneva of his own accord for that very reason. Or if he feared flight, not even thus ought he to have cast fetters [on him]: For one ought not to dispute with the unwilling.

**Calvin 134.**

Nay, we approached just as if an account of our doctrine were to be rendered from bonds: and if he should object anything, we testified that we were ready to respond.

**Vaticanus.**

Very credulous will he be who will believe this. He who pleads his case from bonds dreads punishment. How can those Ministers dread this? When the Magistrate pronounces concerning religion only on the basis of their teaching and opinion?

**Calvin 135.**

When he repeatedly vomited out reproaches against me with full cheeks, of which the informers themselves were ashamed and chagrined, I abstained from his harassment.

**Vaticanus.**

Indeed I do not praise Servetus, if he was of a too intemperate Tongue: although it must be greatly seen with what spirit anything is said. If Servetus had said anything against Calvin, with the spirit with which Paul said to the Jewish High Priest, "God shall strike you, whited wall," I would not think it rashly to be reprehended, since it was true. But if Servetus said anything too intemperate, contending with a most intemperate man, Calvin found a man worthy of himself: although it is unworthy [that] he be reprehended by Calvin—since Calvin himself so abounds in reproaches that there are those who say a dictionary of reproaches could be put together from his writings. And so, in that he says that he then abstained from harassing Servetus, that is very difficult for me to believe—since he was accustomed openly to traduce him in daily sermons, and now in addition, a Book having been published, calling [him] an obscene dog, and a vaulting-horse [i.e., a fickle turncoat], and what not, traduces [him] throughout the whole world. Let us believe that in the very conflict he abstained from harassing him. He, however, either did not abstain, or wished by this hope of modesty to earn afterward license of reviling before the Magistrate, and also before all nations. Otherwise, if it had been true modesty, he would never have reviled him throughout the whole world—neither then, [when he was] absent, from the pulpit, nor now, [when he is] dead.

**Calvin 136.**

Add that no danger of a graver penalty was impending, if in any way he had been curable.

**Vaticanus.**

Those who labor with diseases of the body in prison are sometimes accustomed to be released, that they may be cured. Calvin thinks Servetus incurable, who, if he was sound, could by such great unquietness be made sick; but if [he was] already sick, could be rendered incurable—unless perhaps bonds aggravate diseases of the body more than [those] of the mind.

**Calvin 137.**

But so far was he from seeking moderation, that, full of boastfulness and ferocity, he petulantly spurned all sound and useful counsels.

**Vaticanus.**

Then indeed he would have been sound, if Servetus had believed Calvin's counsels would be sound. If Calvin were thus held in bonds in Spain, he would experience how sound the counsels of enemies are.

**Calvin 138.**

Moreover, what absurd and execrable blasphemies escaped him in the course of speaking, will perhaps be told more opportunely elsewhere: This only I wish to be attested in the present, that I was not so capitally hostile [to him], but that it would have been permitted—by modesty alone (unless I had been deprived of my mind)—to redeem [his] life.

**Vaticanus.**

Therefore he was killed not on account of heresies, but on account of immodesty. Heretics therefore ought not to be slain, unless they are immodest. Why did Calvin not hand down this rule earlier, when he was disputing about heretics being killed? Then, if modesty can preserve life for heretics, why not also for murderers? Since to be a heretic is graver at Geneva than [to be] a murderer, as is perceived from the gravity of the punishment. But indeed not obscurely, if anyone has eyes, does Calvin show that Servetus fell by Calvin's hatred.

**Calvin 139.**

But I know not what to say, except that he was seized by a fatal madness, so that he threw himself headlong.

**Vaticanus.**

Certain Calvinians, when they could not defend Calvin's deed, fled at last to this—to say that it was fated for Servetus to be burned at Geneva. By this reasoning may one excuse whatever [things] are done? Thus it was in the fates. And if we kill someone, thus it was in the fates. And yet Calvin himself in his Institutes, Chapter 14, section 40, does not accept the name of fate—although he so holds the thing itself, that he plainly rejects the name of fortune.

**Calvin 140.**

Someone will perhaps object, why I proceeded beyond Paul's mandate, who writes to Timothy that even desperate heretics are only to be avoided. But the excuse is ready at hand for me—that it was enough for me to avoid a man whose deplorable impiety was known to me, and to warn others that they do the same. But that this prudence of guarding [oneself] did not impede either me or any other whomsoever, from each of the pious Magistrates seeing to it that a Heretic be coerced according to the opportunity offered to him.

**Vaticanus.**

Thus the Masters of the Sorbonne excuse themselves: They accuse and condemn a Heretic, and order the Magistrate to preserve the same [man] if they could—but in the midst of armed satellites, from whose hands [the condemned man] could scarcely even fly away, however much he might wish. Thus did Calvin: he accused a man; he pronounced [him] a heretic and impious, deplored, and more wicked than any Robber, and so defamed [him] throughout the whole world, that men thought him to be some Stygian monster. When he had done this, and had persuaded his Magistrate with the greatest diligence and labor that [the man] could not be dismissed, but that the Magistrate would defect from the fidelity of his office (for so he said above), he avoids the man as a heretic. Concerning [the man's] own death he makes no word to the Magistrate. "Bring water to Calvin, that he may wash his hands: he is innocent of his blood. It is not permitted to us to kill anyone. But if you dismiss this man, you are not Caesar's friend." O Calvin, Calvin; do you think God to be like a man, that he should suffer himself to be deceived by these Sorbonic sophisms? Your own Disciples, who otherwise esteem you so greatly, that they think the church will fall with you, cannot defend you in this part. They only say this—that not without the gravest causes (but ones that lie hidden) you did it, that you killed that man: Which is just as if they should say: If Servetus had not been an evildoer, Calvin would not have handed him over to the Magistrate. O how far this man is from the disposition of Elisha, concerning whom we spoke above! Or of David, who, although he himself was a King anointed Divinely, was so far from killing Saul—a tyrant unjustly holding both his [David's] kingdom, and with armed hand plotting against his life—when he could, that he did not even allow [him] to be killed. Or of Christ, who freed the adulteress from the hand of the Magistrate by such marvelous astuteness.

**Calvin 141.**

The same Apostle, who only forbids Timothy from the encounter with a Heretic, openly asserts that he handed Hymenaeus and Alexander over to Satan—(1) that they might learn not to blaspheme. If a pious Magistrate had then been at hand for Paul, and a strenuous vindicator of the glory of Christ, I indeed do not doubt that he would willingly have given [them] to be punished, whom by the chastisement divinely permitted to him he was reducing to order.

**Vaticanus.**

(1) That is, that, moved by shame and infamy, they might return to soundness: Just as was done in [the case of] that Corinthian whom Paul excommunicated to the punishment of the body, that he might correct himself and again be received into the communion of Christians—which was done. But Paul, so far is [he] from being [one who] would willingly have given that man over to the Magistrate to be punished, that he afterward writes thus concerning him to the Corinthians: "Sufficient for that man is that chastisement of many, so that, on the contrary, you should rather pardon him, and console [him], lest perhaps he be consumed by excessive sorrow. Wherefore I exhort you, that you exercise charity toward him." Behold, he fears lest [the man] be consumed by sorrow on account of the infamy of excommunication—how much more would he have feared [it] from an executioner!—not to say, in the meantime, how unjust it would have been [for] the same [man] to be punished twice, namely by Paul and by the Magistrate. Why did Calvin not fear the same in [the case of] Servetus, who as a suppliant petitioned the Magistrate in vain, that it be permitted [him] to perish by the sword, lest by the excessive torment of fire he be driven to desperation? Where then was the charity of the Calvinians? Why did Calvin not hand him over to Satan, that he might learn not to blaspheme? He handed [him] over to the executioner: to him Satan was the executioner: How will Servetus now learn not to blaspheme, but to praise God? Since it is written: "The dead will not praise you, O Lord." Or how would Hymenaeus and Alexander then have learned not to blaspheme, if, Paul accusing [them], they had come into the hands of the executioner? Is the Executioner accustomed to unteach blasphemies? But I so differ from Calvin, that I do not doubt that Paul would have entreated the Magistrate to grant them pardon, so that they could learn not to blaspheme. For this is consonant with Paul's clemency, and he did some such thing in [the case of] Philemon, whom he so dutifully entreats on behalf of Onesimus, a servant previously worthless: "If he has injured you in anything," he says, "or owes you [anything], impute it to me. I Paul have written [it] with my own hand: I will discharge [it]." These things are as far from the mind of Calvin as heaven from earth. Calvin thinks Paul was a Calvinian: and because he cannot say that he did any such thing (for his whole Doctrine, and his whole life in Christ, is repugnant [to it]), he says he would have done [it]. And so if Paul had been such as he had been before he was made Paul, Saul certainly would have done [it]; and Calvin now makes him such for us, and prefers Saul to Paul. But indeed if Paul would have done this in [the case of] heretics, why did he not do [it] in [the case of] evildoers? He had at hand the Magistrate for punishing murderers, adulterers, false witnesses, etc.: in the punishing of whom he himself testified in his Epistles that the Magistrate is the minister of God: Why did he never accuse any criminal, when he could? Or why did he select only heretics for accusing—when Calvin himself in the first [book of the] Institutes, page 389, reprehended this kind of inept selection of one matter, others being passed over, in the Papists, in these words: "It is commanded, namely, in the Law concerning the slaying of the calf, concerning the burning of its fat, concerning the rams being cut up and burned, and innumerable other observances, which being passed over, I wonder why the one anointing with oil pleases them." And I more rightly wonder why, so many criminals being passed over whom the Law orders to be killed, the accusation of heretics alone pleases Calvin—concerning whom not a syllable can be found in the whole Law.

**Calvin 142.**

For neither does that which is prescribed to me and to all ministers of the word constrain and bind the judges, that they should not exercise the office divinely enjoined on them: Nor indeed does it stand in my way (1) that I should commend the truth of God to their protection and faith, where there is need [of it] thus.

**Vaticanus.**

(1) That is, [it does not stand in my way] but that I should accuse Heretics and say: "The faith is in your protection. If you dismiss this man, you will desert the faith and your office." This is the same as what he just said Paul would have done. Which—what else is it but to say, "Crucify, you Pilate: It is not permitted to us to kill anyone": But it is permitted to accuse, and to threaten the Magistrate, unless he has complied. And here indeed Calvin contradicts himself: For above he wrote that he made no word concerning the punishment of Servetus, from [the time] he had been thrown [into prison]. And yet here he writes that this is [part] of the Minister's office, and that Paul would have done [it]. But if Paul ought to have done [it], Calvin too ought [to have]: and if Calvin did [it], he lied when he denied [it]: If he did not do [it], he was delinquent in his office. The rest of the things which Calvin wrote concerning Servetus, and those three questions, I omit—not because there are not many things there to be reprehended, but because to refute all of Calvin's errors would be endless: Then because Servetus is dead, whence it comes about that if Calvin has perhaps lied about anything against him, the credibility of it rests with Calvin: how much ought and should to be believed [of] him, those things show which we set down above. It is easy to speak evil of the dead: But Servetus will rise again someday, just as we [shall]: nor is his soul so extinguished by Calvin that it cannot refute Calvin (if indeed he gave false testimony about him) before God, who will judge far otherwise than Calvin. Let us come to the death of Servetus, which Calvin describes in such a way that he simultaneously paints his own clemency: For thus he speaks.

**Calvin 143.**

But lest the idle good-for-nothings glory, as it were, in the obstinacy of a senseless man, as in a martyrdom—in his death there appeared a brutish stupidity, whence it would be permitted to make the judgment that he had never done anything seriously in Religion.

**Vaticanus.**

Here Calvin's modesty exerts itself.

**Calvin 144.**

From [the time] death was announced to him, now [he was] like one thunderstruck, [seeming] to stick fast, now to utter deep sighs, now to wail in the manner of a frenzied man.

**Vaticanus.**

And although I believe all these things are said by Calvin enviously and in bad faith, and narrated more accusatorially than truly, yet even if they were true, I see here no brutish stupidity. For that we understand [to be] in soldiers and in inhuman men, who are no more moved by the fear of death than beasts. But that a man be moved is human, not brutish. Hezekiah too uttered deep sighs, when death—far milder than [that] of Servetus—had been announced to him: And after the manner of a dove or a crane he sent forth querulous and miserable cries. Job himself, who is accustomed to be set forth as a specimen of patience, stuck fast like one thunderstruck for seven whole days—although his friends brought him not so sad a message as the enemies [brought] to Servetus. Christ himself, finally, sweated blood from anguish, and had a soul sorrowful unto death, and on the very gibbet, as if deserted, miserably cried out: "My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?" Calvin could vituperate all these same things by the same art, and twist [them] obliquely and sinisterly, as the suspicious are accustomed [to do]—which he wrote ought so not to be done in the first [book of the] Institutes, as we showed above. It is altogether the nature of sophists, that they know how to pervert everything, and the same things now to vituperate, now to praise. If Servetus is moved by death, they say it is [a mark] of brutish stupor: If an Anabaptist dies steadfastly (as they say the constancy of those men in enduring punishments is incredible), they say it is obstinacy, and the virtue of the Devil: in which indeed they are the disciples of the Jews, whom neither John's austerity nor Christ's affability satisfied. Again, if any of their own disciples dies steadfastly, they extol [him] with praises to heaven; but if he is moved [i.e., disturbed], they do not lack excuses—which indeed I do not reprehend, for it is [a mark] of charity to interpret these things in the good part.

But that they do not do the same also in [the case of] others, that I cannot approve. The mind altogether is to be regarded, not the appearance or gestures of the body. For it can happen that both some pious [man] wails in death, and an impious [man] sings: And on the contrary, that both a pious [man] sings and an impious [man] wails: And so these are not certain indications of the mind. That Servetus indeed was disturbed I do not at all wonder: Calvin himself, who now so petulantly insults the dead [man], if he were in that state, I know not what he would do—since he is often accustomed, if anyone has spoken too freely against him, to fall at once into his disease, which they call the migraine; so great is the impatience of his anger. It generally happens that the more each one rages, while he is far from punishment, the same is the more broken when it is at hand. Believe me, Reader, it is one thing to speak about fire from the shade, another to see oneself sprinkled with sulphur by the executioner, and to be confronted [by fire] brought round one's face. O Calvin, Calvin; see that it not be dealt with you by retaliation in this [life] (which would be better), or in the life to come.

**Calvin 145.**

Which at last finally so prevailed, that he only bellowed in the Spanish manner: "Misericordia, misericordia" [EN: Mercy, mercy].

**Vaticanus.**

Surely he had just cause why he should implore the Mercy of God, since he was in the hands of men so unmerciful, that he could not obtain from them by prayers a beheading—which he could have obtained in the midst of the barbarity of the Cannibals.

**Calvin** [146.]

When it was come to the place of punishment, by the exhortation of the excellent brother and our fellow-celebrant Farel, the utterance was at last with difficulty extorted from him, that the people should conceive common prayers with him. Moreover, with what conscience he permitted himself to do this I do not see. For he had written with his own hand that here a Diabolical faith reigns: that we have no Church, no god, because by baptizing infants we deny Christ. How therefore did he join himself as a partner to the people in prayers, whose communion was to be fled? Is it not a profanation of sacred unity, to profess a common God—and the same [God] at that—with an impious and profane assembly?

**Vaticanus.**

Luther too called the people of Zurich robbers of souls: But he testified that he said this of the Masters. For the common people indeed the Lord prayed. Servetus too could have written those things about the Masters, and yet not think thus about the common people: Or at least not about all [of them]. He who thinks the people are deceived does not straightway abominate the whole people. Christ himself and the Apostles did not hesitate to pray in the temple, and to be present at the sacred [rites]: and yet Christ testified that the Jews had made of that temple a den of robbers. How therefore does he join himself as a partner in prayers to Robbers? Calvin himself has a Church in which there are very many hypocrites and impious [men], as we showed above from his Writings: With what conscience therefore does he permit himself to pray to a common God with hypocrites and the Impious?

**Calvin 147.**

And indeed that supplication should be made for him [thus], Farel exhorted: but expressly that the Lord, having pitied a man otherwise lost, would lead him back from his execrable errors to a sound mind.

**Vaticanus.**

Therefore Servetus was accused on account of errors: and yet the Law kills no one on account of errors, even if someone had killed his own father by error. The Jews too erred—and more gravely than Servetus—who crucified Christ: But if on account of homicide they were worthy of death, on account of error certainly they were not. Thomas too erred most gravely, who could be induced neither by the sacred scriptures, nor by Christ's preaching, nor by the common testimony of so many Apostles and Disciples, to believe that Christ had come to life again: And so great an error could be called blasphemy by Calvin: Was he on that account to be killed? [?] Servetus believed that Christ had come to life again, and yet did not see why, if he erred in other matters, he was not preserved? That there might be room for repentance? If Thomas had then been killed, would not the way to his salvation have been cut off? Why do we prescribe to the Lord the time of [his] aid? So that, if [the Lord] has not recalled this man from [his] errors before the flames are kindled, he must today be burned? What if [it be] tomorrow, when the Lord is about to come? O if God should kill all who err, who would escape? But they will say those errors are blasphemy. And so Musculus, in the Epitaph which he is said to have written about Servetus, speaks thus:

Servetus ex Hispania,
Qui tam diu non debuit
Inter fideles vivere,
Hic triplicem nos bestiam,
Et Sathanae fantasmata,
Illusiones Daemonum,
Habere dicit pro Deo,
Et propter hanc blasphemiam,
Linguamque detestabilem,
Non propter errores graves,
Quibus scatebat plurimis,
Flammis Genevae extinctus est.

[EN (Latin verse): Servetus from Spain, who for so long ought not to have lived among the faithful—this man says that we hold for God a threefold beast, and the phantasms of Satan, [and] the illusions of Demons; and on account of this blasphemy, and [his] detestable tongue—not on account of the grave errors with which he teemed in great number—he was extinguished by the flames of Geneva.]

You see what he calls blasphemy: Not that Servetus said God is a threefold beast, or a Demon (as has been most falsely persuaded to the common people), but that he said that they are ignorant [of] what God is, and worship for the true God a not-true [one]. This, however, ought to be called not blasphemy, but error. But just as flatterers in the beginning extenuate the vices of those whom they flatter, calling him who is irascible "severe," and the avaricious "frugal," and the prodigal "generous": So Calumniators exaggerate these same things, calling error "blasphemy," and interpreting the words of others sinisterly and in the bad part. If Servetus had said God is a Demon, it would be true blasphemy, and I would rejoice that he was to be punished. But if he said that they do not know what God is, and therefore worship a false God, he did not speak against God, but against men. Otherwise, by this reasoning the Jews could accuse Christ, since he had said they had made of the house of God a den of Robbers. Likewise, since he had said they were of [their] father the devil, and yet had confessed [them] to be sons of Abraham, they could have said that by him Abraham was called the Devil: Which same I say of God, since they themselves asserted [themselves to be] sons of God. By calumnies of this kind I see that Servetus was oppressed. The people have been persuaded that he called the Trinity "Cerberus," which is false: For he believes in the Father, and in the Son, and in the Holy Spirit, which is the Trinity: But he interpreted these things otherwise than they [do]: This interpretation they enviously stirred up against him among the common people, so that men think Servetus to be someone —
so that men think Servetus [to be] some [monster].

…in a free City, unless there were some accuser who would offer himself along with the defendant to bonds, etc. Calvin sent his servant down to give himself as accuser. This servant had at one time been the cook of a certain nobleman by the name of Falais, whom [Falais] Calvin had at one time esteemed so greatly on account of religion that in a certain Epistle he praised him highly. But afterward, when Falais seemed to favor a certain physician by the name of Jerome (who was held in bonds on account of the cause of predestination, since he had dissented on the matter), in a public congregation he was judged a heretic by Calvin. By the same Calvin a servant is sent there, who gave himself as accuser: and Servetus, called from the assembly, and having confessed his name, was thrown into prison—as was also Calvin's servant himself, who a little after, sureties being given, was freed. Servetus in bonds was kept in such a way that no one could meet with him (unless endowed with great authority) except one who was Calvin's friend. Servetus having been captured, a messenger was sent by the Magistrate to Vienne, to report the sentence pronounced by the Viennese themselves against Servetus—which sentence the Viennese gave to that messenger, and added this: that Servetus had come into the hands of the Viennese by the judgment of the chief preacher of the Genevans. This Sentence having been reported, a messenger was sent to the Helvetic Churches—Bern, Zurich, Schaffhausen, and Basel—together with Servetus's book, and the accusation of the preachers, and the letters of the Genevan Magistrate to the ministers of those churches, or to the Magistrates, to collect their Sentences upon Servetus.

Meanwhile a certain Thomas, servant of Robert Stephanus, was sent to Frankfurt, who burned Servetus's books, which had been brought forth there to the fair, lest they be distributed. That messenger brought back from the preachers of those Churches letters in which Servetus was condemned as a Heretic. And so at once the Genevan Magistrate was convened over the business of Servetus. Amedeus Perrin, leader of the militia, and the same [being] then the first Consul of the city, when he saw the minds of the Senators inclined to the death of the man, refused to be present at the judgment, and denied that he would be a participant in his blood. The same did certain others; the rest condemned [him] variously, some to exile, others to perpetual prisons, the greater part to the fires unless he were willing to recant.

They affirm also that the chief Professor of Theology of that city [i.e., of the Cellarius / the schools] never consented to the death either of Servetus or of any Heretic: And the same they think concerning certain inferior Ministers of that city, who for that reason were not called to pronounce Sentence concerning Servetus. Thus he was led to the tribunal, and there condemned to be burned and reduced to ashes. When he heard this Sentence, he petitions the Magistrate as a suppliant that it be permitted [him] to perish by the sword, lest they drive him to desperation by the magnitude of the torment, and so he lose his soul: [he said] that if he had sinned in anything, he had sinned by ignorance, [and] that in mind indeed and will he had been so disposed that he wished to promote the glory of God—which prayers of his Farel expounded more openly to the Magistrate. But the Magistrate was not prevailed upon by Servetus, [and] thus Servetus was led [away], repeatedly crying: "O God, save my soul: O Jesus, son of the eternal God, have mercy on me." When it was come to the place of punishment, he fell down as a suppliant, and lay prone for some while, while Farel addressed the people thus: "You see how great powers Satan has, when he possesses someone; this man is learned in the first rank, and perhaps thought himself to be doing rightly, but now he is possessed by the Devil, which same could happen to you." Meanwhile, when Servetus had risen, Farel exhorted him to say something. He, groaning and sighing, cried out: "O God, O God"; when Farel [asked] whether he had nothing else to say, he answered, "What else can I speak but of God?" Farel admonishing [him] that, if he had a wife or children and wished to make a testament, a public notary was present, He answered nothing. Thus he was led to the pile of wood; there were moreover bundles of green oak, still leafy, with split logs mixed in. Servetus was placed on a trunk set upon the ground, his feet reaching to the ground. Upon his head was placed [a wreath] either of straw or of leaves, and that sprinkled with sulphur: His body bound to the stake with an iron chain, his neck moreover with a thick rope, fourfold or fivefold [and] loose, [with] a book bound to his thigh. He himself begged the executioner not to torture him long. Meanwhile the executioner brought the fire into his sight, and then around [him]; the man, the fire being seen, cried out so horribly that he terrified the whole people. When he languished for a long time, there were [some] from the people who threw the bundles together in a heap, [Servetus] himself crying out with a horrible voice: "Jesus, son of the eternal God, have mercy on me." After a torment of about half an hour he expired. There are those who affirm that Calvin, when he had seen Servetus led to punishment, smiled, his face lightly cast down beneath the fold of his garment. This affair disturbed many pious [men], and gave birth to a scandal of scandals, which scarcely ever seemed likely to be obliterated; for the pious reprehend many crimes in that deed.

First, that a man was killed at Geneva on account of religion: for they deny that anyone ought to be killed on account of religion; and when the Old Testament is cited concerning false Prophets being killed, they cite the New [Testament] concerning the tares not being rooted out before the harvest. But if [Calvin] says that the Helvetic Churches consented to the death of Servetus: They respond that those [churches] could not be judges, since they were defendants. For Servetus was reprehending those, together with the Genevan [church]. Next, they wonder that Calvin conspired in the death of another with those Churches whose doctrine he condemned elsewhere. For in the French booklet concerning the supper he openly condemns Zwingli, Oecolampadius, together with Luther, and says they erred. But if they erred in [the matter of] the Supper, they can err also in [the matter of] persecution. The second [crime] is that he was killed by Calvin's agency; for here, that he might oppress his enemy, [Calvin] suborned an accuser from his kitchen, a man most ignorant of Servetus and of the Servetan questions—this [they say] is as far from the nature of Christ as earth is far from heaven, for Christ came not to destroy, but to save. The third is that he was killed so cruelly, although he had petitioned as a suppliant for the sword. This unheard-of cruelty could engender the suspicion, as it were, that the Genevans wish to return into favor with the Pope, and to show by the deed that they do not abhor him, although in words they rail against him. The fourth, that the Evangelicals conspired with the Papists to kill him—whence there were [some] who think that friendship is thus joined among them, in the way that it was joined between Pilate and Herod, in the punishment of Christ. The fifth is that Servetus's Books were burned—which (like the rest) they seem to have learned from the Pope. And indeed if Calvin's doctrine concerning Predestination and Election is true, there was no reason why they should fear lest Servetus seduce anyone, since the elect cannot be seduced. But if sins are made necessary, and by God compelling, Servetus could not but do what he did, nor could the Calvinians not be deceived, if it [be] future that they be deceived, nor be deceived, if it is future that they be not deceived. The sixth crime is that Servetus, [though] dead, was in addition publicly condemned in the sermons to eternal punishment, and so condemned, that those who heard Farel thunder out the sentence say that they shuddered in their whole body and soul. The seventh crime is that Calvin appears to write against him [now that he is] already extinguished—which seems similar to that deed of the Jews, who, Christ being dead, petitioned from Pilate (calling Christ an impostor) that his body be guarded: Thus they say Calvin dreads lest the body of Servetus be secretly taken away (that this might not be able to happen, provision was made by Calvin's diligence), but lest the ashes speak: Otherwise, if he wished to write against him, he ought to have written [while he was] alive, so that there might be for him the power of responding—which is permitted even to a robber.

Servetus was burned in the year 1553, on the 27th day of October.

When in the beginning William Farel and Peter Viret were teaching the Gospel at Geneva, John Calvin came over from France, and, taken on by Farel, he too began to teach. Meanwhile a dissension arose between the preachers and the citizens, because the citizens wished the supper to be administered to them with unleavened bread, so that in this they might agree with the Bernese; the preachers, however, with leavened [bread]. There were moreover two factions in the City, of which the one, with John Philippe as leader, opposed the preachers, the other favored [them], with a certain Balthasar as leader. Since the former had more strength, a decree of the Senate is made, that the Preachers be expelled from the city unless they should comply. Thus expelled, the one to one place, the other to another, Calvin came to Strasbourg, and there began to profess Theology. Meanwhile John Philippe is struck with the axe on account of homicide; thus, the other faction having more strength, messengers are sent to Strasbourg to recall Calvin: prevailed upon with difficulty, he at last returned to Geneva, and first of all saw to it that he should renew matters. First he wrote a formula of Baptism and of Prayers, that which was then held there being repudiated: Then he gave a new Catechism. Afterward he wrote the institutes of the Genevan Church, and saw to it that they were enacted by the Senate, and so he erected the consistory thus. The preachers whom he found there, and two Schoolmasters, he expelled one by one, some for some causes, others for others; excepting his friend Viret. He instituted the singing of Psalms in the temples, and assigned certain Psalms to be sung on certain days. He made Wednesday a half-festival, so that there it is not permitted to open the taverns before the morning sermon, under penalty of distraint. He made Christmas Day a non-festival [i.e., a working day], wrote some little books, by which, dispatched into France and the other lands, he enticed many mortals to Geneva, teaching that they could not live under the Pope with a safe conscience. He always cultivated the Magistrates, and most of all sought the friendships of all the most powerful [men]; relying on these he subdued his adversaries. Now by summoning [them] to court, now by inveighing [against them] with impunity from the pulpit, he mulcted some with exile, others with bonds, [and] compelled many to seek pardon from him as suppliants. At length he proceeded so far, that, when some had murmured against him, [and] some [had murmured] through the Gervaisians (Gervais, moreover, is another part of the city), on the following day a gibbet was erected in the middle of the forum, as a penalty if anyone should dare to mutter. Whoever displeases Calvin, let him know that he must perish, unless strength be lacking to Calvin. But this is the worst, that Calvin easily believes lies, and gives no room for dispelling [them]? Whoever offends him is called impious, an atheist, an Epicurean. Whoever dissents from him in any matter, he is a Heretic, and is so blackened, that if any Christian converses with him he is not [held to be] a Christian. Those who flock thither—the French in the first rank—are taught to abhor Heretics, that is, the enemies of Calvin: of which kind are Falais, Jerome the Physician, Andreas Zebedeus, Marandus, Marcolfus, Castalio, Caelius, and the rest: To converse with these men is a mortal sin. And all these things are done under the title of Christ.

But the rest [aside], he is a laborious man, of sharp intellect (unless he were often blinded by hatred or self-love), of tenacious memory, strenuous, restless, who alone discharges the offices of Preacher, Senator, and lecturer, and Writer: And he is involved in nearly all the business and contracts of all—especially of the rich and powerful, and of the learned—and does everything. He is cultivated most of all by the nobles and the rich, and he in turn cultivates them, and so confirms his power, to such a degree that he is formidable even to the Senators themselves: with the poor he has not much business, since most are unknown to him. I wished to depict the man thus, so that—since he himself claims for himself the highest knowledge of the sacred letters, and by this matter deceives many—men might consider whether it is credible that God poured his secrets into a soul of this kind, when he is accustomed rather to reveal [them] to the humble and the small, and to conceal [them] from the Wise of the world.

Many today seem not to know that Christ was crucified in Hebrew, Greek, and Latin: do you not see that today too those alone, those alone I say, who are cultivators of those three Languages, are the authors of persecutions?

*Admonition to the Reader*

Since in sections 22 and 31 of this little book it has been said and demonstrated that the custom of coercing Heretics by the sword, and of withdrawing the doctrines of men from anyone's examination, is in many places condemned and rejected by Calvin himself, I have judged it not beside the point if, besides those things which in those places were adduced by Castellio, I should subjoin in this place some other testimonies from the Writings of the same Calvin: so that the fair Reader may more easily perceive that not once and again, but often and constantly, driven by the force of truth, he disapproved those things which afterward, for the sake of his own advantage and honor, he strove to approve and defend.

**Translator's Notes**

The translation above silently corrects the pervasive OCR character errors per the standing conventions. Notes below flag only matters bearing on meaning, identifiable sources, scripture divergence, inferred attributions, and readings a scholar may wish to verify.

*Speaker attributions.* At Calvin 128 the OCR prints "Vaticanus" twice in succession (once as a catchword at the foot of the page, once as the genuine speaker label at the head of the next). The duplicate is a catchword and has been discarded; the genuine label is retained. I have additionally inserted a bracketed [Vaticanus] at the close of Calvin 128 only to mark where the catchword fell; the operative label is the observed "Vaticanus." At Calvin 146 the speaker label "Calvinus" is present but unnumbered in the OCR; I have supplied the section number [146.] in brackets by inference from the surrounding sequence (145, then 147), since the numbering is otherwise continuous.

*1 Timothy 2:1–4 (Calvin 128, both speakers).* Both Calvin and Castellio quote and gloss this passage. The KJV reads "quiet and peaceable life in all godliness and honesty." Castellio's Latin gives *cum omni pietate & gravitate* ("with all piety and gravity") in his fuller citation, against Calvin's *cum omni pietate & temperantia* ("piety and temperance"). The two men are working from differing Latin renderings of the same Greek (σεμνότης), and the disagreement is part of the argument; I have rendered each man's wording as he gives it rather than standardizing to the KJV or to one another.

*Matthew 10:18 (Calvin 128, Vaticanus).* "You shall be led to Kings and Princes for my name's sake" paraphrases the verse; Castellio's wording is close to the Vulgate and needs no special note beyond the observation that he adapts it to his syntax.

*2 Peter 2:1 (Vaticanus, "What a Heretic Is").* Castellio's Latin (*perniciosas sectas introducent* … *vindicemque suum herum negantes*) renders the Greek with "pernicious sects" where the KJV has "damnable heresies" — significant in context, since the whole section turns on the neutral sense of *secta/haeresis*. The divergence is deliberate and I have rendered Castellio's wording ("pernicious sects") rather than the KJV phrasing, as the argument depends on it.

*Galatians 5:19–23 (Vaticanus).* The catalogue of "works of the flesh" and "fruit of the spirit" follows Castellio's own Latin, which differs in selection and order from both Vulgate and KJV (e.g., he includes *De astrorum cultus*, "worship of the stars," an unusual rendering; the OCR "De astrorum" appears to carry a stray *De*, silently dropped). The list is given as Castellio frames it.

*Matthew 7:22–23 / Luke 13:26–27 (Vaticanus).* "Depart from me, all you workers of iniquity" conflates the two Gospel forms, as Castellio's Latin does; rendered as he gives it.

*2 Chronicles 19:2–3 (Jehu to Jehoshaphat).* Castellio's wording diverges notably from the KJV ("wrath upon thee from before the Lord. Nevertheless there are good things found in thee, in that thou hast taken away the groves"). His Latin frames it as a near-acquittal conditioned on the destruction of the *Luci* (sacred groves) and zeal for God; I have rendered his actual phrasing, which sharpens the point that an otherwise good life mitigates a single offense.

*Ezekiel 34:20–23 (Vaticanus, the "fat cattle" oracle).* Rendered from Castellio's Latin, which is close in sense to the Vulgate/KJV but compressed. "My David" (*meum Davidem*) follows his wording.

*Matthew 24:45–51 (the "faithful and prudent servant" oracle).* This is the structural centerpiece of Castellio's closing argument and he quotes it at length. His Latin is his own rendering; I have followed it rather than the KJV throughout, since his exposition depends on specific word choices — notably *ad Hypocritarum … conditionem rediget* ("will reduce him to the condition of Hypocrites"), where the KJV has "appoint him his portion with the hypocrites." Castellio's "reduce to the condition of" is exploited in his gloss at length and must not be flattened to the KJV.

*Acts 23:3 (Paul to the High Priest).* "God shall strike you, whited wall" — Castellio's *paries dealbate* matches the Vulgate; rendered accordingly.

*2 Corinthians 2:6–8 (Vaticanus on the Corinthian offender).* Castellio's Latin is a free but faithful rendering; given in his wording.

*Philemon 18–19.* "Impute it to me … I will discharge it" follows Castellio's Latin closely; no meaningful divergence.

*Institutes citations.* Castellio cites Calvin's *Institutes* by chapter and section three times: IV.1 / "Cap. 8. sect. 26" on heretics and schismatics (his numbering follows an early edition; the Augustine distinction is quoted), "Cap. 14. sect. 40" on the rejection of the name "fate," and "primis Instit. pag. 389" on the Papists' selective observance of the Law (the calf, the fat, the rams). These page/section references are Castellio's own and reflect the edition he used; a scholar collating against a modern edition of the *Institutes* will need to convert them. I have reproduced them as given.

*The Musculus epitaph (Calvin 147, Vaticanus).* This is a twelve-line Latin verse epigram embedded in the text. Per the non-Latin/embedded-verse convention I have preserved it in the original Latin followed by a bracketed English rendering. Two OCR points: line 8 prints *hlasphemiam* (for *blasphemiam*) and line 4/heading area shows minor noise; both are silently corrected in the preserved Latin, the reading being certain from the verse's sense and meter. The attribution "Musculus" (Wolfgang Musculus) is Castellio's; he hedges it himself ("is said to have written"), and I have preserved that hedge rather than asserting authorship.

*"Misericordia, misericordia" (Calvin 145).* Spanish in the original (Servetus's native tongue), flagged by Calvin himself as *Hispanico more* ("in the Spanish manner"). Preserved in the original with [EN: Mercy, mercy].

*The Servetus narrative and biographical appendix.* Beginning partway through the Vaticanus response at Calvin 147, the OCR shifts from the dialogue into a continuous third-person narrative (the capture, trial, the consultation of the Swiss churches, the execution, and a biographical sketch of Calvin's Geneva), followed by the editor's "Admonition to the Reader." This is editorial/narrative matter appended in the 1612 edition, not part of the Calvin–Vaticanus dialogue proper; the transition is abrupt in the source and I have marked it with a bracketed editorial line where the OCR resumes mid-account, since material appears to be missing between the end of the Vaticanus speech ("some [monster]") and the narrative's resumption ("…in a free City"). A scholar should verify against the scan whether intervening text was lost in transcription here — this is the one place in the chunk where I suspect a genuine omission rather than mere character corruption. The single [?] flag in the body (Calvin 147, "Was he on that account to be killed?") marks not a reading uncertainty but the spot where the OCR *occidend⁹⁹* carried a doubled abbreviation glyph; the intended *occidendus* is clear and the [?] may be disregarded by a reader — I note it only for transparency.

*Proper names in the appendix.* "Amedeus Porrius" is Ami Perrin (rendered Amedeus Perrin); "Falesius" is the seigneur de Falais; "Schaffhuysiam" is Schaffhausen; "Cellarum … summum Professorem" is obscure in the OCR (*Cellarum* for a name or for the schools) and I have rendered it tentatively as the chief Professor of Theology of the city — a scholar should verify the referent against the scan. "Robert Stephanus" is Robert Estienne. The execution date "1553, 27 Oct." is as given (Servetus was in fact executed 27 October 1553).

*"Equum desultorium" (Calvin 135, Vaticanus).* Literally "a vaulting-horse" — the *desultor* leapt between horses in the circus; the metaphor is "turncoat / one who switches sides," and I have glossed it inline accordingly.
