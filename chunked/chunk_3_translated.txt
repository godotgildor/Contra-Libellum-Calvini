Calvin 93.
Do they infect souls with the poison of depraved dogmas, and shall the legitimate power of the sword be kept off from their bodies?
Vaticanus.
The envious also, and the avaricious, and the proud infect souls; for they assiduously beget many like themselves both by words and by examples: the envious [beget] the envious, the proud the proud, the avaricious the avaricious—but over their bodies there is no power of the sword. Herod was killed by an angel of the Lord on account of pride: but by a Magistrate he could not have been killed for that cause.
Calvin 94.
Shall the whole body of Christ be torn apart, that the stench of one putrid member may remain untouched?
Vaticanus.
Putrid members Paul cuts off, when he hands them over to Satan—that is, excommunicates [them]—and that for the punishment of the body, that the spirit may be saved in the Day of the Lord: and so he receives them again, if they have returned to health. This office of the pastor Calvin transfers to the Magistrate, even against himself. For in the case of Bertelier, he contended that this office belongs to pastors, not to the Magistrate. And over this matter he had a most grave controversy with the Genevan Magistrate, to such a degree that [the matter] was sent to the Helvetic churches. Now if the Magistrate cuts off members, the Pastor cannot do this, nor can they again be received: but if the pastor does it, the Magistrate cannot. I say nothing of [the fact] that to kill a man is not to cut off a member. For a member is cut off by unbelief, just as Paul taught, concerning the Jews cut off from the olive tree, who nevertheless were not killed. When the Magistrate kills a citizen, he does not deprive him of the City, but of life. Likewise when someone is killed as a heretic, he is not cut off from the body of Christ, but from the life of the body: otherwise, if the death of the body were a cutting-off, all who die would be cut off from the church.
Calvin 95.
And indeed that princes should act with moderation, I confess they are to be warned and exhorted by pious Doctors, and, after the manner of physicians, should not apply the ultimate medicine except to desperate diseases. But a light and humane method of healing differs much from dissolute softness, which is nothing other than a fomenting of evil.
Vaticanus.
If Calvin had observed this, he would never have accused Servetus of a capital charge on the very first day he saw him, nor afterward have suffered him to be killed so quickly and so cruelly—not to say, have done it [himself].
Calvin 96.
They adduce also testimonies of Scripture, in which they seem to have some color [of justification]: "Allow" (says Christ) "the tares to grow with the wheat, lest the wheat also be plucked up together [with them]." If they deal with us precisely according to the formula of the words, not only is the Magistrate prohibited from the use of the sword, but all discipline must be removed from our midst.
Vaticanus.
It must be explained more openly what it is to pluck up the tares, since the matter is of the greatest moment for this question. When there were among the Corinthians quarrels and dissensions, so that they said, "I am of Paul," "another, I am of Apollos," thus does Paul rebuke them: "I count it of the least [importance] to be judged of myself, either by you, or by any human day: nay, neither do I bear sentence concerning myself; for though I am conscious of nothing in myself, yet I am not therefore to be absolved. But he who is to make judgment of me is the Lord; wherefore judge nothing before the time, until the Lord come, who will bring forth into the Light the hidden things of darkness, and will make plain the counsels of minds, and then at last each shall obtain Praise from God. These things, brethren, I have applied to myself and to Apollos for your sakes, that in us you may learn not to arrogate to any beyond what is written, lest you swell up one over another against the other. For who is to judge of you?" Thus Paul, as if he should say this: He who says "I am of Paul" swells up over Paul, and prefers him by his own judgment to Apollos, and prefers himself to the disciples of Apollos. But this is to judge who may be more excellent and dearer to God. And yet this ought not to be judged by men, nor before the day of the Lord. And so, although I am conscious of no evil in myself, nevertheless I do not wish to judge myself just, even if I am just: for it is not mine to judge concerning myself, but the Lord's, whose judgment has not yet come, but is to be awaited. He who judges someone to be reprobate or elect, judges concerning the hidden things of darkness and the counsels of hearts. For from the counsels of hearts the Lord will reject or accept men. And yet those hidden things will not be made plain before the Light and the day of the Lord's coming: and so before that day it cannot be judged who is reprobate. Otherwise there would be a danger lest the Corinthians reprobate someone whom God would not reprobate, and not reprobate [someone] whom He would reprobate. And so although they have some knowledge, by which they might be able to judge concerning other matters, it is nevertheless not so great that Paul should permit them judgment concerning matters so arduous, since he does not permit it even to himself, who far surpassed them in knowledge. Now compare this passage of Paul with the parable concerning the tares; you will find [it] similar. For as for what the householder says: "Do not pluck them up before the time of harvest"—namely, lest perhaps, plucking up the tares, you pluck up also the wheat—this is the very thing that Paul says: "Judge nothing before the time"—namely, lest you perhaps condemn someone who is not to be condemned. For this is often done by those who hoe the Church with iron. And what that one says: "At the time of harvest I will give command to the reapers," etc.—this is the very thing that Paul says, "Until the Lord come," etc.—that is, when He shall say to the blessed: "Come," and to the cursed: "Go." Therefore to pluck up the tares is to judge someone to be reprobate, and to cut him off from the body of Christ unto eternity. Which thing cannot be done before the day of the Lord. Now what Calvin objects concerning the Magistrate pertains nothing to the tares. For when he kills a Robber, he does not pluck him out of the body of Christ, but out of this life. And often Magistrates hope well concerning the soul of those whose body they slaughter, nor do they kill them because they are evil, but because they have done evil and caused harm. But in heretics there is another reckoning. For those who kill them judge them to be reprobate and damned forever, in which they forestall the judgment of God, which Paul commands to be awaited. Therefore in killing malefactors there is no error, provided only that the crimes are established: for the malefactors themselves confess that they have deserved death. But in heretics there is error, for many pious men have been killed as heretics, confessing indeed that of which they were accused, but denying that it was a sin, or worthy of death.
First, Christ did not deny that of which he was accused—namely, that he had said that he was the Son of God: and for this cause he was killed as a blasphemer. The same happened afterward to the Apostles and the rest, who were killed for this reason, that they professed the Christian religion. Since therefore this befell them on account of the wickedness of men in the cause of religion—but in other causes it does not befall [them] (for those who then suffered on account of crimes were rightly killed, nor were the Jews nor the other nations reprehended by God on account of such punishments)—we ought not to think our age to be better than that one, such that error cannot occur in it, since it is written that the last times—that is, ours—will be the worst. To these things, since Christ here says that the sower is the Son of Man, but that the sower of the tares is an enemy man—that is, the Devil, who sowed the tares after the doctrine of Christ—he does not seem to call robbers and adulterers and the other malefactors of that kind "tares." For the Devil did not sow after the Gospel, since they existed already before the Gospel. But he seems to call "tares" those whom the Devil sowed by false doctrine after the Gospel—of which kind are Heretics and hypocrites, who, through a semblance of the Christian religion, deceive men: and thus they lie hidden among the pious, in the way that weeds lie hidden in the standing grain, until they have produced fruit. These Christ commands to be left until the harvest, lest perhaps some good man be killed together with them. For it is better that all the evil should live until the judgment, than that even one good man should be killed in the killing of the evil: just as a king would prefer that all the Robbers in his kingdom should live, rather than that one son of the king should be killed along with all the robbers. For let us frame the matter thus. There is a king of France who has several sons dispersed in his kingdom, clothed in white garments, but unknown to the people. Mingled with them are certain pretenders who, in order to be honored as sons of the king, have taken white garments, and boast that they too are sons of the King. The king, made more certain of this matter, issues an edict that no one dare to kill or harm a man clothed in a white garment, under penalty of death, lest perhaps some of the King's sons be killed; [declaring] that after a year he will gather and strip them all, and will kill the pretenders, but will save his own sons (in whose body, the garment removed, a Lily would be seen imprinted). Will he not be punished by the King, if anyone kills a white-clad man, whether he be the king's son or not? Now the King is Christ, the white-clad are his Disciples: but the Hypocrites and Heretics have mingled themselves with them and have taken white garments, that they may be honored as sons of God—that is, they live thus in appearance, so that they may seem to be holy and pious. Christ, understanding this, commanded both to be left until the day of judgment, in which, the garment of dissimulation removed, He will bring forth into the Light the hidden things of darkness, and will lay open the counsels of hearts, which now lie hidden. Ought not that Day to be awaited? Or do we think we have such great knowledge that we may see the counsels of hearts, which Paul teaches will not be laid open before the day of the Lord? Do we not act against this edict of Christ, if we kill hypocrites or heretics clothed in white—that is, guilty of no crime (for if they have committed some crime, they are now not white: for a crime is a black garment)? Can it not happen, in killing these, that some pious man be killed, as has always happened up to now? But if we say that we do not err, the very same was always said by those who killed the pious: can we not err, as they erred, in doing the very same things that they did, since we are in no way better than they? Who ever thought that he himself held a false religion? The Jews erred who persecuted Christ and the Apostles: the gentiles erred who persecuted the Christians. The Pope erred, who persecuted Lutherans and Zwinglians. Henry the King of England erred, who killed Papists and Lutherans and Zwinglians and Anabaptists. Luther erred, who called the Zwinglians Devils, and damned [them] into Gehenna. Or will the Zwinglians and Calvinists alone not err? Or will these alone sit on the tribunal of Christ, that to them alone it may be permitted to judge concerning Heretics, and to kill them? O wicked servant, who, when the lord comes more slowly, are made drunk with prosperous affairs, and beat and kill your fellow-servants—the lord will come at an hour when you least think, and will cut you asunder, and will place you there, where the Hypocrites are placed; there shall be weeping and gnashing of teeth.
Calvin 97.
Excommunication is the best remedy for purging the Church: but it will have no place, if it is not lawful to touch the tares.
Vaticanus.
It does not follow. For those whom Paul excommunicated he handed over to Satan for a time, that, subdued by this penalty, they might correct themselves, so that their spirit (when they had corrected themselves) might be saved in the day of the Lord. This was not to pluck up the tares: for once plucked up, the tares perish utterly: nor did Paul hold them for tares: for tares are not gathered into the barn. But Paul was saving those for the barn of Christ.
Calvin 98.
Add that the Lord there deals not only with perverse dogmas, but designates in general whatever corruptions Satan sows to vitiate the good seed. (1) Let Laws and judgments be gone, then, if the tares must be left until the harvest.
Vaticanus.
It does not follow, just as we have already shown. But Calvin, not understanding what the tares are, openly denies that those things are not to be left, which Christ openly teaches are to be left, so that he is the Antichrist—that is, openly contrary to Christ. He thinks the tares are plucked up by the Magistrate, when a man is killed: which is just as if he should think a fever is healed by the executioner, when he kills the fevered man. Thus indeed will Calvin be healed, if he loves this cure. He then proceeds, and after he has compared the church with a field, which is compelled to bear tares, he infers thus.
Calvin 99.
That indeed is true, that as soon as Christ comes forth with his Gospel, he separates the chaff from the wheat with a winnowing-fan. But he begins to do this in such a way that the full purgation is deferred to the last day: on which he will separate the lambs from the kids.
Vaticanus.
If the winnowing-fan is iron, this has nothing to do with the word of the Gospel: but if it is the word, this has nothing to do with the iron of the Magistrate. Moreover, Christ made his sowing with his Gospel; he could not separate the chaff from the wheat at the time of sowing, for this would be against nature: but neither can he, whenever he comes forth with his Gospel: for it is one thing to cultivate the crop still green, another to reap [it]. Altogether the whole harvest will be done at the time of harvest, and that not by the Magistrate, but by the messengers of God: nor will the chaff begin to be separated from the wheat before that day, or to be cast into the fire.
Calvin 100.
(1) Christ therefore does not command every rigor to cease, but admonishes that those evils are to be tolerated (2) which cannot be corrected without ruin.
Vaticanus.

Nor has anyone said that, so far as I know: for excommunication is a rigor, which no one removes: but it has nothing to do with the plucking up of the tares. (2) But Calvin thinks heretics can be killed by him without ruin, whereas there has never existed any greater ruin than that arising from the occasion of those who are held as heretics.

Calvin 101.
The authority of Gamaliel is wrongly adduced by them. Gamaliel persuades that the Scribes should keep quiet, [arguing] that if the doctrine is from men, it will dissolve of its own accord; but if it is from God, it can never be dissolved. For that this was said unskillfully readily appears from this, that it not only overthrows all political order, but also enervates the discipline of the church. And surely the person's [character] had to be taken into account. For Gamaliel, doubting what is right, like a blind man in the darkness dares to turn himself neither hither nor thither, but suspends his judgment. Meanwhile he draws a bad consequence from true principles—that no counsel is to be applied, because God will protect what is His own: but what is from men will go to ruin.
Vaticanus.
It is worth the trouble to see the concord of these men (who kill others who dissent from them). Bullinger, Calvin's associate in the shedding of blood, extols the counsel of Gamaliel with the highest praises in [his commentary on] the Acts, in these words: "Gamaliel, according to his prudence and the modesty of his mind, decreed far otherwise. Those men, accustomed in the temple to butchering cattle, discourse about blood and the slaughterhouse. But this man, accustomed to the Scriptures and the Law of God, draws [them] back from cruelty and counsels wisely. For God has His ministers even in the midst of the impious, whose work He uses for defending His own. Of such a kind we see Gamaliel in this place, whose authority indeed was great, not only among foreign cultivators of the Jewish religion, but also among our own people: for the Ecclesiastical Doctors have remembered him, not without a notable prerogative of honor. For Paul himself in the 22nd chapter of this Book confesses that he thoroughly learned the Law at the feet of this Gamaliel. Hence the divine Jerome makes mention of him in the catalogue of Ecclesiastical writers. Indeed Chrysostom, and after him Bede, on the authority of Clement of Alexandria, say that Gamaliel was in the faith an associate of the Apostles. It is therefore by no means to be wondered at, that he pleads the cause of the Apostles so cordially." Thus Bullinger. But Calvin is plainly contrary to so great an associate in a matter so great. For he resolves (because he pleads his own cause, not that of truth) to enervate all the best things. Above, he had commended Paul's sentence of the Law, which he had learned from Gamaliel; here he makes Gamaliel blind. Let us therefore ask of this so perspicacious Calvin, what he himself would have counseled the Scribes, if he had been present in that council? Doubtless that they should kill the Apostles. For since he calls him blind who dissuaded this, there is no doubt that he himself would have counseled the contrary—that is, death: and thus by Calvin's sentence the Apostles would have been killed: and surely he killed [them], as far as in him lies; but if he denies this, let him show us what he would have counseled them. He would have counseled, I believe, that they should dismiss them, but he would have adduced other reasons. What reasons, then? Would he have said they were innocent? It would have had no place among the Scribes, who did not believe that: and meanwhile counsel had to be given concerning a doubtful matter. What are you doing, Calvin? If someone had given the counsel of Gamaliel to Cardinal Tournon concerning those five men of religion, whom he burned at Lyon on a single pyre a little before you [burned] Servetus, would you say he had been blind? Or what would you have counseled him? That he should dismiss them? On what grounds? Because they cultivated the true religion? But the Cardinal did not believe this, and you could not have compelled [him] to believe. But you would have disputed. But he would not have admitted your disputations and reasons: for he is like the Scribes. Add that disputations are long, and have now lasted more than thirty years: and the present peril of the captives demands some present counsel. What therefore would you have counseled the Cardinal, by which you might provide for their lives? Do we not see that in such a cause nothing is more prudent than the counsel of Gamaliel? For he does not say this concerning a matter which is certain. If it had been a question of punishing adultery, he would never have said: "If it is from God"—for it was certain that adultery is not from God: nor do such things ever come into deliberation. But when it was a question of Religion, which nevertheless was in controversy, he could not have given the Jews a better counsel, even if he had been a Christian (which perhaps he was). First, the reason is true: "If it is not from God, it will go to ruin of its own accord." Such is that [saying] of Christ: "Every plant which my heavenly Father has not planted will be uprooted: let them alone." Likewise that of David, who, though he was able to kill Saul, was unwilling. Likewise the examples which Gamaliel adduces are true. For that Theudas and Judas the Galilean had come to nothing. Now what Gamaliel feared—namely, lest they fight with God—that befell them afterward. But if they had always followed the counsel of Gamaliel, they would never have fought with God. And today the reckoning is plainly the same. For if the Papists were to follow this counsel, they would never punish so many innocent men: which same thing I say of the Zwinglians and Calvinists, the Papists' disciples and imitators in this matter. Now, when they hear that Gamaliel, on Calvin's authority, was blind, if they wish to follow this leader, they will be plainly contrary to Gamaliel, and thus, on Calvin's authority, blood will be shed everywhere, even of the Calvinists. But this man is so cruel and pursues the shade of Servetus with such inexpiable hatred, that he would prefer even his own [followers] to be burned by the Papists, rather than be saved by this counsel of Gamaliel, lest he himself should seem to have sinned. Now, as for what Calvin says, that Gamaliel's saying is of such a kind that it overthrows all political order, and enervates the discipline of the church, he does not say truly. For neither was the discipline of the Apostles enervated [literally: "nerveless"], which nevertheless lacked these "nerves" of Calvin: and the political order remains in the case of certain crimes lacking controversy. In the Law of Moses, 70 Elders judged concerning the easier and lesser cases: and they referred the more difficult cases to Moses: he himself consulted God concerning them. So it can be done today. Concerning adulteries, concerning homicides, concerning false testimonies and the other known and certain crimes, it can be judged without danger: but concerning Heretics the matter is in controversy (for if it were not in controversy, there would be no more disputing about them than about homicides), and therefore it must be referred to the oracle. As, therefore, Moses awaited the time of the oracle, nor meanwhile overthrew the political order: so for us the time of God's Judgment must be awaited, which will be the certain oracle concerning these controversies. Meanwhile, nonetheless, God will use the ministry of His own in matters of controversy.
Calvin 102.
Claudius Lysias writes to Felix that he found no crime in Paul, because he only disputes with the Jews concerning the Law: not that he approves Paul's cause, but because he holds the Law of no account—nay, would wish it altogether abolished, and therefore rejoices that it is shaken by internal dissensions. By the voice of so good a patron, forsooth, Servetus attempted to dissolve the snares of the judgment, as if it were a heavenly oracle that Luke recites as having proceeded from so evil an author.
Vaticanus.
Lysias had been sent to judge concerning misdeeds or crimes. But concerning the Law he could no more judge than concerning music. And so, that he was unwilling to be judge concerning a matter neither known to him nor committed to him, he was wise. But if the Genevan Magistrate had been wise, he would never have judged concerning Servetus, no more than concerning music. For what "Hypostatically" and "cognitatively" may be, and the rest of the words never read in the Bible, which are bandied about in the case of Servetus, and altogether what this thorny and shadowy disputation of Calvin's concerning the Trinity—not even sufficiently explained by Calvin himself—may mean: I know for certain that the Genevan Magistrate knew it no more than Lysias had known Paul's cause. These things their Letters to the Helvetic Ministers show, in which they write that they do not doubt concerning the faith of their Ministers, as if they should say this: that they judge by another's faith, and with another's eyes.
Calvin 103.
They object at last: When Peter was ordered to put back the sword into its sheath, the Lord testified clearly enough how displeasing to him is a violent and armed defense of the Gospel. But what if I take exception, that he did not assert his innocence even in words before Pilate, as by right he could have?
Vaticanus.
This seems to me to be against Calvin. For if he did not fight even with words, how much less with iron?
Calvin 104.
If, willing to obey the decree of the Father, like a lamb before the shearer he became dumb, and for so long abstained from preaching the Gospel, while he expiated sins by the sacrifice of his death—do we marvel if he prohibited Peter from slaughter?
Vaticanus.
It is also the Decree of the same Father that we should suffer together [with Christ], if we wish to reign together [with him].
Calvin 105.
But there is another thing which I would answer. Christ does not grant to his minister the right of drawing the sword: for a private man rashly takes it up.
Vaticanus.
And then he was a private man, when he laid low Ananias and Sapphira. But tell me, Calvin, when you accused Servetus, did you not, a private man, snatch up the sword? For he who accuses, kills. Not you indeed with your own hand—but neither did Herod [kill] John with his own hand.
Calvin 106.
He does not wish the soldiers, by whom he was to be bound, to be repelled tumultuously—namely, lest he should incur this infamy, as if he nourished assassins for the ministers of the Gospel.
Vaticanus.
This Infamy Calvin did not fear.
Calvin 107.
From this no reasoning allows it to be gathered that the hands of Princes are to be bound, lest they exercise their ordinary office.
Vaticanus.
But we deny that it is the ordinary office of Princes.
Calvin 108.
In sum, he forbids Peter to act with force, not in order to disarm Magistrates, or to repel them from a Just defense of the Gospel, (1) but because he does not wish him to rush with headlong impetus beyond the bounds of his calling, nor that any delay be brought to his death, by which men were to be reconciled to God.
Vaticanus.
The office of the Magistrate Christ does not touch in this place. For if the Magistrate had performed his office, he would not have seized Christ, but would have defended [him] against those men—not on account of faith or Religion, but on account of the defense of an innocent man prescribed in the law. Why he prohibited Peter from using the sword, he himself states the cause: "The cup which the Father has given me, do you not wish that I drink it?" This cup Calvin knows how to offer to others, not to drink himself, for he thinks that nothing in it pertains to him, as if Christ so drank it that it need not be drunk by us: nor is Calvin of the number of those to whom it was said: "Ye shall indeed drink my cup." (1) Calvin's calling reaches further than Peter's: for to this man it is permitted to kill, but by another's hand.
Calvin 109.
Thus far I have in good faith reported whatever things are wont to be alleged by the adverse party, and I think I have grieved sufficiently [over them].
Vaticanus.
Nay, many other things are wont to be alleged, which are extant in that book concerning heretics, which we cited above; none of which Calvin truly washes away.
Calvin 110.
It remains that I teach not only that it is free for Magistrates to take penalties from the corruptors of heavenly doctrine, but that what (1) the unskilled are unwilling to be permitted to them, has been divinely mandated—that they cannot give impunity to (2) pestiferous errors, without falling away from the fidelity of their office.
Vaticanus.
He calls "unskilled" Augustine, Hilary, Chrysostom, Luther, and the rest, by whose testimonies nevertheless—especially Augustine's—he is wont often to prop up his doctrine. Let us therefore believe him to be unskilled, who relies on the testimonies of the unskilled, or [else] let him call those skilled, on whose testimonies he relies. 2. The Law grants pardon to errors, even if someone kills a man through error, which error is nevertheless death-bearing.
Calvin 111.
And first indeed, that the sense of nature dictates this—that in every well-ordered polity religion should hold the first place, and be preserved intact by the protection of Laws—the unbelievers themselves are witnesses: let the Philosophers be read, whoever have treated this argument. Certainly they begin from Divine worship, and from that kind of religion which they thought best, so that according to them he is a barbarous and insipid legislator, whose first care is not piety toward the Gods. But it will be shameful for us, that they saw in the darkness what escapes us in the light. Nor can it be attributed to superstition, that they embraced in their Laws the custody of religion.
Vaticanus.
A little before he repudiated the example of Lysias, whom he called an evil author. Now he relies on the example of unbelievers. Why should we imitate unbelievers rather than Lysias? Nor do I nevertheless deny that [their teaching] is to be admitted, if there was anything good in them. But that the same be denied to Lysias—at this I am indeed indignant. Moreover, since those Legislators handed down many faulty things, I am unwilling to follow them even in those things which they seem to have handed down well, unless they are established for me from elsewhere. For it is dangerous to believe him who has at some time deceived you. They handed down that new or foreign Gods are not to be admitted; for this cause the Athenian philosophers repudiated Christ: and [a similar reasoning] persuaded the Romans to repudiate Christ, because he was a new God. [?] But if our ancestors had followed these authors whom Calvin commends to us, they would never have received Christ; and now, if we wish to follow them, we shall repudiate Christ, and shall revert to Philosophy and vain knowledge (to use Paul's words), which would plunder us and deprive us of Christ. And so, if those [philosophers] had resolved that religion is to be defended by iron, I would not wish to follow them, unless I had certain testimony from elsewhere. But not even that did they resolve, so far as I know. They did indeed hold religion to be of first account in their Laws, but not so that they should hedge it about with the protection of iron, as Calvin wishes: for Cicero, in the 2nd [book] On Laws, where he testifies that he will give the best Laws and consonant with the commonwealth, thus begins: "Let them approach the Gods. Let them apply piety with purity. Let them put away riches. Whoever does otherwise, God himself shall be the avenger." And a little after, declaring this passage, he speaks thus: "But that not a judge, but God himself is constituted judge, religion seems to be confirmed by the fear of present punishment." And a little before this passage: he conceived the Law of perjury in these words: "The penalty of perjury is, divine, destruction; human, disgrace." You see here that impiety and the impious man are not subjected to the sword. For those Legislators made two kinds of sins, of which some they left to be punished by God, like those we have just mentioned: others, because they injured men, they mandated to be punished by men, as Cicero himself in the same place writes: "Whoever shall steal or carry off a sacred thing or a thing entrusted to a sacred place, let him be a parricide. The penalty of perjury is, divine, destruction; human, disgrace. Let the pontiffs sanction incest with the supreme punishment."
Calvin 112.
For what is more absurd than, when a Judge severely punishes thefts, to give license to sacrileges? When he keeps each one's honor safe, (1) to expose the glory of God to be torn apart by the impious?
Vaticanus.
An argument from the lesser to the greater: if lesser sins are punished, it is the more fitting that greater [ones] be punished. And first, he himself sins against this Law in those Laws of his, which I mentioned above, in which he sets a lighter penalty for avarice, lying, calumny, and Wrath, than for games and dances: then, in the whole Genevan commonwealth, since there theft and the coining of false money are openly punished against the Law of Moses; but adultery and disobedience to parents (which are far graver, and are sanctioned with [the penalty of] life [i.e., capitally] in the law) are not punished. But if he does not seek these things from his Magistrate, why does he urge this so greatly here? But if, seeking [them], he cannot obtain [them], he has a disobedient and evil Magistrate. And therefore he ought not to have permitted him to persecute heretics, since he has taught that that persecution is not to be permitted except to the good. Next, he sins in that, while disputing about heretics, he names sacrileges, as though indeed sacrilege and heresy were the same. Finally, in that some are wont to argue without distinction: "If the Magistrate is the defender of the second table, how much more of the first?" These are the sophisms of idle men, by which they ensnare the unskilled crowd. For thus, after their manner, it would be permissible to argue: "If pastors can command the minds of Magistrates, how much more their bodies? If by the word they can bind souls, how much more bodies by iron? If by the word it was permitted to Peter to lay low Ananias, how much more Malchus by iron? If it was lawful for Elijah to call down fire from heaven to burn up the King's messengers, how much more for the Apostles to burn up the Samaritans? If it was permitted to Moses to kill the Egyptian, how much more to Christ?" These are all fallacies, by which those sophists impel men to the shedding of blood. The matter can be explained thus: "If theft is punished, how much more shall Hypocrisy and avarice and Envy be punished," etc. Rightly: but theft [is punished] by men, Hypocrisy by God. "If he who tears the honor of a man is punished, how much more he who [tears the honor] of God?" Rightly: but this [is punished] by God, that by men. For the Magistrate punishes the action, God the thought: this man [punishes] drunkenness, that one [punishes] the disposition to drunkenness; this man theft, that one the propensity to theft; this man the adultery of the body, that one [the adultery] of the mind; this man rape, that one rapacity; this man fornication, that one the will to fornicate; this man homicide, that one hatred: finally, that one [punishes] the root, this man the branches; this man the stream, that one the source; this man the body, that one the soul. Unless we distinguish these things thus, we shall confound all things. (1) Herod tore the glory of God, when, the people praising his eloquence, he grew proud, nor attributed the glory to God: nor nevertheless could he have been punished by the Magistrate: for no Law sanctions with [the penalty of] life [capitally] pride of that kind. And so he was punished by God, who punishes the soul. The same I say of Nebuchadnezzar, who, growing proud, said that that great Babylon had been founded by himself, on account of which arrogance he was turned into a beast. The same I say also of those whom God thus rebukes: "O sons of men, how long will you turn my glory into infamy? How long will you love vanity and seek a lie?" To love vanity is to tear the glory of God: and yet, if Calvin should wish all the lovers of vanity to be punished by the Magistrate, no one would be left for him who could punish the Magistrate himself. Let these things be punished by God, not by men.
Calvin 113.
Concerning perjuries no one will dare to move dispute that it should be gravely punished. Why do they confess this, who deny that trouble is to be shown to heretics? Unless because human society is injured by perjuries?
Vaticanus.
Perjury, if it harms another, as happens in false testimonies, is punished by retaliation [in kind]—not by the law of perjury, but by the law of false testimony: otherwise the penalty of perjury is divine—destruction, as we set down above from Cicero, as is plain in Numbers 5, where, if a wife, suspected by her husband of adultery, has perpetrated it, the bitter water having been applied, she is punished by God, not by the Magistrate. But let it be granted indeed; let perjury be punished: what is this to heretics? He who perjures, sins knowingly: like him who kills a man: the heretic (as heretics now [are]) [does] not likewise. Why shall we place in the same position those who sin while erring, with those who do [it] of set purpose? Even perjury perpetrated unknowingly the Law condones, and for this matter the sacrifice was instituted. But to the homicide who has done it unknowingly, pardon is given, and return [from exile] after the death of the High Priest. Why should not the death of Christ furnish the same to those who err?
Calvin 114.
Therefore the glory of God, implicated with the conveniences of men, will be vindicated; but where no one will complain of his own loss, (1) will it lie with much mockery?
Vaticanus.
Nay, it will be punished—but by God, as we said concerning Herod and the rest.
Calvin 115.
It would be superfluous to pursue this with more words. For since the end of right polity is the conservation of legitimate order among men, but the neglect of piety so dissipates this order that without it the life of men is brutish: it is established that the form of governance is mutilated, in which religion is neglected. And [it is established] that Magistrates are only half [magistrates], and shadowy, who, intent only on forensic business, do not think about asserting the worship of God.
Vaticanus.
The legitimate order is, that the Pastor should care for souls, but the Magistrate for bodies. He who hands souls over to the Magistrate deprives the Pastor of his office, and perverts all order. So Moses by prayers, Joshua by the hand, waged war. "Render the things that are Caesar's to Caesar, and the things that are God's to God."
Calvin 116.
An illustrious example of this matter the Spirit of God sets before us in Nebuchadnezzar. For Daniel celebrates his edict, by which he denounces capital punishment, if anyone be a blasphemer against the God of Israel. No common honor, surely, is shown to a cruel tyrant, when the lord assigns his Prophet, as it were a herald, to publish the Laws which [the tyrant] passed, and refers the Laws themselves to his own acts, and reckons [them] among his sacred oracles. What? Is Nebuchadnezzar praised by the eulogy of the Holy Spirit and the prophet—he who undertook to protect the glory of the true God by his authority—in order that holy Magistrates should connive at its impious profanation? And does not the Lord rather, under the person of a profane king, prescribe to them what becomes them?
Vaticanus.
Since before, with such great disdain, Calvin repudiated the counsel of Gamaliel, why he now so greatly celebrates that of Nebuchadnezzar, what the cause may be I do not see—except that the one was milder, this harsher, not to say more savage: and Calvin willingly embraces all savage things, rejects the mild. For as for what he says, that this edict is celebrated by Daniel, the same can be said of the counsel of Gamaliel: for Luke narrates the one just as Daniel [narrates] this: neither interposes his own opinion, whether it be right or not. But Calvin, a Nebuchadnezzarian man, extols this beyond measure, [and] reports that one to be blind. But to me Gamaliel seems milder and more fitting for the new covenant.
Nebuchadnezzar savors of iron and a plainly cruel tyrant, as Calvin himself calls him: and with the same rashness he can be seen to have promulgated this edict, with which a little before he had wished to kill his wise men, because they had not known how to explain his dream: or with which he had adored Daniel, and commanded sacrifice and incense to be offered to him. But if it pleases [Calvin] to imitate this, let sacrifice be offered also to Calvin, not only to God. Such was the not-to-be-imitated rashness of those who wished to offer sacrifice to Paul and Barnabas, and soon, most easily changed, stoned Paul. So here Nebuchadnezzar, after he threatened death to all who should speak evil of the God of Shadrach, Meshach, and Abednego, was himself so far from attributing to that God the glory worthy [of him], that—as is narrated in the following chapter—he grew immoderately proud against him, and for that cause was turned into a brute nature. But let this edict of the tyrant be just indeed, since Calvin willingly approves the deeds of tyrants—nevertheless it is not to be imitated under the new covenant. It can be answered: "You know not of what spirit you are; the son of man came not to destroy but to save." If Christ did not wish that the Apostles should imitate Elijah—a private man [imitate] a private man, a prophet [imitate] a prophet—how much less would he wish that Christian Magistrates should imitate the bloody edict of a tyrant? But let it be; let us concede all savage things to Calvin; let the tyrant indeed be imitated—what will follow from it? That all who speak evil of the God of Israel are to be killed: but by this law indeed no one will be killed: for all confess God. But let us transfer these things to Christ: The Jews are to be killed, who speak evil of Christ. This [pertains] nothing to heretics, who confess Christ, even if they err. For to err is not to speak evil: just as if someone, erring, should say that Calvin is clothed in a white garment, when it is red, he would not thereby straightway speak evil of him. But indeed if it pleases [us] to imitate the edicts of Kings, let us imitate the edict of Cyrus, whom Ezra celebrates far more magnificently than Daniel [celebrates] Nebuchadnezzar. For it was done by the instinct of the divine Spirit, by which he decreed that the Jews who dwelt in his kingdom should set out for Jerusalem, but he compelled no one. So let us try to compel no one to piety: for no one will become good unwilling. And those who wish to compel men to faith do just as if someone should thrust food with a staff into the mouth of a refusing sick man. The Sacred letters, when they speak of teaching charity, use the word "to persuade": these men wish not to persuade by words, but to compel by iron: therefore they have iron Christians, just as an iron Gospel. But let us proceed, and, omitting Calvin's exclamations, now sufficiently refuted, let us come to the matter.
Calvin 117.
But now let us hear what right the lord established in his church. For after he commanded the prophet and the Dreamer, who should attempt to lead the people away from the right worship of piety, to be killed, he extended the same penalty to individuals in these words: "If your brother, the son of your mother, or your son, or your daughter, or the wife who is in your bosom, or the neighbor whom you have as your own soul, incite you, saying secretly: Let us go and serve foreign Gods, which neither you nor your fathers knew, you shall not acquiesce to him, nor hear him: your eye shall not spare him, nor shall it be propitious to him, that you should hide him: but rather, killing, you shall kill him, and let your hand be first, that it may kill him: then the hand of the whole people: and you shall overwhelm him with Stones."
Vaticanus.
These things [pertain] nothing to Heretics: for none of them says: "Let us go and serve foreign Gods": nay, they all profess the same God whom we acknowledge, and our fathers [acknowledged]. Nor does anyone impel us to the worship of Baal, or Astarte, or Neptune. But if Calvin should say that those who understand the sacred scriptures wrongly worship foreign Gods, he will speak ill: for the Sadducees also understood them wrongly, when they denied the resurrection, which error was surely graver than any of Servetus's: and likewise, when concerning the kingdom of God they thought carnally about marriages: nor were they ever accused of this crime: nor did Christ call them worshippers of a foreign God, but said that they erred, not knowing the scriptures, and benignly taught them. Thomas also erred, and indeed gravely, who was unwilling to believe that Christ had risen before he had seen [it], although he had both Christ's preceding preaching and the consenting testimony of all the Apostles. The Apostles themselves also erred, who at first did not think that the gentiles were to be admitted into the grace of the Gospel, which error was of such moment that it would exclude us all from grace; nor nevertheless were they idolaters, or impious, or blasphemers. Finally, Calvin himself contends that the Papists not only err, or are Heretics, but are impious and blasphemers and worshippers of a foreign god—or rather of Gods—and Antichrists. But if they have a foreign God, they baptize children in the name of a foreign God. And yet Calvin himself was baptized by them and holds this baptism ratified. But if that baptism is true, the Heretic Papists must have the true God: thus it comes about that Heretics ought not to be numbered among the worshippers of a foreign God. But if the baptism is bad, Calvin ought to be rebaptized, nor is he yet a Christian. But if he interprets "foreign Gods" [to mean] sins—Avarice, Luxury, and the rest, which in the new Testament are called Gods (of whom "the belly is god," he says, "and avarice, which is the worship of Idols")—and which were signified by those foreign Gods of the old Testament: these [pertain] nothing to the magistrate. Each one ought to be a Magistrate to himself in this business, as Christ teaches: "If your eye, or hand, or foot impedes you, cut [it] off." This is that true stoning which is signified by this Law of Moses: at which, when one has arrived, that [former stoning] has its end: just as the shadows of the Law have their end, after the Light has come. But if Calvin wished to keep the Law, Servetus ought not to have been burned, but stoned by the people, and Calvin himself, as the most Innocent, [ought] to have thrown the first stone.
Calvin 118.
Let them now go and accuse God of cruelty, for whom defection from the pure faith and the worship of God is in the manner of a venial offense.
Vaticanus.
Who these may be, I know not. To me this defection seems a most grave and plainly Lethal sin, and so Lethal that it cannot be punished by men (who do not have eternal death), but only by God with eternal fire, as is shown in the Epistle to the Hebrews: "If we sin willingly" (it says) "after having received the knowledge of the truth, there is no longer left a sacrifice for sins, but a certain dreadful expectation of Judgment, and a vehemence of fire, which is to devour the adversaries." But this defection is [a sin] against the holy spirit, whose punishment is not given to the Magistrate, but reserved to God. And Calvin, having promised that he would dispute concerning heresy, turns aside here, as if it were the same: that he might be able to overwhelm those who dissent from him (whom he is wont to call heretics), overborne with all infamous names. This same art the Scribes and Pharisees once practiced.
Calvin 119.
But before we proceed further, it is worth the trouble to note two things. For neither does he [God] so severely conserve and sanction religion, whatever it finally may be, but [only] that which he had instituted by his word. Next, he does not decree the penalty of stoning against foreigners, but [against] those who, having professed the doctrine of the Law, have perfidiously departed from it. Thus is the offense removed, which deceives many of the unskilled, while they fear lest under this pretext the Pope's executioners be armed for savagery. For if the Lord laid open to his people a certain rule of piety, before he subjected apostates to penalties, what mortal will dare to take this upon himself—that he should sanction with the sword and penalties a kind of doctrine fabricated at his own or another's discretion? Therefore we do not equip Magistrates with a vague and blind power, that under their protection any received religion whatsoever should remain in its state without discrimination: but we contend that the Law divinely fixed beforehand must be held, so that the sanction of penalties may at last succeed [only] to ascertained truth.
Vaticanus.
All sects protect their religion by the word of God, and say it is certain. And so all Sects, armed with this norm of Calvin, will persecute others. Calvin says that his [religion] is certain, and they [say] theirs [is]: he says that they err, and they [say] that he [errs]: Calvin wishes to be Judge, and they wish [to be]. Who will be Judge? Who constituted Calvin Judge of all the sects, that he alone may be able to kill others? By what Judgment does he show that he alone is wise? He has the word of God, and they have [it]. If the matter is so certain, to whom is it certain? To Calvin? Others think it is certain to themselves. But why does Calvin write so many Books concerning ascertained truth? Why does he write no book by which he shows that homicide, or adultery, is a crime? Because the matter is certain. Why does no one suffer himself to be burned in order to deny that those things are true which are in the sacred letters? Because the matter is in confession with all [i.e., acknowledged by all]. Why therefore do there exist those who endure to be killed on account of rebaptism, and certain other things, when nevertheless they admit and revere the sacred letters? Is it not because the matter is in controversy? But if Calvin already has all things examined and known, why does he not wait until others too perceive them? Why, by killing them, does he bring it about that they cannot learn? There are twelve hours of the day: one can learn at the eleventh hour: "how do you know" (says Paul) "whether you will save your wife?" But if Paul still wishes [us] to hope concerning an unbelieving spouse, who nevertheless could not yet be recalled by the daily admonitions of the spouse, why may we not also hope concerning heretics?
Calvin 120.
Wherefore there is no [reason] that the patrons of errors should flatter themselves, who, in the shedding of innocent blood, indulge their foolish zeal and ignorance, that they may at least cast the burden of Inquisition onto others, so that to themselves (1) it may be permitted, from the responses of others, cruelly to kill the holy martyrs of Christ.
Vaticanus.
You open a window to them by that Book, no less than [you do] to your own [followers]. For since they think they render worship to God when they kill the pious, and are moreover impelled by your exhortation (for you have not yet persuaded them that they are evil, so that they should think persecutions are to be abstained from by themselves, and permitted to the Genevans), they will henceforth be carried headlong into blood, like you, and whoever dissents from them, they will kill, like you: so that no Nero ever, no Caracalla, no Heliogabalus—nor finally all the Tyrants together ever—shed so much blood as you and Zwingli have, by those bloody sayings and writings of yours, exarated [written out] in blood, both hitherto shed and will henceforth shed—unless, the human race being regarded by God, princes and Magistrates, bewitched and blinded by you, begin to open their eyes, and henceforth refuse you their ministry in the drawing of blood, (1) just as the Genevan senate, from the responses of Calvin and his associates, killed a man, concerning which causes it could no more judge than the blind concerning colors.
Calvin 121.
To these things is solved the objection of certain ones, who ask whether Jews, Turks, and the like are to be driven to the faith of Christ by the sword. For God does not command the sword to be drawn against all indiscriminately: but he subjects to just penalty Apostates, who have impiously alienated themselves from the true worship, and have attempted to draw others to a like defection.
Vaticanus.
"Ask of me" (says the Father to the Son) "and I will give you the nations as an Inheritance, and the ends of the earth as a possession." Likewise the Son: "All power is given to me in heaven and on earth." And so, since he is king of the whole world, no nation can be excluded from being one to be persecuted by the sword of the Christians, if Calvin's Law is true. Now indeed the Turks and the rest in Asia are Apostates: for there the Gospel was preached and believed: and so these especially would be to be persecuted. What shall I say of the Jews, to whom it was said, "The Lord will raise up a Prophet for you," etc.; whom if anyone does not obey, he shall die? Now that this prophet is Christ, Stephen shows: whom, this Christ, since the Jews repudiated, these are especially worthy of punishment. But if Calvin holds heretics for Apostates, since he judges the Papists to be Heretics, he ought to persecute them first of all, and, an army of Genevans having been gathered, to invade France first of all, which has defected from the true religion of the Apostles. But if he denies that he has strength enough, it will be answered: "Do not fear them; one of you will put to flight a hundred, and a hundred will put to flight ten thousand." But if not even thus does he dare, he ought at least to have apprehended Cardinal Tournon, who passed through Geneva a little before the death of Servetus, setting out (which all knew) to burn pious men, who were at Lyon in chains, whom indeed a little afterward he burned. Here, here ought that cook of Calvin's to have been sent in—or Calvin himself—to accuse that executioner Cardinal: it would have saved their lives. But he prefers to conspire with the Papists, and always to join himself with the more powerful.
Calvin 122.
But now, whoever shall contend that punishment is unjustly inflicted on heretics and blasphemers, will, knowingly and willingly, bind himself with the same charge of blasphemy.
Vaticanus.
Since he finds no passage in the sacred letters which commands heretics to be killed, he maliciously joins them with blasphemers, so that if anyone should wish to vindicate them from death, he may be burdened by the odium of fellowship of name, as one who should wish to patronize blasphemers. So he did in the Book which he wrote against the Anabaptists and Libertines, in which, that he might burden the Anabaptists, he joins them with the Libertines, when those two sects are no less contrary to each other than Calvin and Servetus. He learned this art from the Jews who crucified Christ among robbers. He then proceeds to exaggerate his zeal for piety: he commands that paternal love—whatever there is of benevolence between brothers, kinsmen, and friends—be gone, and he does all this that he may be able to overwhelm his enemies. For if he himself were as zealous for preserving the Law as he pretends, he would have begun from his own [people] (he himself will understand me when he reads these things). Why does he begin from heretics, whom the law nowhere names? Why not rather from adulterers, of whom Geneva has a great number, and whose penalty is named expressly in the Law? But if he does not obtain this from the Magistrate, what kind of Magistrate does he have? Why does he more easily obtain [punishment] from him concerning the uncertain errors of heretics, than concerning the certain crimes of adulterers? Nor do I say these things because I wish adulterers to be punished with the Mosaic penalty. I would prefer that other penalties too be softened rather than that this one be intensified, and a return be made to Moses: I know what kind of men we are today: if we were all punished with death, who deserved death according to the Law of Moses—good God, who would escape? But since Calvin calls us so severely to that law, it is unworthy that he should connive at such well-known crimes, especially since he has a Magistrate so compliant, that through him he can kill those whom the Law nowhere commands to be killed—not to say meanwhile that he allows the coiners of false money and thieves at Geneva to be killed against the Law, since the Law punishes thieves not with death, but with a fine. Now, what kind of thing in God's name is this, that he cites the Law concerning the utter overthrowing of the cities of those who worship foreign Gods, together even with their cattle and household goods? Therefore, if Calvin obtains the strength, he will invade France and the other nations which he holds for Idolaters: he will go, he will overthrow cities, he will slaughter all the men, he will spare neither women nor those worn out with age nor sucklings: moreover he will slay the cattle, and will burn the household goods, heaped up in the middle of the marketplace, together with Servetus. Let his words be weighed: They tend to this: To this that Jew recalls us, who reads Moses with veiled countenance, nor understands that the end of the Law is Christ, nor allows place to be given to that oracle: "The son shall not bear the iniquity of the father." For what did the infant deserve, if the father is an idolater? Shall we never, the shadows of the law being left behind, come into the Light?
Calvin 123.
But where Religion is pulled up from its foundations, detestable blasphemies against God are brought forth, souls are seized into destruction by impious and pestiferous dogmas: finally, where defection from the one God and his pure doctrine is openly attempted, it is necessary to descend to that extreme remedy, lest the mortal poison creep further.
Vaticanus.
This monster Calvin describes to us, such as I—far be it that I should wish to defend it—let them perish by all means, if any openly teach defection from the one God. But that those who dissent from Calvin, and whom Calvin holds for heretics, are of this kind, I do not believe. For many, whether Zwinglians, or Lutherans, or Anabaptists, or Papists, dissent in the gravest matters, who nevertheless venerate one God, and teach [him] to be venerated: nay, I do not believe that even Servetus himself (whom Calvin wished here to depict) was of this kind. For he would never have wished to die, but rather would have recanted, so that thus the blasphemies of Servetus and the innocence of Calvin might be made plain. There must have been in his books something which Calvin dreaded. For he did not burn [them] fearing lest the errors of Servetus be published, since Calvin himself published to the whole world, by this his book, those things which he thinks to be that man's most detestable errors. I believe that Servetus in that book had uncovered many errors of Calvin, as he seems to signify in Calvin's own book on page 223, where Servetus speaks thus: "Would that it were free for me to make a Catalogue of your errors." And so Calvin, fearing lest he be uncovered, burned both the man and the book. But God sees him, whose eyes he does not dread; all things will be made plain: Calvin will never extinguish so many that there will not survive those who see and uncover his errors. The more diligently and the longer he has covered himself, the more hateful will he afterward be to those very ones whom he deceived.
Calvin 124.
Moses the Judge, how humanely he conducted himself in punishing other crimes, is established: but in vindicating the worship of God he wholly burns [with zeal]. "Consecrate," he says, "your hands to the Lord: let no one spare his brother or kinsman: as each one comes in your way, kill [him] without pardon." Do we think he was suddenly seized by frenzy, and so forgetful then of his accustomed clemency, that he commanded the sacred Levites to be made wet with blood?
Vaticanus.
This passage refutes Calvin: those were Levites; we have no other Levites than Christ, who is a priest according to the order of Melchizedek: and all Christians, who are a royal priesthood. But if the deed of the Levites is to be imitated, it had to be imitated by Christ, who succeeded them, and indeed he imitated [it]. For as those bodily priests bodily did not spare their brothers and kinsmen, so the spiritual priest Christ spiritually does not spare his brothers and kinsmen: "Who is my mother" (he says) "or who are my brothers?" "Get behind me, Satan," he says to Peter. Likewise: "he who has not renounced father and mother," etc., "and even his very self, cannot be my disciple." This is that spiritual killing of kinsmen, signified by that deed of the Levites, and declared to us by this our eternal Levite, whom if we wish to retain, it is necessary that we allow those figures to vanish: but if we retain the figures, we are nothing other than Jews, and we have fallen from grace, who wish to be under the law: for both cannot be retained.
Calvin 125.
If certain ones deny that this order agrees with the kingdom of Christ, which is spiritual, and with the present age, because nowhere in the Gospel is such a mandate given. Therefore, because Christ and the Apostles do not in express words enjoin with what kind of penalty adulteries and thefts are worthy, shall magistrates therefore cease from their office? If someone takes exception, that the polities which were not abolished at Christ's coming should take penalties from misdeeds as they are constituted by Laws: indeed I willingly subscribe to this response. (1) That there were no Magistrates, to whom the Apostles might prescribe what was right, I forbear to say. With this one thing I am content: that by Christ's coming neither was the political order changed, nor anything detracted from the office of Magistrates. Come now—what Paul teaches, that the sword is not borne by them in vain—ought it to be restricted to one species?
Vaticanus.
And if we conceded all these things to Calvin, and even admitted the whole Law of Moses, he has nevertheless effected nothing. He had said that he would show that Heretics are to be coerced by iron; what a Heretic is, he nowhere defines: which is a thing very faulty—nowhere to define the matter (especially a controverted one) about which you dispute by name, since a disputation ought to set out from a definition. But he did this maliciously. For if he had defined a heretic, he would nowhere have found a Law which commands him to be killed: he preferred therefore to confound the matter, and to join heretics with blasphemers and Idolaters and Apostates, so that those whom he could not kill separately, he might slay joined with the rest. But this fraud does not satisfy us: let Calvin show a Law which commands heretics to be killed: there is none. But concerning Apostates and blasphemers there is [one]. What have we to do with Apostates and blasphemers? The question is concerning heretics. Show a law concerning heretics. Or are heretics blasphemers? Define then what a heretic is, and that from the sacred letters, so that we may understand whether a heretic is a blasphemer. For who will concede to you that those who today are held heretics are blasphemers or Impious? Who in his right mind will concede to the Pope that Lutherans and Zwinglians and Calvinists are blasphemers, even if they dissent from him in [the matter of] the Supper? Whom nevertheless he condemns no less than they [condemn] the Pope. Who will concede to Zwingli that the Anabaptists are blasphemers, even if they dissent from him in Baptism? Who will concede to Calvin that the Zwinglians and Lutherans are blasphemers, whom nevertheless he condemns concerning the Supper in his little book? Likewise Luther and Brenz and the rest, who dissent from him in this very question concerning persecution? Than which scarcely any [question] can be graver, inasmuch as in it the matter treated is the life of a man, on account of whom Christ died. The blasphemer and the one erring are not the same: these must be distinguished, especially in a capital cause, lest we so lightly kill men on account of an unknown crime, nowhere named in the law. I deny that anywhere in the whole Law there is a precept concerning killing those who err or heretics: and Calvin has now for a long time mocked us in unworthy ways, while he seeks their penalty from the Law, and they are nowhere named in the Law. Now he comes to Christ, that he may show that under him the Law remains, and that the same reckoning of punishing endures even in causes of Religion which is in the law: but if anyone should concede this to him, nevertheless not even thus would he have any law concerning the punishing of Heretics. But who in his right mind will concede this to him, that the law remains under Christ? Who will suffer Christ to be snatched from him, that he may return to Moses with Calvin? Let Calvin be a disciple of Moses with his Jews: for us the Messiah has now come, our Legislator, whose Law we wish to obey. For let us consider the matter thus: The Law was a pedagogue until Christ, until the promised seed should come: when Christ came, the pedagogue ceases, to such a degree that Paul says those who wish to be circumcised have fallen from grace. And no one is admitted into the polity of Moses without circumcision: for this is the entrance into it: and so those who wish to be under Moses ought to be circumcised, and to fulfill the whole Law. Let the epistle to the Galatians be read diligently, and likewise [the epistle] to the Hebrews, in which it is shown that the Aaronic priesthood has been transferred to the tribe of Judah—that is, to Christ, who is a priest according to the order of Melchizedek—where it is said in express words that, when the priesthood is transferred, it is necessary that a transfer of the Law also be made. Here you see the Law transferred—namely, from Moses to Christ, from shadows to Light, from figures to the thing itself, from flesh to Spirit. But as for what Paul says, that the Law is spiritual, this is the very thing I wish. The mind of the Law is spiritual, but the external actions [are] carnal. Circumcision is spiritual, concerning which Moses, speaking, says: "Circumcise the foreskin of your heart": but the people of the Israelites did not understand this Circumcision: and so they clung to the circumcision of the flesh made by hand: which Paul shows, when he says that they have a veiled heart while Moses is read, nor do they notice the end of the Law that is to be abolished. But those who have come to Christ—from these the veil is removed, and abolished by Christ. And they have the spirit of the law—that is, the circumcision of the heart. For to this the Law sends men, but does not lead [them] through, but remains beyond the Jordan. But since the Law is partly Ceremonial, partly judicial (concerning the moral I do not speak at present), each part has been transferred. For those sacrifices migrated into the sacrifice of Christ, and in him have their end, just as the letter in the Spirit. But that a transfer has been made of the judgments, the transfer of the sins shows, for the punishing of which the Judgments were constituted. It was a sin to work on the Sabbath day; for this sin the penalty constituted was the death of the body. Now that bodily Sabbath has been transferred into a spiritual [one], which same I say concerning the sin: for he who works on the Sabbath day does not sin. What therefore will be the penalty of the violated spiritual Sabbath? Doubtless spiritual. The same I say of Circumcision, that just as then the bodies of those were killed who had bodies uncircumcised, during the [time of] circumcision of bodies: so now the hearts of those are to be killed who have hearts uncircumcised, after the circumcision of the heart has been shown to us, and succeeded the carnal [one]. And so here the judgments of the Magistrate will have no place. As in the Law of Moses: for they cannot touch the uncircumcised heart, as [they can] the body. The same I say concerning that law: "A Prophet from your Nation and from your brothers" (says Moses) "like me, your God will raise up for you; him you shall hear. But whoever will not hear his words, I will become an avenger." And this law the People confirmed, when they said to Joshua the Prophet: "Whoever shall contradict your mouth, and will not obey all the words which you shall command him, let him die." Here I see a corporal penalty. But when it came to Christ, who is that true Prophet, as Stephen shows, and concerning whom the Father thus commanded: "Hear him": the penalty is of souls, not of bodies, if anyone does not obey him, as is taught in the epistle to the Hebrews: "He who makes void the Law of Moses dies by two or three witnesses: with how much graver punishment, do you think, will he be afflicted, who tramples on the son of God, and shall have said the blood of the Testament to be polluted, in which he is sanctified, and shall have done insult to the spirit of grace? For we know who said: To me [is] vengeance, and I will repay. And again: God will judge his people. It is a fearful thing to fall into the hands of the living God." You see here that the penalty of unbelief or of defection from Christ is not mandated to the magistrate, but reserved to the living God. But if it were committed to the magistrate, the Jews ought to be punished first of all according to this law, because they were unwilling to hear that Prophet, Christ, raised up for them by God; whom nevertheless Calvin (above) excluded from the sword of this his magistrate. Therefore, just as to those who believe in Christ life is promised—not temporal life, but eternal—so unbelievers death awaits, not temporal, but eternal. And just as how those are to be punished who have sinned against the Law of Moses must be learned from the law itself: so how those are to be punished who have sinned against Christ must be learned from the law of Christ itself, which is thus: "He who will not have believed shall be damned"—that is, he shall give penalties to God, not to men. What therefore Calvin asks—what Paul teaches, that the sword is not borne by them in vain, whether it ought to be restricted to one species—this is wholly against Calvin. For if he wishes this to be understood concerning each species—that is, concerning causes both of religion and civil—Calvin ought to render back to us all the Ceremonies and sacrifices of the law, so that those who sin in it may be punished by the law: he ought to render back the Asylums, so that he who kills a man unknowingly may flee into the Asylum until the death of the High Priest, so that thus we may have a new Judaism. But if he says that those ceremonies have migrated into others—namely, Circumcision into Baptism, and Passover into the Supper: first I will ask, why have the other ceremonies too not migrated into others: have they perished, or rather have they migrated into the thing itself? But if they migrated into the thing—as Aaron's bodily pontificate into the spiritual [pontificate] of Christ—I ask why it cannot also be said that the Circumcision of the flesh too migrated, not into the Washing of the flesh, but into the circumcision of the heart, just as Baptism and the Supper are new Ceremonies of the new testament, which nevertheless signify the same things that those did. Next I will ask why the penalty too has not been changed, the Ceremonies having been changed: ought those who sin in these ceremonies to be punished with the same penalty with which those were punished who sinned in those? If they ought, Calvin must be punished, who does not baptize children on the eighth day. Likewise [for the fact] that he sometimes was unwilling to administer the Supper with unleavened breads, when the Law forbids leaven during the days of Passover. Those too ought [to be killed] who do not approach the Supper of the lord, or who eat unworthily—to be killed, just as those were killed who neglected the Passover, or did not act rightly. But if he should say that there is now another penalty—namely, spiritual, so that he who eats unworthily eats judgment to himself, that is, a spiritual penalty from God, not a corporal [one] from the Magistrate—this is the very thing we wish: namely, that the corporal penalty has migrated into a spiritual [one], which, of what kind it is, must be learned from the new Testament, not from the old, since that one hands down no Law concerning Baptism and the Supper. Thus it will come about that in causes of Ceremonies, or of Religion, the corporal sword has no place, inasmuch as it has migrated into a spiritual [one]. But as for what Paul says, that the Magistrate bears the sword not in vain—this is most of all against Calvin. For Paul speaks concerning every Magistrate, and most of all the Roman, who then ruled nearly everywhere, who surely had not received his law from Moses. And so [the fact] that Christ paid tribute to the Romans for himself and for Peter—he did not pay it in order to obey the Law of Moses in that, but rather did [it] against the Law of Moses. For the Law of Moses nowhere commanded that the free should pay tribute: and he himself, since he was Free, did not owe [it]. And so [the fact] that Calvin says the political order was not changed by Christ's coming is valid to this end: that that order should remain which then was—that is, that, since we are gentiles, not Jews, we ought not to seek the Law from Moses, but to follow our own Laws ourselves, but only in civil causes. For as far as Religion pertains, it is wholly to be sought from the Law of Christ, otherwise Christ himself would be rejected, since the gentile Laws admit many Gods, and repudiate the unknown or new [ones]. Therefore, to dispatch the matter: Christ taught the Law of faith and Charity, which was the true mind and scope of the Mosaic law, on account of which mind the Mosaic Law is called spiritual by Paul. To the carnal Law indeed, since he brought a spiritual [one], he was unwilling to subject the Gentiles, whom he taught: for flesh cannot agree with spirit, since this gives life, that one kills. But as for [the fact] that he left the political order—he so left [it] as [he left] agriculture and Medicine and the rest of the things necessary to life. And [the fact] that Paul teaches that the Magistrate is to be obeyed, he speaks no more concerning the Mosaic Magistrate than concerning the Gentile [one]: and [the fact] that the Magistrate punishes malefactors, he does not punish because Moses commanded, but because the Law of nature and equity commands, which was both before Moses and in the other nations, which Paul testifies have a Law written in their hearts. And so those who were Magistrates either before Moses, or in other Commonwealths, punished homicides, adulteries, and the other misdeeds not by the Law of Moses, but of nature: so now those who are not under Moses punish the same things by the Law of nature, not of Moses. And just as a Christian who loves God, loves [him] by the faith of Christ of his own accord, not because Moses commanded: so a Christian Magistrate who punishes malefactors, punishes [them] from love of God, not compelled by the yoke of the Law. In Christ there is neither Greek nor Jew, nor husband nor wife, nor male nor female, but a new creature: and yet there are Christians [who are] Greeks, Jews, spouses, males and females: which same I say concerning the Magistrate. The Magistrate is altogether necessary, as long as there are evil men: and those who abolish the Magistrate do just as if they should take away the rod from a householder, so that the household might sin the more licentiously: but in causes of Religion there is no other Magistrate than Christ, and those whom Christ sent armed not with iron, but with the word. Someone will ask whether it is not permitted to a Magistrate to use the political law of Moses? To which I answer: It is not only permitted, but even far better than other [laws], yet in such a way that he does not use it because Moses commanded, but because it is right. For example: Moses commanded that, when Houses are built, their roof should be fortified with some enclosure, lest anyone fall down from there. That law had some shadow and signification of a future thing, which has been fulfilled in Christ, so that it is now not necessary, if you build a house, to apply a parapet of this kind, any more than to be circumcised. Nevertheless, since that thing brings great use to life, inasmuch as it avails for avoiding accidents, if anyone in those regions where the roofs of houses are flat does it, he will do more rightly than otherwise: nor nevertheless will he be a slave of the Mosaic law: So I say concerning the Magistrate: if anyone punishes malefactors as Moses instituted, he will do far more rightly than if he follows the Laws of others: inasmuch as the Mosaic Law, passed by divine [authority], is in all ways the most just of all: nor nevertheless will he be subject to the Mosaic law: for he will not do [it] because Moses commanded, but because it is right, and always was. But I would wish this to be done without superstition, and I would prefer always to incline to the side of clemency rather than of severity, lest perhaps, by too much and inconsiderate zeal for Moses, sin be committed against the clemency of Christ. And so [the fact] that that law of Retaliation—an eye for an eye, a tooth for a tooth—is now nowhere observed, not even at Geneva, where nevertheless the Magistrate is Calvinist, does not displease me. But if I were a Magistrate and had to judge concerning that adulteress, whose life Christ preserved by marvelous prudence: or concerning the unchaste woman who anointed his feet, I would not dare to condemn her. For that saying terrifies me: "Whom God has joined, let not man separate: Whom God has purged, do not you pollute: Whom Christ has absolved, let not man condemn." The same I say concerning the Robber, who believed in Christ on the cross. And likewise concerning Onesimus the servant, for whom Paul wrote to Philemon: And concerning David, who perpetrated adultery and homicide. For it makes much difference whether pardon be given to a pious man, lapsed through ignorance or negligence and correcting himself, or to one who begins to be pious—or to an impious man. But as for what Calvin writes, that there were no Magistrates to whom the Apostles might prescribe what was right, I do not see to what it pertains: for they ought not therefore to have refrained from prescribing, because there were not yet Christian Magistrates: who, since they were writing a norm of life even for the future, ought to have had regard for the future, if they knew there would at some time be Christian Magistrates: just as Moses, when he prescribed laws for the Israelites, and forbade them to create a King, nevertheless, since he foreknew it would come to pass that they would create a king against the Law, prescribed for the future the Rights of the King. And Paul seems to do this very same thing when he writes: "Princes are not a terror to good work, but to evil. But do you wish not to fear the power? do good, and you will have praise from it: for he is God's minister to you for good: but if you do evil, fear. For not without cause does he bear the sword: for he is God's minister, an avenger in wrath to him who does evil," etc. All these words, if anyone duly weighs [them], he will catch that here the office is tacitly prescribed to the Magistrate—namely, that he should punish external misdeeds, not internal sins, Envy, Hypocrisy, Pride, which he cannot touch with his sword, although they are greater than the external [ones], nor likewise Religion. For since Paul speaks concerning the Magistrate who then was, if he permitted men to be punished on account of Religion, Paul himself would be punished first of all, who professes a Religion from which then all the Magistrates—both the Jewish, and all the rest—shrank in horror. And so [the fact] that he says: "Do you wish not to fear the power? do good," is just as if he should say: "I speak of that Magistrate who punishes the maleficent, not the beneficent." For as far as those Magistrates pertain who persecute us Christians—who do good, and are not malefactors—on account of Religion, these are not God's ministers: since God has commanded his ministers to protect the beneficent, not to attack [them]. But they are his ministers who defend the beneficent and the pious from the injury of the evil. And so they ought to punish misdeeds, but ought not to handle Religion (for they will do nothing other than pervert all things), but to leave it to us, the prelates of religion, to be handled thus, as the parent of religion, Christ, prescribed. These things Paul teaches, but a little more obscurely on account of the tyranny [of the time].
Calvin 126.
Those men confess, with whom I now dispute, that judges are divinely armed for punishing other crimes, provided they abstain from religion, (1) so that, with them keeping silent, impiety may freely run riot.
Vaticanus.
(1) Nay rather, [so] that it may be coerced by the spiritual and divinely powerful arms of Paul, with which he leads captive every thought to obedience to Christ, and has at hand vengeance against every disobedience.
Calvin 127.
But now let us hear how the holy Spirit cries out against [this] in innumerable places—who pronounces that under Christ's reign Kings will be patrons and vindicators of sound Doctrine and legitimate worship. When David exhorts all princes to kiss the son, what does he demand of them more than that they defend sound Doctrine by their protection? When he so often says that they will come to offer tribute to Christ, what else does he understand than that they will be presidents of pure adoration, and guardians of pious doctrine? How also will that prophecy of Isaiah be fulfilled—that they will be nurses of the church—unless they confer their power for protecting the state of piety?
Vaticanus.
Nothing could have been said more openly against Calvin. For Isaiah, in that place which is here cited by Calvin, speaks thus: "[You shall suck] the milk of the other nations, and you shall suck the breasts of kings." And soon: "I will bring it about that for your administration there shall be peace, and for your Magistracy, Justice. No longer [shall there be] cruelty in your land, no devastation or calamity shall be heard in your borders, and you shall call your walls salvation, and your gates Praise." And in Chapter 63 Christ speaks thus: "I have trodden the winepress alone, nor was anyone from the peoples with me: and when, perceiving that there was no helper, no supporter, I marveled, victory [came] to me by my own arm, my own wrath assisting me, and, angered, I crushed the peoples, and made [them] drunk with my wrath, their power being cast down to the earth." And in Chapter 65: "The Wolf and the Lamb shall feed together, and the Lion and the Ox shall eat straw, and the food of the serpent shall be dust; he shall not be harmed, nor shall there be corrupt dealing in all my holy mountain." And in Chapter 2: "They shall forge ploughshares from their swords, and sickles from their spears: nor shall some nations bring arms against others, nor shall they learn wars any more." These things, and other [things] infinite, are written concerning the future time of the reigning Church, which time Calvin in this place cites against himself. For if there shall be Justice for the Magistracy, the power of the nations being cast down to the earth, and all powers being abolished by Christ, and swords being converted into sickles, with what sword will the Magistrates use, that they may protect the truth, which no one will be able to attack? And, that it may be understood how far other Christ's victory will be than Calvin reckons, Christ will fight with the word alone, concerning which it is written: "The Lord made my mouth like a sharp sword." Likewise: "The Lord gave me a learned tongue." So far will it be from Princes patronizing this sharp and fiery word, that all nations will oppose themselves to it, and will be conquered, and thus, subdued, will be rendered the footstool of Christ's feet, and will serve him and his church thenceforth unto all ages, so that whatever kingdom, and whatever nation will not have served him, will be overthrown. Concerning this victory it is thus written: "The pious shall exult gloriously, rejoicing in their beds. Having the praise of God in their throat, and a two-edged sword in their hand, to take punishment from the nations, to seize penalties from the peoples, to bind their Kings with chains, and their Nobles with fetters of iron, to exercise upon them the prescribed judgment, which is for an ornament to the pious." Here you see that it will come to pass that kings will not defend the Church with iron, but will attack [it], and will be conquered by it, by the word and the spirit of the mouth of the Lord, so that they come to its feet, and lick the traces of its feet. And so [the fact] that it is written that Kings will be nurses of the church, must be taken thus: that those who before had reigned will be servants, so that they are compelled to cherish the Church not with iron, but with their service. For the state of things will be turned about, and those who reigned will serve those over whom they reigned, and those who were captives will command those whom they served. For he will depose the powerful from the seat, and will exalt the humble. The hungry he will fill with good things, and the Rich he will send away empty. Then we shall have a powerful city, to which salvation will be for walls and rampart, when the Lord will have struck the earth with the rod of his mouth, which will be that iron scepter of Christ. O you who make mention of the Lord, hasten this time, do not give intermission either to yourselves or to him, until he composes and renders Jerusalem praiseworthy on earth. Calvin (lest perhaps anyone be deceived) is so far from thinking that this time already is (although he already arms his Magistrates with that iron, the nurse of his church, as though those times had already come), that he does not even reckon it will be in this life. For, as we showed above from his Institutes, he cherishes a Church in which there are very many hypocrites and impious men. And in his Commentaries on Isaiah, writing concerning this very time of the reigning church, concerning which we speak, he says that those greatly play the fool who think these things future in this life: for we have here nothing of that felicity except a taste. And in the Epistle [prefixed] to his Catechism he writes thus: "Unless God marvelously succor from heaven, I seem to myself to see extreme barbarism. And would that our sons may not (a little after) perceive that this word was rather a prophecy than a conjecture. Wherefore we must labor the more, that we may gather by our writings whatever remnants of the church (which will remain after our death, or even emerge)." Since therefore he expects extreme barbarism, he does not here arm kings for the defense and nurture of the church because he hopes they will be such, but he arms himself with their protection (without which he sees his word to be inert and invalid), so that he may be able by iron (because by the word he cannot) to overwhelm his adversaries.
