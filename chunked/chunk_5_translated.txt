*Certain passages taken from the writings of John Calvin.*

**I**

Let us give our effort, and labor as much as we can, that we may be a golden or silver vessel. For the rest, to shatter the earthen vessels belongs to the Lord alone, to whom also the iron rod has been given: Nor let anyone claim for himself what is proper to the Son alone—as if he were sufficient to winnow the threshing-floor and to purge the chaff, and [as if] all the tares were to be separated by human judgment. That obstinacy is proud, and [it is] a sacrilegious presumption which a depraved fury arrogates to itself. *Institutes* VIII, section 20. The same [in] Cyprian, *Epistles*, Book III, [Letter] 3, page 77.

**II**

But this power of the Church which we have spoken of, if it be compared with that [power] by which now for several centuries the spiritual tyrants have peddled themselves among the people of God—[tyrants] who have falsely called themselves Bishops, and Prelates of religion—will be no better an agreement than [that of] Christ with Belial.

Nor is it here my purpose to set forth in what manner and by how unworthy means they exercised their tyranny. I will only report the Doctrine which they defend today, first by writings, then by sword and fire. *Institutes* VIII, section 146.

**III**

Will he be the vicar of Christ, who, by persecuting the Gospel with furious endeavors, openly professes himself Antichrist?

Will he be the successor of Peter, who rushes with sword and fire to demolish whatever Peter built? *Institutes* VIII, section 129.

**IIII**

Wisdom is driven from our midst while the matter is conducted by force. Calvin, Preface to the Acts of the Apostles, to the King of Denmark.

**V**

For he denies that it is episcopal to defend the faith and the right of the Church by arms. *Institutes* VIII, Section 182.

**VI**

For those whom the Lord has destined for so great an office, he first equips with those arms which are required for fulfilling [it], lest they come empty and unprepared: These are those Spiritual arms, powerful unto God for the demolition of fortifications: With which the faithful soldiers of God may demolish counsels, and every loftiness which exalts itself against the knowledge of God, and may lead captive every thought unto obedience to Christ. Behold the highest power, with which it is fitting that the pastors of the Church, by whatever name they may at last be called, be endowed. Namely, that by the word of God they may confidently dare all things: that they may compel every Power, Glory, Wisdom, [and] Height of the world to yield and to obey His majesty: that, supported by His power, they may command all from the highest even to the lowest: that they may build the house of Christ, [and] overthrow [the house] of Satan; feed the Sheep, kill the Wolves, instruct and exhort the teachable, [and] reprove, rebuke, [and] subdue the rebellious and obstinate: that they may Bind and loose: that they may finally flash like lightning if there be need, and thunder: But all things in the word of God, etc. [*Institutes*] VIII, Section 145.

**VII**

For neither is it fitting that those who have refused to comply with our admonitions, we should hand over to the Magistrate. *Institutes* VIII, § 171.

**VIII**

Since they take for granted that a universal council is the true image of the Church: This principle once assumed, they at the same time without doubt determine that such councils are governed immediately by the Holy Spirit, and therefore cannot err: and so, since they themselves (the spiritual tyrants) govern—nay rather, constitute—the councils, they in reality claim for themselves whatever they contend ought [to be granted] to councils. Therefore they wish our faith to stand and fall at their arbitrary will: so that whatever they shall have determined in either direction, may be firm and settled in our minds—so that, if they have approved anything, it is approved to us without any doubt: [and] if they have condemned anything, that too ought to be held as condemned. Meanwhile, by their own caprice, and the word of God being despised, they forge dogmas: to which they afterward demand that faith be given on this account. For [they hold] that one is not a Christian unless he assents with certainty to all their dogmas, both affirmative and negative—if not with explicit faith, yet with implicit—because it lies within the Church's [power] to establish new articles of faith. *Institutes* VIII, [section] 146.

**IX**

But this too, on the other hand, is worth the effort to see: what was once the true use of Ecclesiastical Jurisdiction, and how great an abuse crept in—so that we may know what is to be abrogated, and what is to be restored from antiquity, if we wish, the kingdom of Antichrist being overthrown, to erect again the true kingdom of Christ. Here the first aim is that scandals be met; [and] that, if any scandal has arisen, it be abolished.

In [its] use, two things are to be considered: That this spiritual power be entirely separated from the right of the sword: Next, that it be administered not by the will of one [man], but through legitimate assembly. Both were observed in the purer Church. For the holy Bishops did not exercise their power either by fines, or by prisons, or by other civil penalties, but used, as was fitting, the word of the Lord alone. For the most severe vengeance of the Church, and as it were [its] last thunderbolt, is excommunication, which is not employed except in necessity. That [vengeance], moreover, requires neither force nor hand: But is content with the power of the word of God. *Institutes* VIII, §. 172.

**X**

Although between the Apostles and their successors there is this difference, as I have said, that those were the certain and authentic amanuenses of the Holy Spirit: and therefore their writings are to be held as the oracles of God: But others have no other office than to teach what has been delivered and consigned in the sacred scriptures.

We establish, therefore, that it is not now left to the faithful to hammer out some new dogma, but that one must simply adhere to the doctrine to which the Lord has subjected all without exception. When I say this, I wish to show not only what is permitted to individual men, but also what [is permitted] to the whole Church. As far as individuals are concerned: Paul certainly had been ordained an Apostle to the Corinthians by the Lord: yet he denies that he lords it over their faith. Who now would dare to arrogate to himself a dominion which Paul testifies does not belong to him? But if he had acknowledged this license of teaching—namely, that whatever a pastor delivers, he should demand that certain faith be given to him in it—he would never have delivered this discipline to those same Corinthians: that, two or three Prophets speaking, the rest should judge; and that if something were revealed to another sitting by, the first should be silent. For thus he spared no one whose authority he would not subject to the censure of the word of God. But concerning the universal Church, someone will say, the case is different: I respond: that Paul meets this doubt elsewhere, when he says that faith is from hearing: and hearing from the word of God. That is, if faith depends on the word of God alone, if it looks to and rests upon that alone, what place is now left for the word of the whole world? Nor will anyone be able to hesitate here, whoever rightly knows what faith is. For it must be supported by that firmness with which it may stand unconquered and undaunted against Satan, and all the engines of hell, and the whole world. This firmness we shall find in nothing but the one word of God. Next, the reasoning which it is fitting to consider here is universal: that God therefore takes away from men the faculty of putting forth new dogma, in order that He alone may be to us the master in spiritual doctrine: as He alone is truthful, who can neither lie nor deceive. This reasoning pertains no less to the whole church than to each one of the faithful. *Institutes* VIII, § 145.

**XI**

Whence it follows that all human doctrines smack of their own uncleanness. For nothing is pure except what has proceeded from God. Calvin, on Isaiah, Commentary [on chapter] 6, [verse] 5.

**XII**

They have Moses and the Prophets. Luke 16:29. Even if the Law and the Prophets did not suffice, God would not deny other aids. Hence we recognize that all things which are added to the word of God are to be condemned and rejected. For the Lord willed that we depend entirely upon His word, and be content with it. If, therefore, we lend our ears to others, we do Him a great injury. Calvin, Commentary on Isaiah 8:20. Likewise Beza in the Confession of Christian faith, chapter 4, section 6.

**XIII**

But since there is no one who is not enveloped by some little cloud of ignorance, either we must leave no Church [standing], or we must pardon a delusion in those matters which can be unknown both with the sum of religion inviolate and without the loss of salvation, etc.: Meanwhile, if we strive to amend what displeases [us], we do this out of our office. And to this pertains that [saying] of Paul: If something better be revealed to one sitting by, let the first be silent. Whence it is established that the zeal for public edification has been committed to the individual members of the Church, according to the measure of his grace: provided [it be] becomingly and according to order, etc. *Institutes* VIII, section 13.

**XIIII**

Le regne de Sathan ne peut consister, ou un chacun a Franchise & liberté, de mettre en avant son opinion, touchant le faict de la religion. [EN (French): The reign of Satan cannot stand where each one has freedom and liberty to put forward his opinion concerning the matter of religion.] [In] *A contius* [i.e., Against Castellio?], Book VI, folio 215. L'empeschement de ceste liberté est satanique. [EN (French): The hindering of this liberty is satanic.] The same, Book VI, folio 216.

**XV**

Donques ce chois est presenté aux pasteurs, s'ils aiment mieux, par la liberté, souffrir toutes ces difficultés, & que la vraye cognoissance demeure en l'eglise: ou de vivre en repos, & ouvrir la fenestre a ignorance. [EN (French): Therefore this choice is presented to the pastors, whether they prefer, through liberty, to suffer all these difficulties, and that the true knowledge remain in the church: or to live at rest, and to open the window to ignorance.] The same, Book VI, folio 223.

*From the disputation on legitimate homicide, published in print and proposed for disputation in the Theological College of the Most Illustrious Lords, the States of Holland and West Friesland, in the year 1602.*

**Thesis V**

Concerning Idolaters, moreover, Heretics, and Blasphemers against God, a greater question is ventilated today: But nevertheless, because God so expressly commanded that those be removed from the midst—whoever from among the People of God shall have worshipped alien Gods, or shall have seduced the people to false Gods, or shall have blasphemously usurped the divine name—we judge that it is to be felt entirely with the Holy scriptures, whatever human reason may clamor against [it].

**VI**

But diligent caution must here be employed: For not every Idolater, or Heretic, or Blasphemer against God and His worship, is to be punished by the Magistrate with the ultimate penalty, as the Laws teach, [and] reason and usage persuade. For he who is stained with these vices, has sinned either secretly and covertly, or manifestly and openly. He who [has sinned] secretly and privately, is by no means bound to render penalties to the Magistrate, since the cognizance of these things pertains not to the human Forum (as they say), but to the President of the heavenly tribunal, who alone is the knower of hidden things, and therefore alone is Judge. But he who [has sinned] manifestly and publicly is not immediately to be punished even with death, but [is] to be dismissed alive (yet chastised at the discretion of the Magistrate, according to the circumstance of the matter): Namely, if he has not, 1st, sinned of set purpose, willingly and knowingly; 2nd, been previously accused of this crime; 3rd, [if,] admonished, heaping crime upon crime obstinately, he [has not] proceeded to walk in impious ways; 4th, attempted to disturb the peace of the Church; 5th, solicited others to the same deed.

**VII**

But that it may better be established who is to be judged an Idolater, Heretic, [or] Blasphemer worthy of death, we shall propose a definition of each: then we shall prove, by reasons sought from divine and human law [and] from the testimony of saner men, that those who are found such as the description requires, are to be killed.

**VIII**

An Idolater worthy of capital punishment is he who, openly, of set purpose, [and] with admonitions neglected, obstinately, in order to disturb the Church and seduce others, has held some fictitious [god] in place of the one true God, or, besides that one [God], has worshipped some creature, joining it to Him as it were a partner, or finally has honored indeed the one true God, but not in the legitimate manner prescribed in the sacred letters.

A Heretic is he who, separating himself from the Church of which he had previously declared himself a member by external profession, defends an error opposed to the foundations of faith and to the Prophetic and Apostolic doctrine delivered in the scriptures—concerning which, however, [though] often and duly admonished and convicted from the word of God, both publicly and privately, with admonitions despised, he will not desist, nor acknowledge the truth revealed in the writings of the Apostles and Prophets: but rather, resisting it with unconquered and stubborn impiety, strives to tear apart the peace and concord of the Church, and, opening publicly and privately the [whole] game of his perfidy, [strives] to bring into the church of God the faction and gangrene of [his] pestiferous dogma, by his writings and sermons, as much as in him lies, or to foster [it once it has been] brought in by others.

A Blasphemer is he who, publicly, with deliberate mind, [and] with admonitions stubbornly neglected, either with a slanderous tongue contumeliously blurts out something unworthy concerning the true God—whether by attributing to Him what does not belong to Him, or by taking away what is proper to Him, or by transferring to a creature what belongs to God alone—or, by an impious deed, hinders, tramples upon, [the] true divine worship, or attempts to hinder and trample [upon it].

**X**

Whoever therefore shall have been caught guilty of any of these crimes, in the manner we have stated, that he is to be punished with death by the Magistrates, we demonstrate by these reasons. 1. Because the voice of God commanded, *whoever sacrifices to Gods, save to the Lord alone, shall be cut off*. Elsewhere: *The Prophet who shall have presumed to speak a word in my name, which I did not command [him] to speak, or who speaks in the name of alien Gods, such a Prophet shall die.* 2. Again, *he who shall have cursed his God, shall bear his sin; he who shall have blasphemed the name of the Lord, let him be punished with death; the whole assembly shall utterly overwhelm him with stones, whether he be a native or a sojourner.* 3. Because those who killed them are praised by God in the Holy Scripture. Examples are Moses, 4. Asa, 5. Josiah, [6.] Jehoshaphat, 7. Hezekiah. 8. Nay, even when the Magistrate did not do his office in destroying false Prophets, God roused up Heroes by His spirit, who discharged the office of Princes in assailing them, as is to be read in the History of Jehu and Elijah. III. And lest anyone calumniate [that] this was the custom only among the Israelite people under the law, but was not practiced by pious men after the coming of Christ, let him diligently weigh the deed of Paul handing over Hymenaeus and Alexander to the devil, and blinding Elymas the Magician: and of Peter killing Ananias and Sapphira. But if Peter justly punished such [persons] with death, [and] Paul with blinding and handing over to Satan (which all pious [men] feel, and rightly believe)—though the sword had not been handed to them—what shall we say of the Magistrate, who has received the sword from God for this [purpose], that he may chastise those straying from the law of God, if he has not punished those who are openly factious, disturbers of the Church, desperate Apostates, and publicly seducing many away from God to the figments of their own brain? For although by [its] penalty it cannot blot out the opinion of the mind, nevertheless it ought by no means to tolerate a pestilent and impious profession.

**X**

IV. To these add the pious Princes of the primitive Church: Constantine wished not only the Arians and Idolaters to be killed, but also their books to be burned, and the prefects who had neglected to do this to be beheaded: The same the Emperors Theodosius, Valentinian, Gratian, Martinian, Honorius, [and] Justinian commanded. V. Nay, even those also who neither knew the true God, nor had heard anything of the legitimate manner of worshipping Him, judged—by the instinct of nature alone—that those were to be killed who changed anything in the publicly received religion: Witness to us is the death of Theodorus the Atheist, of Protagoras of Abdera, and of the great Socrates, whom, when Melitus accused [him] of Impiety, he framed the indictment with these words: Ἀδικεῖ Σωκράτης, οὓς ἡ πόλις νομίζει θεοὺς οὐ νομίζων, ἕτερα δὲ δαιμόνια καινὰ εἰσηγούμενος. [EN (Greek): Socrates does wrong, in not acknowledging the gods which the city acknowledges, and in introducing other new divinities.] The Athenians also, that they might judge how greatly it pertained to the safety of the faith that the mockers of the Gods be removed: enacted that for anyone who had accused someone of Impiety, unless he had carried through [the conviction of] the accused, this should be capital [for the accuser]. 4.

**XI**

VI. Finally there is added a reason, [namely, that what has been] said... [is] more grave or more light according to the dignity of the object: And therefore, since the Idolater, Heretic, [and] Blasphemer, such as we have described, sin primarily against God, the most excellent object: then against the Church whose peace they disturb, and [since] their crime is public, done to the injury of all, as the laws assert: then both on account of the dignity of the object and on account of the gravity of the crime, reason persuades that they are by no means to be spared—so that at least a temporal penalty may seize him whom Spiritual discipline has not corrected: lest, from the neglect of punishing such [persons], other men, who are by nature prone to evil, grow accustomed to the same offenses and impious acts. For where there is greater pardon, there sprouts among them a greater abundance of offenses.

*From the annotations on the second [Epistle] to the Corinthians. Chapter 10.*

For the arms of our warfare are not carnal, but [are] powerful through the divine [power]. Paul's warfare was to assail and dislodge the thoughts of men: that is, to speak and teach with such power that the minds of men, conquered by the truth of the matter, were compelled to think thus: He speaks true things, and [things] which cannot be refuted. For that spiritual battle he used weapons not carnal but spiritual; for just as he would be mad who should wish to conquer a sword or a missile by thought, so he would be mad who should wish to conquer a thought by the sword. And yet the power of the Christian herald is placed in conquering thought, from which it follows that he cannot use any weapons except those by which the thoughts of men can be conquered. We have an example in Nero: He, traversing Greece, put on and undertook contests of music himself: if he conquered other musicians, he was crowned: this was a legitimate contest: but if he was conquered by them, he saw to it that they were killed: this, however, was not to conquer in music, but to kill a man: By which absurd iniquity that [result] followed—that, by the feigned voice of men, he was praised as though [he were] a victor in [their] presence: and the rest of the Musicians thenceforth deliberately sang badly, lest they offend him; the thoughts of men indeed (although he was the most powerful prince of all) he did not conquer—nay, they held him then for a madman, and we hold [him so] now. But the rest of the musicians, who surpassed him in skill of the art, conquered the thought not only of others, but even of Nero himself, so that his mind, although unwilling, spoke thus: these sing better than I. Similarly it happens also in Religion. There is disputation about many matters: if we conquer someone by words, we are crowned: and this is a legitimate contest: If someone cannot be conquered by us, he is called a heretic and is killed: This, however, is not to conquer a heretic, but to kill a Man: by which absurd iniquity we obtain that [result]—that in [their] presence we are pronounced victors by the feigned voice of men, and the rest thenceforth speak less well than they could, lest they perish. The thoughts of Men indeed (although we are powerful) we do not conquer—nay, those who are not blind hold us, and afterward will hold us, for madmen, and thus it is judged concerning us, as we now judge concerning Nero and the rest of the persecutors of Christians. For nothing is so covered that it shall not be uncovered. God promised to the obedient so great a power that one [might] put a hundred, and a hundred ten thousand, to flight. Thus fought Paul, who alone could, with those divine arms of his, dislodge not a hundred, but the thoughts of all men.

But now we, [a] hundred [of us], kill individuals one by one, and defend Christ with carnal arms, so that it is understood that we lack spiritual arms, and that, on the other hand, we hold a carnal Redeemer Christ. For if the Apostles, who planted the church for us, were content with spiritual arms, it appears that those who employ carnal [ones] do not sufficiently trust the spiritual [ones]: Which nevertheless they would trust, if they had them—being indeed omnipotent, as will appear at the coming of the Lord, who will vanquish Antichrist, that is, the supreme enemy of Christ, with no other arms than the spirit of His mouth. It is written: That the Tabernacle was made from the gifts of the people freely offered, no one being compelled. Which same [thing] is to be understood concerning the church, as is plain in Psalm 110. In which the forces of Christ are said to be voluntary. And likewise in the law, in which it is commanded that the timid depart, lest in the battle-line they soften the courage of the rest. Thus must the war of Christ be waged—by soldiers [who are] voluntary, but compelled by the word of God alone: otherwise those who serve as soldiers, serve another than Christ. And so the outcome and our morals show what is the fruit of compelled soldiery. THE END.

*From the Book of the defense of Sebastian Castellio against Theodore Beza.*

Title 3. Concerning the word Heretic he writes these words: A certain [translator], to whom he who is called a Heretic [is] whoever is obstinate, renders *Heretikon* [as] "the obstinate one": Which passage I had already corrected thus, "A sectarian man": And in the margin I had written these words: "Addicted to a vicious sect." But whom he himself may call a Heretic, he bids [us] seek from Acts 5:17 [recte: Titus 3:10]. Where he defines a Heretic with these words: He is called a Heretic who so strays from the received doctrine of the Church that, the judgment of God and of His church being despised, he remains in [his] opinion and, disciples having been gathered, violates the concord of the church.

There are many causes why I cannot approve this definition. First, because it is composite, when it ought to be of a simple thing: For it comprises first the dissenter, then the contumacious [one], thirdly the pertinacious [one], fourthly the master, fifthly the factious [one]. To define which five things, just as many definitions would need to be employed. Next, [I object] that he says, "from the received doctrine": more rightly (in my judgment) he would say, "from sound doctrine." For what if the church itself should receive a heresy (which the church of the Jews had done in Christ's time, and would that the church of the Christians had never afterward done [the same])—will it be a crime to disagree? Thirdly, because he constitutes only the masters [as] heretics: He excludes the Disciples: For he says, "disciples having been gathered." What then [of] the disciples themselves? will they not be heretics? Will Nicolaus alone, then, be a Heretic? [And] not likewise the Nicolaitans? Now why does he join pertinacity to his definition, which in my first translation he not undeservedly repudiates? For someone can be a heretic, and yet not pertinacious: just as Paul, before he had come to know Christ, was a heretic, namely a Pharisee, and yet did not remain in [his] error. I could mention more faults of his definition: but these are easy to perceive from those [things] which I have said: which indeed I have adduced for a grave cause.

For he has also deduced his disputation from a similar definition of a heretic, in the Booklet which he composed, on heretics being punished by the civil Magistrate: From which it is easy to conjecture how true his opinion on so great a matter is to be believed [to be], which sets out from so false a definition. Go now, [you] Princes, and hasten to shed blood according to his Doctrine.

*The true definition of a Heretic.*

Indeed, a heretic properly is he who is from some heresy, that is, [some] Sect: and he is so called by the same form [as] an Academic, a Peripatetic, a Platonist. But just as *valetudo* [is used] for ill health, and in the sacred letters *Gentes* ["the nations"] [is used] for profane nations (whence the name *Ethnici* ["heathens"] arose): So also *heresy* is used by Paul for a vicious sect: Whence he derived the name *heretic*, by which he indicated him who follows a vicious sect, whether he be pertinacious, or be not: whether admonished, or otherwise: whether he has gathered Disciples, or is himself a disciple.

But that Paul bids a heretic be avoided, he bids [it] not on account of heresy, but on account of pertinacity in heresy: which he judges when he says, "After one or a second admonition."

And for this cause I had at the outset translated *heretic* [as] "pertinacious," being more intent upon Paul's opinion concerning the pertinacious heretic than upon the force of the word, which I afterward discussed more diligently.

Therefore the Apostle Paul bids a heretic not be avoided, especially [by] Titus, that is, [by] a master: but rather be admonished and taught. But if afterward he should find [him] pertinacious, let him avoid [him]: just as he ought to avoid a drunkard, a Blasphemer, and other [men given over] to disgraceful deeds, if they do not comply with admonitions. This I will add—that Paul calls that pertinacious Heretic αὐτοκατάκριτον [autokatakriton], that is, "condemned by himself," [and] that this is not (in my judgment, at least) to be taken as though he, knowing and willing, condemns himself and sins against [his] conscience, as certain [men] teach, who say that heretics sin maliciously and obstinately—although they deny [it], [being] pertinacious even unto death. For if they deny [it] even unto death, who could attest this to you after their death? Have you penetrated into their hearts (which belongs to God alone)? But this expression seems to me similar to that: "By the very [act] by which you condemn another, you condemn yourself," that is, you show [yourself] worthy of condemnation. For they do not knowing and willing condemn themselves, who condemn others.

Likewise that [saying]: "Happy [is] he who does not condemn himself in that which he approves," that is, who in that of which he has knowledge, and which he approves, so conducts himself that he commits nothing on account of which he is to be condemned. But that [other saying is] by far the most plain: "Because you reject the divine doctrine, and judge yourselves not worthy of everlasting life, know that we turn to the gentiles." For the Jews certainly did not knowingly judge themselves unworthy of everlasting life; But they so conducted themselves that they showed themselves unworthy of it. So in this place I judge that that pertinacious Heretic is called αὐτοκατάκριτον [autokatakriton] by Paul for this reason: because he does those things by which he offers himself worthy of condemnation. For indeed heretics often believe an error—God sending them (because they did not admit the love of truth) so great a force of error that they believe (he says) (let this word be marked) a lie.

*Concerning the sons of the flesh and the sons of the Spirit.*

Just as then he who had been born according to the flesh persecuted him who had been born according to the spirit: So also now. But what does the Scripture say? Cast out the Handmaid and her Son: For the son of the Handmaid shall not be heir with the son of the Free [woman]. Galatians 4.

Weigh these words of Paul, Reader. Ishmael, born according to the flesh, persecuted Isaac, born according to the spirit. So also now the carnal persecute the spiritual. Nor can it be otherwise. For as Isaac, because he was younger, and therefore weaker, could not persecute Ishmael: so now Christians, because they are born after the Antichristians and are weaker, cannot persecute the Antichristians: and this very [thing] has been signified by many other examples: For neither did Abel persecute Cain, nor Noah the Giants, nor Lot the Sodomites, nor David Saul, nor the Prophets the false prophets, nor Christ or the Apostles the Scribes and Pharisees—but the contrary. But if some could do [so], yet they did not do [it], as David: who, when Saul persecuted him through the mountains like a flea or a partridge, David, having found him once and again destitute of protection, could have killed [him], but would not. So today the pious are like a certain flea, whom the impious persecute. But if at any time it happens that they can harm the impious, they are so far from harming [them] that they even profit [them], and are zealous for their salvation: For this they have learned from their own David: just as those [others] learned to harm them from Saul their father, from whom they received benefits. You have here the signs and marks of the pious and the impious. The impious are the noble, the Rich, the lettered, the powerful, the mockers, the persecutors, the cruel, the proud, the renowned, and the ambitious: Like Cain, the Giants, the Sodomites, Saul, the Egyptian Magi, the false prophets, the Scribes, the Pharisees, Herod, Pilate, Annas, Caiaphas, the Philosophers. The pious are the ignoble, the poor, the unskilled, the weak, the mocked, the harassed, the peaceable, the meek, the humble, the submissive, the obscure, the lowly, the despised, the infamous, the offscourings and shavings of the world, and contemners of their own honor, and zealous for the honor of God, who render good for evil, [and,] assailed with reproaches, bless: [who] finally follow the lamb Christ through all crosses and virtues.

Now set before yourself, Christian Reader, such a little people before [your] eyes, and consider whether it can happen that a little people of this kind, dispersed throughout the whole world, should persecute the impious, that is, the Giants and the Lords of the world conspiring among themselves. But if it cannot happen (as certainly it cannot), do not doubt that those who persecute others on account of faith are Ishmaelites, since they imitate the nature of Ishmael. But what does the Scripture say? Cast out the handmaid and her son; For the son of the handmaid shall not be heir with the son of the Free [woman]. What is the handmaid? It is the Law, by Paul's own interpretation. The sons of the handmaid are those who wish to be under the Law, that is, under the Letter; and therefore they persecute those who wish to live under the spirit, as Ishmael [persecuted] Isaac. And so, that it may be understood that they are true Ishmaelites, they cite the letter of the Law, which bids false Prophets be killed. But the false prophets they interpret [as] Heretics: and Heretics they interpret [as] those who dissent from themselves: In which they are the sons of those who said: We have a Law, and according to the Law he ought to die, because he made himself the son of God. You see how the sons of the Law persecute the sons of the spirit, and cite the Law for that purpose. But what should Sarah do, that is, the Free and spiritual Church? She herself does not cast out the son of the handmaid, that is, the servants of the Letter: Nor does Isaac likewise cast out Ishmael, for he cannot—that is, the pious do not cast out the impious: for they cannot: but Sarah asks her husband, that is, the Church [asks] God, to cast out that persecuting Law or letter, and its persecuting sons, lest they be heirs of the heavenly kingdom. And so it is not [the part] of the Church to cast out the impious from the heavenly inheritance: but this belongs to the husband Christ, who will cast them out with the rod of His mouth, when He will say: Go, [you] cursed, into eternal fire. To pronounce this sentence is not [the part] of Sarah, nor of Isaac, but of Christ. Therefore let them see what they do, who condemn heretics: for heretics are not wont to be condemned as Robbers [are]: For concerning robbers even those who hang them often hope well, and pray to God for them, nor think them condemned to Gehenna. But those who afflict heretics with punishment judge them to be utterly rejected by God and consigned to eternal fires: Which while they judge, they sit plainly on the tribunal of Christ, and pronounce that sentence: Go, [you] cursed, into eternal fire. Which can be pronounced by no other than Christ, nor before the day of Judgment. For it is therefore called the day of Judgment, because then, [and] not before, it can be judged who are the elect or the reprobate: As Paul taught, when he said: Judge nothing before the time, until the Lord come, who will illuminate the hidden things of darkness, and lay open the counsels of hearts. Which words, while I weigh [them] with myself, I have nothing to add, except that, while the matter is one of condemning someone as a heretic, that is, as consigned to everlasting punishments, and rejected by God unto eternity (for such are heretics thought [to be]), I admonish all that they consider seven times what they do; And here I will again subscribe the same sentence in larger Letters (so that it may be read at a glance).

*From the epistle of Castellio to Nicolaus of Blasdiken.*

Since there is everywhere such savagery against the Anabaptists in your parts, I grieve, and I wish the persecutors a sound mind—not because I favor the errors of the Anabaptists, but because the persecutors themselves labor under errors no less grave, I believe, and under the gravest crimes. And even if neither were [the case], and the rest were absent, this [one] error, and this [one] crime, would be graver than any error of the Anabaptists whatsoever: that they kill men on account of religion, and think [they] ought to be killed. For altogether this spirit of persecution is alien from the spirit of Christ; and those who are impelled by it do not know of what spirit they are. And you, brother, beware—by God, I beg you, beware—lest you, even unwitting, contaminate yourself with that fellowship of persecution. I do not speak of your opinion, which I am persuaded abhors these persecutions, but of the outcome, which follows your disputations with the Anabaptists, even against your will. For I have heard that they, summoned by you to gratify the prince, are refuted. Then, when [though] refuted they nevertheless persisted in [their] opinion, [they are] punished. If it is so, I would prefer you to be occupied in chastising and refuting the very errors and sins of the Princes, with which you are not ignorant that they teem, and that you persuade them of this first of all, that men are not to be killed on account of religion. Thus at last it might be permitted to dispute with no peril—I will not say [to the] Princes—of the Anabaptists. Now, while, the princes being infected with that error, you refute the Anabaptists, and that [at] the bidding of the Princes themselves, it comes about that those whom you willingly refute, you unwillingly harm, nor do you profit the Princes themselves at all. For they are not in fact rendered better by your disputation about anyone's error, but only [rendered] more confirmed in their unjust deed (not to say anything harsher). It would be holier, and more acceptable to God, to give effort to refuting the Princes at your own peril, than, disputing at their [peril], to augment the misery of the wretched. Nor indeed do I deny that errors are to be refuted, but I do not judge that they are to be refuted thus: this [is what] you do not doubt is said by me kindly and in a friendly way. Both their misery and your peril move me. You know what you have often said, while I was listening: "Lead us not into temptation." Satan is crafty, and little by little draws whomever he can into the fellowship of blood, from which we ought to be so far distant that we should shrink [even] from a garment polluted with blood. Finally, that we may know what is [the part] of our office, let us think thus: What would Jesus do if He were here? And we shall straightway hear the answer in our minds. Concerning France we have heard nothing but utter madnesses. The report is that Rouen has been captured, and that very many fell on both sides, and that, the city being stormed, [the victors] so raged against the townsmen that mothers, to escape the hands of the enemy, plunged themselves and their own children into the river. In short, nothing more wretched than all France can be imagined. Woe to the authors, woe to the bloodthirsty and the instigators of Princes. These are the fruits of the Doctrine concerning persecuting heretics. They loved blood, and (O just heavenly Judge) you gave them blood. But I will make an end: For I accomplish nothing, [but] only pour out my most just grief into the bosom of [my] most faithful and best friend. Farewell.

*Sebastian Castellio to Edward the Sixth, most renowned King of England, Greeting.*

When I was bringing into the light the books of the sacred letters, translated by me into the Latin tongue, most serene King of England, [my] friends advised me to dedicate them to your Majesty: And that I should be willing to do this, they persuaded [me] by three reasons. First, because they said that the sacred letters could be offered to no one more fittingly than to him whose kingdom was an asylum to those who were harassed on account of the study and defense of the sacred letters. Next, because you had recently entrusted this same task of translating the sacred books to learned men, but had been hindered by the death of one [of them]. Lastly, because, besides the other disciplines and tongues, you were studious also of Latinity especially (of which we have here had some regard), and for that matter had a learned master. By these causes I was led to dare to offer my night-labors to you, a most illustrious King—I, a man of the lowest condition: hoping that it would come to pass that, the higher the summit of dignity in which God's kindness had placed you, the more clemently you would admit the lowly, for the love of CHRIST, who, though He was equal with God, did not hesitate to lower Himself to the lowest. But if it pleases [you], and if you do not refuse to read this translation, King, I will now set forth to you what was my purpose in this business. I gave effort that this translation should be faithful, and Latin, and clear, as far as it could be done: Lest the obscurity of the discourse, or [its] roughness, or even the unfaithfulness of the interpretation, should thenceforth call [anyone] back from the reading of these books. But I reckoned clarity and fidelity the chief consideration. For as far as Latinity is concerned, discourse is nothing other than a certain garment, as it were, of the matter, and we are tailors. The matter indeed remains the same, nor is it made better by the elegance of the discourse, nor worse by [its] meanness: Nor does he truly love the matter, whom an uncultivated discourse draws back from coming to know it. Nay, we even see that the mysteries of the Gospel have been delivered to us in unpolished words, taken from the midst of the unlearned common people, lest anything thence should be attributed to human eloquence. But since, for [one] occupied in this study, it was necessary to weigh not only the words, but also the matters themselves (without which the words often cannot be understood): if meanwhile I noticed anything rather obscure, pertaining not only to the words but also to the matter, I attempted to illustrate it in a few [words]. But in declaring the words indeed, I was somewhat more confident. But that I touched upon the matters more sparingly, the cause was partly my present purpose of translating, which is concerned with words: Partly, and indeed much more, my own ignorance. For since these books are written about divine matters, it is necessary that we, the more we are men, have the less understanding of these [things]. And truly, if we wish to confess the truth, our age is still sunk in the deep darkness of ignorance: of which thing the most certain testimony is [our] dissensions—so grave, so pertinacious, so pernicious: so many, and likewise fruitless, assemblies concerning these controversies, and so great a number of books daily being born, and of [books] disagreeing among themselves by the whole heaven.

For if there is one Spirit of God, and one truth: it is necessary that those in whom the same spirit and the same truth reside, be one, and feel the same in spiritual matters: and if the day of the truth had risen most clearly for us, we should never kindle so many obscure lanterns of books. And indeed, to me seeking the cause of this ignorance, it seemed difficult to find. For this cannot be attributed to ignorance of letters and arts, since nothing can be done more erudite than [in] our age: and yet, with such great studies, with such great knowledge of tongues, with so many arts, through so many years, so far is it [from being the case] that much progress has been made, that matters go from day to day into the worse. And so, when I considered this matter more attentively, I seemed to myself to have found the one and truest cause of this ignorance—viciousness and impiety. And so the angel says to Daniel: "The impious shall act impiously, nor shall any of the impious understand." And David, on the contrary: "[His] secret is laid open to those who fear [Him]." And Isaiah: "Bind up the oracle, seal the doctrine, among my disciples." But if we wish to know the secrets hidden in these books, Jehovah is to be feared and worshipped by us, and to be obeyed, as befits His disciples. This [is] the true way to the knowledge of divine things, this [is] the one key to opening this seal of the divine secrets: which [key] whoever has, will require no other: whoever has [it] not, will in vain apply the others to this lock. But if there were in us a true love and fear of God, and if we placed as much zeal and labor in it as others place in acquiring money, others in letters, others in disciplines, others in honor, others in another matter—not only would this gross ignorance be absent (let me be permitted to say what I feel—this gross [ignorance], I say, which holds the age): But the earth would also be full of so great a knowledge of Jehovah, that is, of true piety, as has been promised to us, if we are wise.

And thus would be born that truly golden age, in which all would be divinely taught: And there would come to pass what the arms-mighty Jehovah promised in these words: "THEY shall hammer ploughshares from their swords, and sickles from their spears: nor shall some nations bring arms against others, nor any longer learn wars, and they shall dwell each under his own vines and fig-trees, with none to terrify [them], since the mouth of arms-mighty Jehovah speaks." And that [other]: "The wolf shall turn about with the lamb, and the leopard shall lie down with the kid, and a little boy shall lead the calf and the lion and the bull coupled together, and the cow and the bear shall feed together, their young lying down together: and the lion shall feed on chaff like cattle, and the suckling infant shall play over the hole of the asp, and the weaned boy shall wave his hand over the cavern of the viper. Nowhere shall [anything] be done ill or wickedly in my holy mountain: Because the earth shall be full of so great a knowledge of Jehovah, as the sea is covered with waters." Likewise that [word] spoken to Jerusalem, that is, to the Commonwealth of Christians: "TO you shall humbly betake themselves the men who afflicted you, and all your revilers shall venerate the traces of your feet: and they shall call you the city of Jehovah, Zion of the Augustus of the Israelites. In place of [your] being deserted, hated, and inaccessible, I will make of you an eternal sublimity, a perennial delight: so that you shall suck the milk of other nations, and the breasts of kings, and you shall know that I am Jehovah your savior, and your avenger, the deity of the [sons] of Jacob. For bronze I will bring in gold, for iron I will bring in silver, for wood bronze: and I will bring [it] to pass that for your administration there be peace, and for Magistrates justice. No longer [shall] cruelty [be] in your land, nor devastation or calamity be heard in your borders: And you shall call your walls salvation, and your gates praise. No longer shall the Sun by day furnish you light, nor shall the Moon's splendor shine [for you]: But Jehovah shall be to you an eternal light, and your God [your] glory. No more shall your Sun set, nor shall your moon wane: For Jehovah shall be to you an eternal light, the day of your mourning ended. But your people, all righteous, shall possess the earth forever, the stock sown by me, the work of my hands, that it may be to me for an honor. The least [shall grow] into a [great one], the smallest shall grow into a populous nation: Which I, Jehovah, will hasten in its [own] time." Likewise that [word]: "I WILL summon you from the nations, and, gathered from all lands, I will lead [you] into your fatherland: And, having sprinkled [you] with pure Water, [so] that you may be cleansed from all your foulnesses, I will expiate you from all your dunghill gods: And I will give you both a new heart, and a new spirit within [your] breasts: And, the stony heart being removed from your bodies, I will give you a heart of flesh: and, my spirit being given into your bodies, I will bring [it] to pass that you obey my institutions, and keep my judgments: By doing which, you shall remain in the land which I gave to your ancestors: and you [shall be] a people to me, and I will be a God to you, and I will deliver you from all your impurities."

This is, in these and many other places, and most of all in the Apocalypse, the felicity promised to Christians, O King, and the perfect knowledge of God, and obedience: Which indeed, the more I consider [it] in studying the sacred books, the less hitherto fulfilled I see [it]—however you may understand those oracles. But there arise the voices of the cowardly [and] the diffident, who hold [a man] for mad, if anyone dare to hope and trust for better [things] than ours [now are]: As if forsooth God's hand were either shortened, or [His] ear dulled: Or, as if [we] therefore ought to despair, because it has never been thus: And not rather [as if we] ought therefore to hope, because it has not been [thus]: and [as if] He has [not] promised, who neither ever lies, and can even restore life to the dead. For my part, either it must be confessed that these things are to come, or [that they] have already been, or God must be accused of lying. But if anyone shall say [they] have been, I shall ask of him, when they were. If he shall say, In the time of the Apostles: I shall ask why the knowledge of God and piety—which had been promised [as] both eternal and more abundant than the waves of the sea—was not on all sides perfect, and grew obsolete so quickly. But if he shall say, In our time: We shall marvel at this peace, which hammers swords from our ploughshares and sickles, and turns [our] furniture and houses into cannons and ramparts. But this peace is, I believe, among the nobles of letters, and the Masters of the people. Whence, then, [come] the wars of tongues and pens, far more harmful than [those] of iron? Whence so many, so grave controversies, which neither in so many ages now, [nor] by so many disputations, could be settled, and almost burst forth into the blood of the weaker? While there is no one who doubts of his own judgment: There is no one who does not condemn others. We envy, we slander: Not only do we weigh evil against evil, but often [we weigh] good against evil: And if anyone disagrees with us in even a point of religion, we condemn him, and assail [him] through all the corners of the earth with the javelin of tongue and pen, and rage [against him] with iron and flame and waters, and remove [men who are] by the nature of things defenseless and helpless: and we say that it is not permitted to us to kill anyone: and yet we hand [him] over to Pilate, [and] if he should release this [man], we deny [that Pilate] is a friend of Caesar: and—what is the most unworthy of all—we cry out that we do all these things by the zeal, and at the command, and in the name of CHRIST, and we cover the ferocity of the wolf with a lamb's skin. O Age! Forsooth, by zeal for CHRIST we will be Bloodthirsty—[we], for [the sake of] whom, lest the blood of others should have to be shed, He shed His own. By zeal for CHRIST we will root out tares—[we], for [the sake of] whom, lest the grain should be rooted out, He bade the tares be left until the harvest. By zeal for CHRIST we will persecute others—[we], whom He bade, if our right cheek be struck, to turn the left. By zeal for CHRIST we will inflict evil on others—[we], whom He instructed to render good for evil. Where, then, will be for us that charity so greatly commended [to us], without which, though you have the tongues both of men and of angels, and know all things, and by faith move even mountains, and bestow all your [goods], and even hand over your own body to be burned, you are nothing? But if anyone boasts of charity, let him consider whether he has the fruits of charity, and performs those [things] which Paul delivers [to be] of charity. Charity is kind, he says: Charity is not rash, is not puffed up, does not behave disgracefully, is not zealous for itself, is not irritable, thinks no evil, does not rejoice in injustice, but rejoices in the truth: bears all things, believes all things, hopes all things, endures all things: Charity never fails. Now indeed there remain faith, hope, [and] charity: but the greatest of these three is charity, and in it is placed the fulfilling of the law. Finally, charity covers a multitude of sins, and heals them. But if today it has so grown cold that in its place hatred reigns—and yet God has promised that pious and just age which I commemorated above—it must be confessed altogether that many things promised in the oracles are not yet fulfilled. The ignorance of which thing is the cause why we make the gifts of God exceedingly jejune, meager, and lean: and interpret most of the oracles of the prophets ineptly, not to say ridiculously: while we attempt to declare even without the spirit those things which are to come, and which can be understood only by those who are taught either divinely or by the outcome of the matter—fearing, forsooth, lest we seem to be ignorant of anything. Since these things are so, O King, and since our age still gropes in so great a fog of ignorance, I feel that we must again and again take care lest we commit anything imprudently. But if there are any controversies in the business of religion (and there are very many), in these I judge that the deed of Judas Maccabaeus and his [men] is to be followed, who, when they had not [a means] to determine anything concerning the altar of solid sacrifice, placed its stones on the mount of the temple, in a suitable place, until some prophet should come, who would render an oracle concerning them. Or rather [the deed] of Moses, to whom it had been just now commanded in express words, that if anyone had committed [a deed] against the law of set purpose, he should pay the penalty with his head: and yet [Moses] would not kill him who had gathered wood on the day of the Sabbath, except after an oracle had been reported by name concerning that matter. And Moses was a faithful minister of God, and not sparingly endowed with His spirit. [To say] nothing meanwhile of the counsel of Gamaliel, who showed that, if that matter were from men, it would be dissolved: but if it is from God, it cannot be dissolved, lest perchance one fight against God. But if we think these matters are more certain to us than that [matter] was to Moses (since the times are such that no one thinks himself to err)—yet certainly we ought not be both accusers and judges, and rather Paul is to be obeyed, who instructs thus: "If anyone is of weak faith, help [him] with no doubting of mind. Who are you, who condemn another's servant? To his own Lord he stands or falls. And he shall stand: for God is able to establish him. But why do you condemn your brother? or you, why do you despise your brother? Each of us shall plead his cause for himself before God. Wherefore let us not now condemn one another. For if we condemn, we shall be condemned, and the same measure shall be meted back to us."

It would be better (in my judgment, at least) to follow the Roman law concerning *vindiciae*, which was of this kind. If anyone who had hitherto borne himself as free was summoned into court by someone who asserted him to be a slave, meanwhile while the case was pending, the *vindiciae* were granted according to liberty: That is, he whose liberty was [put] in peril remained in the same condition as the free, until it had been made plain in court, and pronounced, whether he was a slave or free. And rightly. For since [the matter] of his condition was [in] doubt, if it perchance happened that he was free, and meanwhile, the suit pending, he had been treated as a slave, a grave injury would have been done to a free man. But how much more just is it that this be done in a capital case? Especially in religion, in which there is sinning most gravely.

Let us await the sentence of the just judge, and give effort, not that we ourselves condemn others, but that we commit nothing on account of which condemnation must be feared by us. Let us obey the just judge, and leave the tares until the harvest, lest perchance (while we wish to be wise above [our] master) we root out the grain. For not yet is the final end of the world: Nor are we angels, to whom this province has been committed. Add that it is absurd that a spiritual war be waged with earthly arms. The enemies of Christians are vices, against which one must contend with virtues, and contrary evils [must be] cured by contrary remedies: that doctrine drive out ignorance, that patience conquer injury, that modesty resist pride, that diligence be opposed to sloth, that clemency fight against cruelty, that a sincere [mind]—and a religious mind approving itself to God, and a pure spirit, and [a spirit] which is zealous to please God alone—lay low dissimulation. These are the true and truly victorious arms of the Christian religion: not that the province of a teacher be entrusted to an executioner, and that the outer [parts] of the cup be purged before the inner [parts]. And I wish these [things] to be meditated by me concerning religion only. For as to what pertains to crimes—homicides, adulteries, thefts, false testimonies, and the rest of this kind, which God both commanded to be punished, and prescribed in what way they were to be punished—these are not called into controversy. Neither did God command obscurely concerning these, and they pertain to the defense of the good: unless we wish to be slaughtered in our [own] beds, as indeed the times now are. Nor truly is there [any] peril, that if the Magistrate, constituted by God to defend the good, should put to the cross someone convicted of homicide, he would kill a good man: nor did anyone ever defend homicide—not even a homicide [himself]. But [the matter] of religion and of the understanding of the sacred letters is a far different case. For since these [things] are delivered obscurely, and often through enigmas, and there is now disputing about them for more than a thousand years, nor could the matter yet be settled, nor can [it] be settled in the absence of Charity, the arbitress of all controversies and the put-to-flight of ignorance: and [since] for this cause the earth has been filled with innocent blood: we ought truly (although we seem to ourselves to know all things)—we ought, I say, to fear, lest among the robbers (whom we deservedly crucify) we also crucify CHRIST undeservedly. And if we suffer not only Turks and Jews (of whom the former love CHRIST little, the latter even hate [Him] capitally) to live among us, but even endure the slanderous, the proud, the envious, the avaricious, the unchaste, the drunken, and the rest of the disgraceful deeds of men, and live, feast, [and] rejoice with them—we ought at least to grant this common air and breath to those who profess the same NAME of CHRIST with us, and harm no one: And are of such a mind that they would rather die than say or do anything other than what they judge ought to be said or done. Not to say, meanwhile, that no kind of men is less to be feared. For he who prefers to lose [his] life than to say what he does not feel (for he would sin, if he did [so]: and he who compels [him], compels [him] to sin)—is not, I believe, to be feared, lest he can be corrupted by money or by another thing.

And for my part I am persuaded of this: that there are none more obedient to princes and magistrates than those who simply fear God, and who, in that which they know, show themselves faithful. The obedience of the rest indeed is feigned, nor does it last longer than [either] it is compelled by fear or enticed by advantage. But he who is impelled to obey by conscience, and who, God teaching, has learned that magistrates and powers are to be obeyed—even unjust [ones], much more just [ones]—his obedience must be true and eternal, since God (who is the cause of that obedience to him) remains true and eternal. That [point] indeed (to return to [our] purpose) is true beyond controversy: He hastens to repent, who judges quickly: And it has repented many to have judged, [but] it has not repented [any] to have suspended judgment. And he who is more inclined to clemency than to anger, imitates the nature of God: Who, although He knows us to be guilty, nevertheless defers sentence long, and waits, until amendment of life come to pass. For he who slays straightway, leaves no room for repentance. But if anyone shall dare to contradict these [things], it will be necessary that he confess blood to be assailed by himself, [and] defended by us. But of which of the two the pleading of the case will be the easier before God the judge, if anyone is ignorant: let him hear Solomon saying thus: "The words of the impious lie in wait for blood: But the mouth of the upright delivers him." That [conclusion] I hold [as] settled: that it can repent no one of clemency, patience, kindness, [or] obedience; [and that] it cannot but repent [one] of cruelty and rash judgment. But if that [former] way is the safest, [and] this [latter] full of perils: he must be mad, who knowing and willing should hurl himself into perils. Nor indeed do I say these things to you, most illustrious King, because we have received anything sinister concerning you. For since you are still of that age which is guarded under the care of tutors, your character (especially to us, separated by so great an interval of lands) cannot be sufficiently known. They say indeed, who come from there, that your disposition and humanity are such that they hope all the best things of you—to whose hope we so far accede that we hope and wish even [things] better (if it can be) than the best. Since therefore we see that our bodies have been delivered by God into the hand of you Kings, but that your souls are in the hand of God: We are moved by examples, and attempt to the best of our strength to go to meet the peril, and judge that an evil can never be turned aside too early. And so we pray God that He may inject into you that mind, that you may be so much the more solicitous concerning your souls (which are in the hand of God), than those are concerning their bodies, who see their life to be placed in your hand: by how much the destruction of the soul is graver than [that] of the body—we desire, I say, that you be such as God bids [you] be in these words: "When it shall have happened [that] the king sit on the throne of his kingdom, let him write out for himself this law from the Levitical priests in a book, and let him have it with him, and read [it] through all his life, that he may learn to fear Jehovah his God, and take care that he obey all the words of this law, and these institutions, lest he wax insolent in mind against his [own] kinsmen, nor turn aside from the discipline to the right or left, that he may live long in his kingdom—both he himself, and his offspring—among the Israelites." Likewise Solomon, speaking of a good king, [in] these words: "ORACLE the King has on [his] lips, whose mouth commits no crime in judgment. To act impiously is hateful to kings: For Justice is the throne's foundation. Just lips please kings, and they love those who speak rightly. The royal wrath is the messenger of death, and a wise man assuages it. In the serenity of the King's countenance is life, and his favor is a kind of [thing] like a late[-season] cloud. To have wisdom is far more excellent than gold. But the head of wisdom is the fear of God: which ought to be turned before our eyes day and night, and to be a bridle to evils, and a spur to good [deeds]."

These [things] I had at hand, King, concerning which I would admonish you—not indeed as a prophet, not as one sent divinely: But as one of the many, who hate dissensions and quarrels, and desire that religion be exercised by charity more, and by piety of mind, than by questions and external matters. Nor indeed does it escape me, that nothing [has been] said by me which has not been said by others before me. But [things] which are right, [it] is not useless [that they be said] both by many [men], and for so long—until they be obeyed. Receive therefore, King, this our labor courteously and humanely: And if you have leisure (and you ought to have leisure), and if it pleases [you] (and it ought to please [you]), read the sacred letters with a pious and religious mind: and prepare yourself for ruling the kingdom thus, as a mortal man and one who will render an account to the immortal God. I wish you the clemency of Moses, the piety of David, and the wisdom of Solomon. Farewell. At Basel, in the Month of February: In the year one thousand five hundred fifty-one.

*To the most ample Consul, and the senate of Basel, his Lords, most clement and to be honored; Sebastian Castellio [sends] Greeting in the Lord.*

It has been shown to me, my most clement Lords, by the Magnificent Lord Rector, and the rest of the Doctors and Pastors of the Church: how letters were written to you, in which I was most gravely accused: of which accusation the parts are two: One taken from the book of Theodore Beza, the other concerning my translation of the dialogues of Bernardino Ochino. And to me requesting [it], that part of the letters was recited in which I was accused, and I was interrogated as to what I would respond to it. I responded that I asked that, if it could be done, a copy of that part be given to me, that, accused in writing, I might respond in writing. Which since they said was not permitted to them, except by your leave, and moreover thought that I could respond to the very criminations which were alleged in those letters, I determined to do thus—that, the numbers of the pages having been noted from those letters, in which I am accused in the book of Beza, I [should] here respond to you in a few [words] against those criminations of the accuser, as far as I can recall and comprehend them, [being] Prepared to dissolve [i.e., refute] the rest of the criminations of the same [man] also by a public writing, just as [I have been] accused by him by a public writing. I beg you, my most clement Lords, that you deign to hear me pleading [my] cause with that mind with which each of you would wish himself to be heard in a similar cause.

The first crime is that I am a libertine, from which crime I most constantly affirm that I am as far distant as heaven is distant from earth, and I allege [as proof] of my innocence all [my] writings, [my] sayings rightly understood, and my deeds: And so before all things I ask that my accuser prove this crime, or if he does not prove [it], it is necessary that he be a calumniator. And moreover, [if] it is no [matter of] scruple to him to have accused an innocent [man]: I affirm that he himself is akin to the heresy of the libertines.

The second crime is that I am a Pelagian, and that I have denied the grace of God and original sin. To which crime I respond the same [thing] which [I responded] to the first, and I demand that the accuser prove this too. For as to what pertains to Beza's writing, in which he attempts to prove this from those [things] which I wrote on chapter 7 to the Romans, I say that he interprets my writings sinisterly, and wishes to elicit from them those [things] which never came into my mind, and this I am prepared to prove by a public writing.

The third crime is that I am the patron of all the criminal, the heretics, the adulterers, the thieves, the homicides, and that I wish to render them safe from accusers and judges: and that I do not wish the Magistrate to mix himself in any way into the affairs of religion, and concerning this crime, I respond the same [thing] which [I respond] concerning the foregoing—[that it] is most false, and I demand that the accuser prove it. But I, for my part, promise that I will show even from my writings the contrary. Nay rather, if there be need of trustworthy witnesses, I will make [it] plain that certain [men], who felt sinisterly concerning the Magistrate, were so called away from that error by my effort, that they confessed to me that I had accomplished more with them by gentleness alone than any [men] ever driven by swords could have [accomplished]. For concerning the Magistrate I have altogether always felt, and feel, the same [thing] which [is held] in the Sacred letters. And [as] is held in the Basel confession: Namely:

Es hatt Gott der waerheyt seiner dieneren das schwert / und höchsten vsserlichen gewalt / zu schirm der gutteren / im, raach und straff der bösen besholden. [EN (German): God has, in truth, committed to His servants the sword and the highest outward power, for the protection of the good, [and] for the vengeance and punishment of the wicked.]

And this I add—that it ought to endure even in the time of the new testament, so long as there shall be criminal men, who are to be punished: and he who resists that power, resists the divine ordinance.

The fourth crime is that Castellio is a papist, and a blasphemer against the grace of God. And to this crime I respond the same [thing] which [I respond] to the foregoing: [that it] is false, nor will any mortal ever prove it. For so far am I from being a papist, that I have refused, [when] solicited by great men, not once, with a great reward proposed, both long ago and not so long ago, to migrate over to them. But as to [his saying] that I am a blasphemer against the grace of God, this too is no less false than that. For [that] we are justified and saved by the free benefit of God, through faith in Christ: [this] I have always both believed and taught, and this also my writings openly testify.

The fifth crime is that Castellio is an Academic, and that he has an Anabaptistic spirit. The Academics were certain Philosophers who said that nothing could be known, and affirmed nothing. From this crime Beza himself, in this very book, most of all vindicates me: For he inveighs against me throughout the whole book for this reason—because I affirm many [things], which he himself is indignant be affirmed by me. But as to what he writes concerning an Anabaptistic spirit—what spirit the Anabaptists have, or what they feel or write concerning the word of God, let them see to it. I both wrote, and now write, and feel, that the controversies which are among Theologians concerning Religion cannot be settled from scripture, unless there be present at the same time both the Spirit of Christ, which may open minds, and Charity. And this I add—that unless we shall have given effort to have charity, it will come to pass that, the more letters we have, the less Spirit we shall have, and the more we shall daily disagree among ourselves, and go into the worse; nor, besides, [do I] not add that other [saying] of Paul: "If I speak with the tongues both of men and of Angels, and have not charity, I am sounding brass or a tinkling cymbal. And if I have so much of divination that I hold all secrets, and all knowledge, and if I am so endowed with all faith that I move even mountains, and yet am not endowed with charity, I am nothing. And if I lay out all my faculties in liberality, and if I even hand over my body to be burned, and yet am not endowed with charity, I profit nothing." These are [the things] which concerning Christ, the spirit, and charity I have both written, and [do] write, and feel: and so far am I from dreading this crime, that I say, pronounce, [and] cry out that these [things] are true, and the outcome itself has both hitherto taught, and henceforth will teach, [them] to be true: For if anyone hopes to settle these controversies without the spirit of Christ and charity, I say that he does just as if he should wish to make mortar out of sand and lime alone, without water. This I add—that my accuser here contradicts himself triply, for if I am a libertine, I cannot be either a Papist or an Anabaptist; or if [I am] an Anabaptist, I can be neither a Papist nor a libertine: since indeed the three sects differ from one another—I add also from the sect of the Academics—as fire from water; which I could easily prove, did I not persuade myself that this is known to your prudence.

There are also other crimes, my most clement Lords, which Beza and Calvin impose upon me, of which one or another (concerning which you can most easily judge, even without my defense) I will mention in passing. One is that I translated the Bible at the impulse and instinct of the devil. Consider how plausibly they wrote this. The other is that I seized my neighbor's wood with a grappling-hook. From this know, my most clement Lords, what kind [of men] my accusers are. For if you believe their accusation, it will be fitting that not only I, but also my neighbors then, and many fishermen, and other citizens of Basel—perhaps more than three hundred—pay with me the penalties of theft, since they openly seized the same wood with me.

Since these [men] have interpreted this [as] theft openly, in books published throughout the whole world, consider what kind [of men] they are also in other [matters]—especially if they have hated someone; for they have interpreted my writings no more candidly indeed than [they interpreted] the grappling-hook. These and many [things] of this kind (which they have written concerning me) if my accuser wishes to prove, let him come forth before your Senate, and before the same [Senate], even in German words (however much I scarcely even stammer in German), I am prepared to plead [my] cause. But if he has a bad conscience, and thinks that I ought to respond, not to him privately, but to those who accused me in published books, by published books—and [that he] ought to have interrupted [i.e., challenged] Beza when he was passing this way—know, my Clement Lords, concerning this matter, that, as far as my having hitherto not done it [is concerned], the [fault] has not rested with me. For both then I met with D. Cocceius, and further with him D. the Rector, Simon Sulzer, and I would have met with Beza together with them, had he not departed, hastening to the war: and now I am prepared to respond to all the crimes which they, in their writings, have imposed upon me, by a public writing: And moreover [I ask this] of you, my most Clement Lords: That this be permitted to me, since I am thus accused, I earnestly beg. Or if I do not obtain this, I beg at least this—that if perchance it be not permitted [me] to respond, [my] keeping silence be not [counted] to [my] harm [i.e., be not held as an admission of guilt]. But if they are well conscious to themselves [i.e., have a clear conscience], let Beza himself and Calvin appear, and prove before you, my Judges, all the crimes which they have committed to letters against me: And I (that you may understand how much I trust in the goodness of my cause)—if they shall have proved those [things], I freely offer my head to just punishment. But they ought not, unless they are ill conscious to themselves, to dread the Basel tribunal, [they] who did not dread to publish those [things] to the whole world. I hope that you, my most Clement Lords, [as] God's vicars in this business, will employ in this cause—as you have hitherto [done], both in [the causes] of others and in my own cause—also that prudence, the spirit of truth inspiring [you], that you pronounce nothing except [when] the matter has been rightly examined. Great and powerful are my adversaries and accusers, but God also is powerful (who, having no respect of persons) casts down from the throne. But I am a humble little man: but God also regards the humble, and avenges their blood (if it is unjustly shed). To slip is easy, and one evil man easily inflicts in one moment a wound which afterward a hundred good physicians cannot heal in many years. O God, who knowest both my heart and the hearts of my adversaries, arise, and judge my cause.

As to what pertains to the second part of the accusation, namely, that I translated the dialogues of Bernardino Ochino, I do not think it ought to be [counted] to my harm. For I translated [them] (just as I had translated other works of the same [author]) not as a judge, but as a translator, and [as one] accustomed to make a profit from work of this kind, for the support of [my] family: and the printer said that he had offered the book [to me], and that it had been approved according to the Basel institutions, by the censor[ship].

THE END.

Translator's Notes

Closing tag of the *Benevole Lector* (You, kind Reader...): These four indented lines are Castellio's adaptation of Horace, *Epistles* I.6.67–68 (*"si quid novisti rectius istis, / candidus imparti; si non, his utere mecum"*). The 1612 editor has substituted *nosti* for Horace's *novisti* and *istis* for the original. Rendered as verse to preserve the lapidary, near-epigrammatic form of the source.

Calvin extract I (*Cyprian* citation): The marginal/inline reference "Idem Cypr: Epist: Lib: iij. Ex. iij pag: 77" is a bibliographic locator, retained in the translation as Calvin's own citation apparatus. "Ex. iij" is ambiguous in the OCR; I have rendered it as "[Letter] 3" on the assumption that it refers to a letter number within Book III, but a scholar may wish to verify against the source.

Calvin extract IIII (*Pellitur e medio sapientia*): The OCR "Pelliture medio sapientia dum vi res geritur" has been read as *Pellitur e medio sapientia dum vi res geritur* ("Wisdom is driven from our midst while the matter is conducted by force"). The line scans as a hexameter-like sententia and may be a quotation; I have not identified a definite source.

Calvin extract XIIII–XV (French passages from *Contre la secte des Libertins* / anti-Castellio work): The source citation OCR'd as "A contius. lib. vj." is uncertain. This is plausibly a reference to Calvin's French writings against the "spiritual libertines" or against Castellio; the abbreviation has not been securely identified, and I have flagged it inline. The French itself is clear and has been corrected silently where OCR introduced minor errors (e.g., *vivre* for *viure*, retaining period orthography otherwise). A scholar should verify the work and folio references against Calvin's French corpus.

Thesis IX/X numbering: The disputation theses as transcribed have irregular and duplicated numbering (two sections marked "X"; internal Arabic enumerations 1–8 and I–VI that do not align cleanly). I have preserved the section markers as they appear rather than silently renumbering, since the irregularity is in the source/OCR and renumbering could misrepresent the document's structure. The internal logic (scriptural proofs 1–8, then historical proofs IV–VI) suggests the Roman/Arabic mixture is original to the disputation's citation scheme.

Scripture divergences (Thesis X, proof 1–2): Castellio's/the disputant's Latin renderings of Exodus 22:20 (*quicunque sacrificat Dijs... exscindetur*), Deuteronomy 18:20 (the false prophet), and Leviticus 24:15–16 (the blasphemer) diverge from both Vulgate and KJV phrasing and are rendered here following the disputant's actual Latin wording rather than standardized to KJV, per Rule 9. These are the disputation author's renderings, not Castellio's own, but the same principle applies.

Greek in Thesis X (Socrates indictment): The OCR ("Adikai Sokrates, Hous he polis nomiz ei Theoushou nomizon, hetera de demonia kena eisegoumeuos") is a badly mangled transliteration of the classical indictment of Socrates (cf. Xenophon, *Memorabilia* 1.1.1; Diogenes Laertius 2.40). I have reconstructed it to the standard Greek text (Ἀδικεῖ Σωκράτης, οὓς ἡ πόλις νομίζει θεοὺς οὐ νομίζων, ἕτερα δὲ δαιμόνια καινὰ εἰσηγούμενος) and translated accordingly. The reconstruction is secure because the indictment is a famous fixed text; the errata table itself flags these lines as approximate transliterations. Marked here per Rule 11 as a non-Latin passage with significant OCR damage.

*autokatakriton* (Castellio contra Beza, Titus 3:10–11): The OCR "Afrokatakriton" / "Aftokatakriton" renders Greek αὐτοκατάκριτον ("self-condemned," Titus 3:11). The errata table confirms this (B.1 verso: "coforaplixsitos → autokatakritos"). Corrected silently in the body and noted here. Castellio's exegetical point turns on this word, so the reading matters: he argues against taking it to mean the heretic sins knowingly against conscience.

"Nicolaus of Blasdiken" (epistle heading): The addressee "Nicolaum blasdikensem" is rendered tentatively. The toponym "Blasdiken"/"Blasdicum" is uncertain; I have transliterated rather than guessing at a modern place-name. A scholar may wish to identify the correspondent (possibly a reference to a specific locality in the Low Countries or the Rhineland).

German passage (Basel Confession, in the letter to the Basel senate): The blackletter German quotation was OCR'd with characteristic errors. I have lightly corrected it toward intelligible Early New High German (*höchsten* for *hoecstein*, *bösen* for *bosem*) but several forms remain uncertain: *besholden* (read as *befohlen*, "committed/entrusted"?) and *raach* (read as *Rache*, "vengeance") are flagged here. The sense is clear enough—that God has entrusted the sword and outward power to the magistrate for protecting the good and punishing the wicked—but the exact wording should be verified against a reliable text of the Basel Confession (the First Helvetic / Basel Confession of 1534).

Edward VI dating: Castellio dates the dedicatory epistle February 1551. Edward VI's regency ("still of that age which is guarded under the care of tutors") is consistent with this date; the king was thirteen. Retained as a source date, not an error.

Isaiah/Ezekiel paraphrases in the Edward VI preface: Castellio's renderings of Isaiah 2:4 (swords into ploughshares), Isaiah 11:6–8 (the peaceable kingdom), Isaiah 60 (the glorified Jerusalem), and Ezekiel 36:24–28 (the new heart) are his own distinctive Latin versions, frequently diverging from Vulgate/KJV in vocabulary (e.g., his characteristic "Iova" for the divine name, "armipotens Iova" for the LORD of hosts). Per Rule 9 I have rendered his wording rather than substituting KJV phrasing; the divergences are pervasive and deliberate, reflecting Castellio's program of fresh translation from the Hebrew. I have not flagged each individually as it would overwhelm the notes; the general principle is noted here.

"vindiciae secundum libertatem" (Roman law passage): Castellio invokes the Roman praetorian rule that in a suit over a person's free or slave status, interim possession was granted in favor of liberty (*vindicias dare secundum libertatem*; cf. Livy 3.44–48, the Verginia case). Rendered with the Latin legal term retained and glossed, since it is a technical term of art.

Errata table: Reproduced in Latin per the standing convention. Several rows contain the editor's own corrections of transliterated Greek (N.3 verso, B.1 verso) and these are themselves OCR-approximate; I have preserved the editor's bracketed flags. Note that the errata entry K.5 "Geneve → Vienna" is substantively interesting (correcting the place of Servetus's first condemnation from Geneva to Vienne) but is preserved without comment per the documentary-record convention. Two entries reference folio markers garbled in the OCR (F.5 vers. appearing as ".7." and "P.5. vers."; F.7 vers. as "47. vers.") which I have annotated inline with bracketed corrections.

A general note on the appended dossier: This chunk comprises not Castellio's dialogue proper but the 1612 editor's appended documentation—Calvin's self-contradicting *loca*, the Leiden disputation theses (the "monsters of opinions" the editor decries), Castellio's scriptural annotations, his letters, and his apologia to the Basel senate. The shift from dialogue to documentary apparatus is structural in the source; I have marked the major divisions with the source's own headings and rule-breaks rather than imposing editorial section titles.
